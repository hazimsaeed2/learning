1 · Core Example — SPY Strangle Case Study: Plan → Trade → Evaluate

WHAT — The Complete Workflow

Sell SPY options to capture variance premium (Implied Volatility > Realized Volatility). Every step from OTS-04 and OTS-05 combined into one trade: choose expiry → structure position → calculate delta bands → forecast P&L → execute → post-trade analysis.

Setup (June 3, 2019)

  • Expiry: 1-month (Jun 20). Short-dated = higher vol. All three expiries had similar ATM IV (18.8%) → pick shortest
  • Structure: Short strangle — sell 262 put + sell 286 call (SPY at 275). Sold for $3.09 each
  • Risk cap: Bought 100 × 232 puts at 2-delta to limit crash risk (half-million loss → capped)
  • Hedging vol: Average of VIX (14.1%) + GARCH (13.7%) = 13.9%
  • Delta bands: Whalley-Wilmott with κ=0.24, λ from broker costs

HOW — Expected vs Actual P&L

2 · The Delta Hedging Problem — Why, What Goes Wrong, and Approaches

WHAT — Why Delta Hedge?

Premium has two major drivers: volatility + underlying price. To capture pure volatility alpha, you must mask the price impact.

Opening: premium = f(implied volatility). Closing at expiry: settlement = f(realized volatility). Your P&L captures the difference between IV and RV.

Basic hedge: If you have a call with delta=0.56, sell 0.56 shares → positional delta=0. If stock moves, delta changes to 0.68 → sell 0.12 more shares. Repeat continuously.

WHY — Continuous Hedging Fails

  • Transaction costs can explode: every tick may require rehedging
  • Slippage: can't always buy/sell at exact price assumed
  • P&L contamination: costs eat into volatility profits
  • Simulation result: on average some profit, but median = −$27 (50% of trades lose money)
  • P&L benchmark: Vega×(IV−RV) is the average intuition, but real hedging costs and slippage matter

Core tension: You hedge to isolate volatility, but hedging itself is costly. The lecture's goal is a practical compromise rather than perfect continuous hedging.

HOW — Three Hedging Approaches

ApproachMethodIssue
Time-basedHedge once/week or every N daysRisk of imbalance between hedges
Price-basedHedge every N std devs of price$10 move ≠ same for $1000 vs $1 stock
Delta-basedHedge when delta exits a bandMost fundamental — uses delta itself ✓

Preferred route in the lecture: Delta bands. Instead of targeting delta=0, define a range (e.g., 0.4 to 0.6). Then hedge when delta exits the band. This is presented as a cost-aware compromise.

TERMINOLOGY: Dynamic hedging = continuously adjusting hedge. Discontinuous hedging = hedging at intervals or thresholds. Pin risk = uncertainty near strike at expiry.

3 · Whalley-Wilmott Approximation — Calculating Delta Bands

WHAT — The Equation

A utility-based method that outputs TWO delta values (upper and lower band):

Band = dV/dS ± √(3/2 × e−r(T−t) × λ × S × Γ² / κ)
dV/dSPosition delta (the Greek)
rInterest rate (per annum)
T−tDays to expiry
λ (lambda)Proportional transaction cost
SUnderlying price
Γ (gamma)Rate of delta change (squared!)
κ (kappa)Risk aversion metric (YOU)

WHY — Each Term Matters

  • Delta (dV/dS): Center of the band — what you're hedging around
  • Time (T−t): Band narrows as expiry approaches (theta decay → more sensitive)
  • Lambda (λ): Higher transaction cost → wider bands needed (hedge less often to save costs)
  • Gamma (Γ²): PRIMARY DRIVER. High gamma → delta changes fast → bands adjust. Gamma is squared because it's the most impactful variable
  • Kappa (κ): YOUR personality. Risk-averse → low κ → narrow bands → hedge more → higher cost. Risk-tolerant → high κ → wider bands → hedge less → lower cost

Narrow bands: More hedging, higher cost, less risk exposure. Wider bands: Less hedging, lower cost, more risk tolerance.

WORKED EXAMPLE — Calibrating λ and κ

# Lambda (proportional transaction cost):
# Trading Apple at $130, cost = $1
lambda_aapl = 1 / 130        # = 0.0077

# Trading SPY index at $100, cost = $0.05
lambda_spy = 0.05 / 100      # = 0.0005

# Kappa (risk aversion — from YOUR experience):
# "I'm comfortable not hedging if delta < 0.3"
# Substitute delta=0.3 + known values into
# Whalley-Wilmott equation → solve for kappa
# Example result: kappa = 0.24
# This kappa is YOURS — reuse it as a starting point for similar contracts

# Reading the bands:
# If call delta = 0.4, bands = [0.35, 0.45]
#   → position delta is 0.38
#   → 0.38 is WITHIN [0.35, 0.45]
#   → NO hedge needed (save costs!)
# If delta moves to 0.48 (outside upper band)
#   → HEDGE: bring delta back within bands
#   → Don't need to bring to zero!

GOTCHA: Without delta bands you aim for delta=0 every time, which makes hedging expensive. With delta bands, you accept a range and typically hedge when delta exits that band.

4 · Choosing Hedging Volatility — Implied vs Realized

WHAT — The Two Options

When calculating delta, gamma, and bands for hedging — which volatility do you plug in?

InputImplied VolRealized Vol
MTM VarianceLOW ✓HIGH ✗
Final P&L AccuracyUNCERTAIN ✗ACCURATE ✓
Daily Std Dev$100$231
Final Std Dev$4,460Much less

Can't know RV until expiry → use forecast (GARCH or VIX) as proxy for realized vol.

WHY — Monte Carlo Evidence

Hedge at IV (40%): Monte Carlo shows final P&L ranging $2K–$18K across simulations. Wide scatter — you can't predict where it lands.

Hedge at RV (30%): Most simulations cluster around $8K. Narrow scatter — final P&L is predictable.

But daily view flips: using IV, each day's P&L is smooth. Using RV, daily P&L is volatile (day 150 might show $8K profit or a loss).

Decision framework:

  • Care about daily MTM (risk management, margin) → use implied vol
  • Care about final outcome (profit target, edge validation) → use realized/forecasted vol

WORKED EXAMPLE — Greeks with Python

# Calculate Greeks using a pricing library
import mibian                    # options pricing library

# Inputs: stock=100, strike=105, rate=5%,
#   days_to_expiry=30, volatility=30%, type=call
c = mibian.BS(
    [100, 105, 5, 30],           # [S, K, r, DTE]
    volatility=30                 # which vol to use?
)
# If hedging for daily accuracy → use IV (market)
# If hedging for final P&L → use forecast (GARCH)

print(c.callDelta)               # delta value
print(c.gamma)                   # gamma value
print(c.callTheta)               # theta
print(c.vega)                    # vega
print(c.rho)                     # rho

# KEY: delta & gamma change with vol input!
# IV=30% → delta=0.38
# RV=25% → delta=0.35 (different bands!)
# Stick to ONE methodology for entire trade

GOTCHA: Switching between IV and RV mid-trade invalidates your analysis. Pick one approach at entry and stick with it until exit.

5 · Hedging Frequency & Drift Adjustment

WHAT — How Many Times to Hedge?

Dispersion ∝ 1/√(number of hedges)

More hedges → more accurate P&L prediction (dispersion between theoretical and actual P&L decreases). But costs increase linearly.

Elbow curve: Plot dispersion vs number of hedges. 0→50 hedges: sharp accuracy gain. 50→100: diminishing returns. Find the "elbow" — that's your cap.

Same concept as K-Means inertia elbow — utility drops after optimal point. Additional hedges cost more than the accuracy they buy.

WHAT — Drift / Trend Adjustment

Black-Scholes (BSM) assumes no drift — only considers current price, not trend direction. This creates a flaw when markets trend strongly.

PositionMarketVol Bias
Short gammaTrending up ↑LOW
Short gammaTrending down ↓HIGH
Long gammaTrending up ↑HIGH
Long gammaTrending down ↓LOW

Practical meaning: If shorting vol in a bull market, don't sell at current price — wait for higher entry. Bias your volatility input LOWER to account for the upward drift BSM ignores.

WORKED EXAMPLE — Drift in SPY Case

# SPY case study — drift impact:
# Model said: short at premium = $282.92
# Market was trending UP strongly

# Without drift consideration:
# → Shorted at $282.92 (current price)
# → Expected P&L = $16,900

# With drift consideration:
# → Waited for higher entry (limit order ~$300)
# → Or biased vol input LOWER
# → Expected P&L = $20,100 (closer to actual)

# Actual: SPY rallied 275 → 293
# If we had waited, entry would be better

# Path dependency:
# Options P&L depends on the PATH price takes
# Same start+end but different path = different P&L
# A single backtest = single path (unreliable)
# Use Monte Carlo: simulate MANY paths
# → Average across paths for robust expectation

GOTCHA: Path dependency means even rigorous backtests show only one possible outcome. A 1% change in volatility or price can produce a meaningfully different premium path. Ideally, simulate multiple scenarios.

6 · Expiration Mechanics — Pinning, Early Exercise, Settlement

WHAT — Expiration Day Behaviors

  • Many out-of-the-money options end up effectively worthless at expiry
  • In-the-money (ITM) options → delta ≈ 1 (moves like underlying stock)
  • Greeks look "weird" — extreme values near expiry (normal, not a bug)
  • Delta can jump 10-20× in final minutes — not useful for decision-making

Pinning Pattern

On expiry day, the underlying often tends to settle near a strike price more than random chance would suggest. Example: Nifty trading at 25,571 may close nearer 25,550 or 25,600 than exactly 25,571. Useful data point for timing exits.

HOW — Early Exercise Decision (American Options)

Rule: Exercise early if what you gain > what you lose (time value + opportunity cost).

Example — Call + Dividend:

ScenarioValue
80-strike call on $100 stockPremium = $20.10
Intrinsic value$20.00
Extrinsic (time) value$0.10
Upcoming dividend$1.00
Exercise → get$19 + $1 div = $20
Hold → get$19.10 (price drops by $1)

Decision: In this example, dividend ($1) > extrinsic ($0.10), so early exercise makes more sense than holding. Practical rule of thumb: compare dividend with remaining time value.

Cash vs Physical Settlement

TypeWhat HappensRisk
CashGet $difference in accountNone — position closed
Physical (Stock)Stock delivered to dematLeaves overnight risk unless you later sell it
Physical (Commodity)Inventory delivered/warehousedShipping charges + warehouse rent!

Most retail exchanges: cash settled. But commodity exchanges may physically deliver — traders have been surprised by warehouse charges appearing in their ledger.

European options: Cannot exercise early. Only close by taking opposite position (sell call to cancel long call) or wait for expiry settlement.

7 · Trade Evaluation Framework — Performance Metrics & Analysis

WHAT — Common Mistakes

  • WRONG: Judging by cumulative returns alone
  • WRONG: Validating by a single trade outcome
  • WRONG: Comparing Sharpe ratio of a vol strategy vs momentum strategy
  • WRONG: Using same entry/exit metrics for evaluation

What a good equity curve looks like: Trends UP consistently. Limited time below zero. Quick recovery from drawdowns. Consistent profit per trade — not a few big wins followed by big losses.

Minimum: Aim for roughly 100 or more trades when judging a strategy statistically. Maintain a trade journal, whether manual or automated.

HOW — Performance Metrics

RatioFormulaRisk Defined As
Sharpe(R̄ − Rf) / σStd dev of ALL returns
Sortino(R̄ − Rf) / σ_downStd dev of LOSSES only
CalmarReturn / Max DDMaximum drawdown
SterlingReturn / Avg(3 worst DDs)Average of 3 largest drawdowns

Sharpe flaw: Penalizes large UP moves equally as down moves. Strategy with returns [4%, 10%, 40%, 80%, 2%] has BAD Sharpe (high σ) despite being hugely profitable.

For vol strategies: "If IV changes by X%, how much alpha do I capture?" is better than Sharpe.

WORKED EXAMPLE — Multi-Dimensional Analysis

# Compare two strategies:
# Strategy A: profit/year=$50K, win rate=30%
#   Win avg=$4, Loss avg=$1 → high risk/reward
# Strategy B: profit/year=$20K, win rate=90%
#   Win avg=$1, Loss avg=$8 → dangerous!

# Single metric → misleading
# Multi-dimensional evaluation:

# 1. Regime analysis:
#   Bull market: which performs better?
#   Bear market: which has smaller drawdown?
#   Range-bound: which generates more signals?
#   High-vol: which survives?

# 2. Evaluate strengths/weaknesses:
#   If momentum strategy → expect loss in range
#   If vol strategy → measure by IV edge, not Sharpe

# 3. Backtest ≠ Live results because:
#   - Slippage (can't buy at exact backtest price)
#   - Alpha decay (others deploy same strategy)
#   - Path dependency (single path ≠ all paths)

# 4. Journal metrics to track:
#   - Trade frequency per month
#   - Average P&L per trade
#   - Win rate & win/loss ratio
#   - Max drawdown & recovery time

8 · Volatility Trading Process & Practical Wisdom

WHAT — The Complete Volatility Trade Process

  1. Select expiration: Short-dated → higher vol. Compare ATM IV across expiries
  2. Choose structure: Straddle (simpler, higher cost) or strangle (wider range, lower cost). Add protective legs to cap tail risk
  3. Calculate delta bands: Whalley-Wilmott with your κ (risk aversion) and λ (transaction cost)
  4. Choose hedging vol: IV (for daily accuracy) or RV/forecast (for final P&L accuracy)
  5. Consider drift: Bias vol input based on market trend direction
  6. Calculate expected P&L: Use Vega×(IV−RV) PER position. Express as average ± std dev, NOT a single number
  7. Execute & hedge: Typically hedge when delta exits bands. Cap total hedges near the elbow point from your analysis
  8. Post-trade analysis: Compare actual vs expected. Find WHY deviation occurred. Improve forecasts

WHY — Key Wisdom from Varun

  • "Hard-earned profits are lost by very few uncontrolled risks." Control risk > maximize profit
  • "Treat trading as a business, not a single trade or single week." Look at quarters and years
  • "Find an edge. Don't judge if it's extremely profitable. Simple and stupid can work if statistically sound"
  • "You don't get paid to be an all-rounder." Specialize in your market regime
  • "It's okay to forego a risky trade than to get held in a risky trade and try to optimize it"
  • Edge convergence: strategy may deviate for months but converge to average over time. Give enough time
  • AI/ML models often work for ~6 months then stop. But some converge back — don't abandon too early

Varun's personal practice: Journaled every hour since 2015 (now automated with agents/LLMs). Runs multiple ML models — evaluates each in bull/bear/high-vol regimes separately before going live.

WORKED EXAMPLE — Idea → Trade → Strategy

# Three distinct levels:
# IDEA: "I see big orders every 20-30s in bullish LME"
#   → Just a hypothesis, NOT a trade

# TRADE: Substantiate with analysis
#   → When does pattern occur? (expiry day only?)
#   → What's the win rate over 100+ occurrences?
#   → Backtest with slippage included

# STRATEGY: Complete risk framework
#   → What if pattern breaks?
#   → Entry rules, exit rules, position sizing
#   → Performance in different market regimes
#   → Expected P&L as range (avg ± std dev)
#   → Post-trade review process

# Expected P&L should NEVER be a single number:
expected_pnl = 16900          # average
std_dev = 8200                # uncertainty
range_low = expected_pnl - std_dev   # $8,700
range_high = expected_pnl + std_dev  # $25,100
# "I expect $8,700 to $25,100" — NOT "$16,900"

GOTCHA: A profitable trade doesn't mean good analysis. The SPY case made $24K but forecast was $16.9K — we were wrong by 43%. Post-trade analysis reveals improvement opportunities that single P&L numbers hide.

KEY TAKEAWAYS

Delta Bands Save You

Continuous hedging can become prohibitively costly. Whalley-Wilmott gives a practical delta range. Hedge when delta moves outside that band. Gamma is a primary driver, and κ comes from your own risk tolerance and trading experience.

IV vs RV: Know Your Goal

Implied vol → smooth daily P&L, uncertain final number. Realized vol → volatile daily P&L, accurate final number. Can't know RV until expiry — use GARCH/VIX forecast. Pick one, stick to it.

Expected P&L = Range

Don’t stop at a single expected P&L number. Use an average plus a spread such as standard deviation. SPY case: $16,900 ± $8,200. Actual: $24,218, which stayed within range. Post-trade, inspect why it deviated.

Evaluate Multi-Dimensionally

Don’t rely on a single metric, especially not just Sharpe. Analyze by market regime such as bull, bear, range, and high-vol periods. Aim for a large enough trade sample, often around 100 or more. Journal and review risk closely.
