The Difference Between a Strategy and a Robot

Week 11-1 · Trading & Backtesting Platforms · TBP-04 (with Dr. Hui Liu)

It is tempting to think that a good idea is most of the battle. It isn't. A trading strategy is just the spark; a trading robot is the whole machine — data plumbing, execution, risk controls, monitoring, and a platform that lets you test and trade without rewriting a line. This lesson zooms out to that whole machine, then zooms back in to build and honestly judge one concrete model.

1. The road every robot travels

Keep this sequence in your head: idea → collect data → build a mathematical model to prove or disprove it → visualise → program it → backtest → forward test → live with a small portfolio → live with the full portfolio. Notice how much sits between the idea and real money. Skipping steps is how traders lose savings.

2. Why bother automating?

Here the instructor is refreshingly blunt: automation does not promise more profit. What it genuinely delivers is discipline — emotionless execution, fewer human errors, and far less stress. He describes professional traders glued to six or eight or ten screens from the open to the close; a robot frees you from that. Once you trust what you've deployed, you stop checking prices obsessively and spend your time where edge actually lives: research.

On AI versus hiring a coder. Use AI freely, but never blindly — you must be knowledgeable enough to verify what it produces. If you can tell the AI exactly what you want and check that it did a good job, use it; if you're an experienced trader with little programming, a human coder is often easier to direct.

3. If you want to run money

Many EPAT students dream of their own fund, so the lecture sketches the path: research strategies, decide your style (the instructor favours blending fundamental and quantitative, since a Fed rate decision moves everything), translate to Python, host it somewhere, summarise results often, improve, and build a track record. When clients arrive, you'll meet two account structures:

4. Choosing your tools: five categories

Ask any AI for "Python algo trading tools" and you'll drown. The instructor's own taxonomy cuts through it, and every category has a sting in the tail:

  • Web-based platforms (Quantopian-style): nothing to install, vast data, instant backtests, gorgeous analytics — but no privacy. To go live you'd hand your broker login to a third party who can then see every keystroke of your strategy. A serious trader won't do that.
  • Low-level wrappers (ibpy, ib_insync): quick to live-trade, but no backtesting, pro-developer only, forum-only support.
  • Backtesting engines (zipline): great community and backtests, but not built for live trading — research only.
  • Brokers' native APIs (IB): you can live-trade with support, but no backtesting and a long road to a real robot.
  • Self-hosted solutions (the instructor's platform): privacy first, easy, and — crucially — backtest and live trade in one place with no code change; the cost is that it's just software (you supply data) and you install it yourself.
The one requirement that matters most: a platform that runs your backtest and your live trading from the same code, unchanged. The moment you rewrite logic to move from research to production, you invite human error.

5. Backward, forward, and the privacy picture

Backward testing runs the robot on historical data — essentially research. Forward testing runs it on a paper account going forward, treating simulated fills as if real. The warning is firm: paper performance is not the same as live performance, so even a flawless paper run must be proven live with a little money before you scale.

As for privacy in a self-hosted setup: your computer talks to the broker through IB Gateway or TWS, and you type your trading id and password into that official software. Your strategy — just Python files sitting in a folder you control — never sees the credentials. That's the whole privacy argument, and it's the question to put to any other tool.

6. The platform's two heartbeats

Most self-hosted platforms expose the same minimal shape: an initialize function that runs once for setup, and a handle_data function that runs every second, receiving fresh market data and your account, where all your logic lives. Three cornerstone functions — get the real-time price, get historical data, and place an order — are enough to build surprisingly complex robots.

7. Trend, swing, and a model to test them

Two mindsets underlie almost every idea. Trend (momentum): if it went up, you bet it keeps going up — and buy-and-hold is the ultimate trend bet. Swing (mean-reversion): if it rose too far, you bet it falls back. The instructor's friendship analogy is lovely — a long, warm relationship is a trend bet that the past predicts the future.

The example strategy is pure swing: if the price fell versus yesterday, buy; if it rose, short; trade daily — buy low, sell high. To test whether the market actually behaves this way, we build a linear-regression model. The trick is feature engineering by shifting: because a model reads one row at a time, we line up, on the same row, today's move (the predictor) and tomorrow's move (the target):

close_yest = Close.shift(1)
chg_y2t = (Close - close_yest) / close_yest   # predictor: yesterday -> today
chg_t2m = chg_y2t.shift(-1)                    # target:    today -> tomorrow
LinearRegression().fit(X = chg_y2t, y = chg_t2m)

8. Reading the result — honestly

On our index the regression coefficient came out negative (about −0.08). A negative slope means an up-day today tends to be followed by a down-day tomorrow — we live, as one student put it, in a "swing world." The quadrant scatter confirms it: the fit line tilts downward. So far, so encouraging for the mean-reversion idea.

But look at the strength. The correlation is only about 0.08. A statistician wouldn't call anything below 0.6–0.7 a real correlation, so this is very weak. And the backtest is sobering: the swing strategy lost about 3.4% while buy-and-hold made about 58.3% over the same window, with a worse Sharpe (0.05 vs 0.73) and a sub-coin-flip win rate (47%). Test the same model across other timeframes and even the sign of the coefficient wobbles. The structure (a swing tendency) is genuinely there, but the edge is paper-thin — exactly why the instructor says don't put your life savings on it, and why you verify live, with small money, before scaling.

The one-paragraph version

A trading strategy is just the idea; a robot is the whole machine, built along the road map idea → data → model → visualise → program → backtest → forward test → live (small → full). Automate for discipline, not magic. Choose a platform that gives you privacy and backtest + live trading from one unchanged codebase; web platforms cost privacy, wrappers and native APIs lack backtesting, engines lack live. Distinguish backward (historical) from forward (paper) testing, and remember paper ≠ live. The market mindset is trend vs swing; we tested swing with a linear regression built by shifting features, found a negative coefficient (a swing world) but a ~0.08 correlation — real structure, thin edge — and a backtest that lost to buy-and-hold. The lesson: structure is not the same as profit, so verify live before risking real capital.
