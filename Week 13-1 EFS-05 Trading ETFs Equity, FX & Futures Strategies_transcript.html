2️⃣ INDEX CONSTRUCTION — Cap-Weighted Indices & Corporate Actions

WHAT: How Indices Work

WHY: Every ETF represents an index. Understanding index construction = understanding what your ETF actually holds.

Index providers: MSCI, FTSE Russell, S&P, NSE (ISL subsidiary in India). They create indices, set rules, and charge subscription fees. They are extremely powerful — removing a country from MSCI Emerging Markets Index forces all asset managers to pull money.

DIVISOR RULES (from Core Example):

EventIndex Changes?Divisor Changes?Why
Stock SplitNoNoTotal MV unchanged
DividendNoYes ↓MV drops by div amt
ReconstitutionNoYesNew stock ≠ old stock MV

WORKED EXAMPLE: Reconstitution (Stock Swap)

T-1: X1=$100×50K=$5M, X2=$25×100K=$2.5M → Total=$7.5M

T: X2 goes OUT, X3 comes IN (price=$50, shares=200K)

New MV: X1=$5M + X3=$50×200K=$10M → Total=$15M

Keep index at 1000 → New Divisor = $15M / 1000 = 15,000

GOTCHA: Reconstitutions are known 14–25 days in advance. Index providers announce early so APs can prepare. Nobody wakes up surprised.

💡 DAILY CALCULATION: Index weights and divisor are recalculated once per day (beginning of day). Not real-time. Corporate actions are scheduled events with known dates.

NIFTY 50 REBALANCE FREQUENCY:

~4 index changes per year. Nifty Next 50: ~8/year. Not frequent, but each requires the ETF manager to adjust baskets — usually done at end of trading day to minimize tracking error.

3️⃣ MUTUAL FUND PROBLEMS & THE ETF SOLUTION — Why ETFs Were Invented

Problem 1: Free Liquidity

WHAT: When someone redeems from a mutual fund, the fund manager must sell securities → transaction costs → ALL remaining investors pay.

WORKED EXAMPLE:

3 investors × ₹1M = ₹3M total

1 investor redeems → FM sells → cost ₹2,000

₹2,000 spread across ALL investors' NAV

Over years: 1.5–2% CAGR drag!

WHO benefits: Day traders (in-and-out). WHO loses: Long-term investors (subsidize the trades).

Problem 2: Tax Inefficiency

WHAT: When an active fund manager sells winning stocks, capital gains taxes are triggered for ALL investors — even if they didn't sell anything.

This creates a constant tension: the FM wants to realize gains, but investors don't want tax events. The FM's decision affects every investor's tax bill.

⚠️ SCANDALS: Late trading (buying after market close at stale NAV), market timing. Regulators now audit extensively, but structure isn't watertight.

ETF SOLUTION: In-Kind = No Cash

WHAT: ETF transactions are in-kind: you give baskets → get units, or give units → get baskets. No dollars change hands with the fund.

WORKED EXAMPLE:

Day 1: 3 APs bring baskets (₹2.5M each)

FM gives 100K ETF shares each → 300K total

NAV = ₹7.5M / 300K = ₹25

Day 2: 1 AP redeems 100K shares → gets basket

Remaining: ₹5M / 200K = ₹25 (unchanged!)

✓ Free liquidity: SOLVED (AP bears transaction costs)

✓ Tax: SOLVED (in-kind = no taxable event)

4️⃣ ETF PRIMARY MARKET — The Warehouse of Stocks

WHAT: The Warehouse Analogy

WHY this image matters: The ETF fund manager is a WAREHOUSE MANAGER, not an active trader. He takes in baskets, gives out receipts (ETF shares), and vice versa. Like commodity warehouses where traders deposited commodities and traded receipts.

CREATION (AP → Fund):

1. AP assembles basket (buys all stocks in secondary market)

2. AP adds cash component (for rebalancing needs)

3. AP brings basket to fund house

4. Fund gives AP 100,000 ETF shares (creation unit)

5. AP can now sell ETF shares in secondary market

REDEMPTION (Fund → AP):

1. AP brings 100,000 ETF shares to fund

2. Fund extinguishes the shares

3. Fund gives AP the basket (stocks + cash)

4. AP sells the basket stocks in secondary market

⚠️ KEY RULE: You CANNOT buy 1 ETF in primary market. You must transact in fund-defined lots. In this lecture's worked example the creation unit is 100,000 shares, but actual lot size varies by fund. Only Authorized Participants (large brokers, HFT firms, market makers) have access.

💡 BASKET DATA: Fund manager publishes the basket composition DAILY. It's in the FM's interest — APs need it to transact. Available on fund websites and market data terminals.

5️⃣ SECONDARY MARKET & ARBITRAGE — Where HFTs Make Money

WHAT: Premium, Discount & iNAV

WHY: APs have access to BOTH markets. They compute iNAV (indicative NAV) from basket × current prices, compare it to the secondary market bid/ask, and arbitrage any gap.

iNAV CALCULATION:

In the later arbitrage worksheet example, the updated basket prices imply

iNAV = about ₹24.79

APs then compare that live estimate with the secondary-market bid/ask.

ConditionMeaningiNAV vs MarketAP Action
PremiumMarket > iNAV24.79 vs 24.86/24.91Create basket → sell ETF on market
DiscountMarket < iNAV24.79 vs 24.74/24.76Buy ETF on market → redeem basket
At ParMarket ≈ iNAV24.79 vs 24.78/24.80No arbitrage (90% of time for liquid ETFs)

HOW: Arbitrage Worked Example

CASE: Market at Premium (best bid = ₹24.86)

1. Compute iNAV = ₹24.79 (from basket)

2. Best bid in secondary market = ₹24.86

3. Gap = ₹24.86 − ₹24.79 = ₹0.07 per share

4. AP creates basket in primary → gets 100K ETF shares

5. AP sells 100K shares at ₹24.86 on secondary

6. Profit = 100,000 × ₹0.07 = ₹7,000

(minus transaction costs of ~20 bps)

MARKUP REQUIREMENT (Case 2: 35 bps):

35 bps markup on ₹24.79 = 0.0035 × 24.79 = ₹0.087

Minimum sell price = ₹24.79 + ₹0.087 = ₹24.877

Best bid = ₹24.86 < ₹24.877 → NO TRADE

35 bps markup covers funding costs of the AP's capital

💡 WHO COMPUTES iNAV? HFTs and systematic traders compute iNAV themselves from basket data + live market prices. They do NOT wait for third-party feeds. Speed determines who captures the arbitrage.

Markup ranges: Liquid counters: ~35 bps. Illiquid counters: 100–200 bps.

6️⃣ REPLICATION STRATEGIES & ETF SELECTION — Full vs. Optimized

WHAT: How ETFs Track Their Index

StrategyWhat It DoesWhen Used% of Market
Full ReplicationHold EVERY stock at EXACT index weightSmall indices (Nifty 50)~50%
OptimizedHold SUBSET of stocks, minimize tracking error using quant modelsLarge indices (5000+ stocks)~50%

WHY optimized? If index has 5,000 stocks, buying all 5,000 in exact proportions is impractical (illiquid small-caps, high transaction costs). Quants select a subset that minimizes tracking error — this is where quant jobs exist in ETF shops.

Tracking Error = deviation of ETF returns from index returns. Lower = better. THE key metric for selecting between ETFs tracking the same index.

HOW: Selecting an ETF (When Multiple Track Same Index)

ETF SELECTION CRITERIA (ranked):

1. Tracking Error — lower = better alignment with index

2. Expense Ratio — lower = cheaper. ETFs charge rock-bottom fees (single-digit bps for large funds)

3. AUM — larger = more liquid, tighter bid-ask spread

4. Bid-Ask Spread — tighter = less implicit cost per trade

⚠️ THIS IS NOT MUTUAL FUND SELECTION: For mutual funds, you look at alpha, Sharpe, drawdown, fund manager track record. For ETFs, you DON'T want alpha — you want INDEX returns. So you look at tracking error and expense ratio instead.

GOTCHA — Replication must be disclosed: Fund must specify "full replication" or "optimized" in its prospectus. You can't say full replication and secretly optimize. Regulators enforce this.

💡 ETF FM trades are RARE: Unlike active FMs who trade all day, ETF FMs trade mainly at end-of-day when basket composition changes (rebalances, corporate actions). During the day, usually zero trades.

7️⃣ TRADING MODELS — Low, Mid & High Frequency ETF Strategies

LOW FREQ: Factor Models

WHAT: Most stock returns are explained by a few factors (market, size, value, momentum). Why pick stocks when you can invest in factors via ETFs?

The math (CAPM/Fama-French):

R_stock = α + β₁·R_market + β₂·R_size + β₃·R_value + β₄·R_mom + ε

WORKED EXAMPLE (Indian IT sector):

130 CNX200 stocks regressed on 4 factors

IT sector: R² ≈ 70–75%

→ 4 factors explain 75% of variance

→ Idiosyncratic stock risk is 25%

Conclusion: Just buy factor ETFs instead of picking individual stocks. Dimensional Fund ($256B AUM) does exactly this.

Factor data (free): Prof. Jayant Varma (IIM Ahmedabad) website

Book: "The Incredible Shrinking Alpha" — why active managers can't beat benchmarks

MID FREQ: Robo Advisors

WHAT: Fintechs that take your risk/return preferences and allocate across ETFs algorithmically, rebalancing periodically.

The process:

1. Investor specifies risk tolerance & return goal

2. Algorithm selects mix of ETFs (equity, bond, commodity)

3. Portfolio is rebalanced (monthly/quarterly)

The math: Efficient Frontier (textbook version fails in practice). Better: simulate frontier SURFACE, not a single line.

Book: "Efficient Asset Management" — statistically robust portfolio allocation using ETFs

💡 INDUSTRY TREND: Most robo advisors got acquired by banks. Fintechs raised money but couldn't compete with bank distribution. The tech now sits inside bank infrastructure.

HIGH FREQ: Market Making

WHAT: Model order arrival in the limit order book as a stochastic process (Poisson), estimate parameters, provide liquidity and capture bid-ask spread.

The approach:

1. Model order arrival/cancellation as Poisson process

2. Estimate λ (arrival rate) from real-time data

3. Set bid/ask quotes to capture spread while managing inventory risk

4. Continuously update as order flow changes

WHO does this: Top HFT firms acting as APs. They dominate the ETF primary-secondary arbitrage space. Speed is the differentiator.

Pre-requisites: Queuing theory, stochastic processes, C++, low-latency infrastructure

🎯 KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. ETFs solve mutual funds' two biggest problems: free liquidity (in-kind transactions push costs onto APs, not long-term investors) and tax inefficiency (no cash = no taxable events inside the fund).

2. Primary market = warehouse. AP assembles basket of stocks + cash → gives to fund → receives creation units. The lecture example uses 100K ETF shares, but actual creation-unit size varies by fund. Redemption is the reverse. No dollars are exchanged with the fund — all in-kind.

3. HFTs/APs compute iNAV themselves from basket × live prices, compare to secondary market bid/ask, and arbitrage any premium/discount. This keeps ETF prices aligned with NAV. Speed wins.

4. $20T market, 76% equity, 72% US. ~50% use optimized (not full) replication. Active ETFs exploding ($1.4T). When selecting ETFs: track tracking error and expense ratio, NOT alpha.

THE MASTER FORMULA: iNAV = (Σ basket_qty × current_price + cash_component) / creation_unit_size — Compare iNAV to secondary market bid/ask — if premium: create & sell; if discount: buy & redeem

THE ONE THING TO NEVER FORGET: ETF primary market transactions are in-kind — baskets in, units out (or reverse). No cash changes hands with the fund. This is what makes ETFs tax-efficient and protects long-term investors from free-rider costs.
