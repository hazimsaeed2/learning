Volatility: Measuring It, Forecasting It, and Trading It

A plain-English walk through the fourth options session — why there is no "true" volatility, only estimators; how to forecast it without the rolling-window trap; and how every volatility trade, from a humble covered call to a weekend straddle, reduces to one tidy equation.

The previous options lectures priced options and met the Greeks. This one is about a single Greek — Vega — and the quantity it responds to: volatility. The session moves in three acts: measure volatility, forecast it, and then structure trades around it. The recurring lesson is that a volatility number is meaningless until you put it in context, and that the profit from any vol trade can be written as Vega × (IV − RV).

1. Measuring volatility — a number with no true value

The natural first question is "how many data points, at what frequency?" The honest answer: it doesn't pin down a true value, because volatility cannot be measured exactly — it can only be estimated. If the true volatility were 0.30, repeated measurements scatter around 0.30; with more data points the scatter shrinks, but no single number is "right".

The finance default is the standard deviation of log returns using closing prices. It has a subtle but real flaw: the statistical formula assumes you hold the entire population, yet stock prices are forever just a sample — tomorrow's price could exceed any high you've ever seen. So close-to-close systematically misrepresents the truth. That motivates a family of estimators.

The Parkinson estimator and its low bias

The Parkinson estimator uses three inputs — the number of observations N, and each day's high and low. Because it squeezes two prices out of every day instead of one, its error is smaller and its precision higher than close-to-close. But it has a blind spot: high and low only describe the market-open session, so it ignores the overnight gap between yesterday's close and today's open — where geopolitics, currency moves and macro releases land. As a result Parkinson is biased low: with ten days of data it reads only about 0.81 of true volatility (on real Apple 2023 the ratio is about 0.90: Parkinson 1.122% against close-to-close 1.252% daily, or 17.8% versus 19.9% annualised).

Capturing the night. You can split total variance into an overnight piece (previous close → today's open) and an intraday piece (the high-low range), then blend them by the hours the market is closed versus open: σ²_total = (t_c/D)·σ²_overnight + (t_o/D)·σ²_intraday, with a 24-hour day, roughly 6.5 open hours and 17.5 closed. Other estimators go further — Garman-Klass uses all four of open/high/low/close, Rogers-Satchell adds a drift term, and Yang-Zhang adds jumps.

The punchline is liberating: since no method is "true", stop chasing accuracy and instead contextualise. It is like measuring temperature — the thermometer matters less than what you do with the reading. Pick one estimator (the course uses Parkinson) and use that same method everywhere — measurement, forecast and trade — then compare today's number against its own history to decide whether the market is in a high- or low-volatility regime. And don't be seduced by tick data: over tiny intervals most "volatility" is just a big bid or ask, not real risk, and it still misses the overnight move.

2. Forecasting volatility

Measurement tells you where volatility is; trading needs where it will be at expiry. The naive tool is a 30-day rolling window, and it breaks badly. A single violent day — Netflix's +17.4% earnings jump on 18 October 2016 — makes the rolling volatility spike, sit elevated for thirty days, then crash on day 31 when the big return drops out of the window. In the notebook the 30-day rolling vol leaps from 31% to 59%, holds near 57% for a month, then collapses to 27% around day 35. Positions built on that would whipsaw you in and out — untradeable.

EWMA — let the shock fade gently. An exponentially weighted moving average, σ²ₜ = (1−λ)·r²ₜ₋₁ + λ·σ²ₜ₋₁ with λ between 0.90 and 0.99, gives more weight to recent data. After the same Netflix shock the EWMA reading glides down smoothly (72% → 50% → 39% → 38%) instead of cliff-crashing — far more tradable, and exactly the intuition GARCH formalises.

GARCH(1,1) and the three faces of volatility

The workhorse forecast is GARCH(1,1): σ²ₜ = γ·V + α·r²ₜ₋₁ + β·σ²ₜ₋₁, where the three coefficients sum to one. You don't need to memorise it if you see that each term encodes one empirical characteristic of volatility:

characteristicwhat it meansGARCH term
Clustershigh vol is followed by high vol; regimes persist (the VIX sits at 15-20 in calm phases, 20-30 in stress)β·σ²ₜ₋₁
Depends on recent returnsthe latest moves shape current conditionsα·r²ₜ₋₁
Mean-revertsany value you see today was already reached in the past; vol drifts back to a long-run levelγ·V

Notice volatility is unlike price: prices trend for years, but volatility just oscillates around its mean and clusters. In theory you find α, β, γ by maximum likelihood; in practice traders simply assume α between 0.01 and 0.1 and β between 0.9 and 1, then set γ = 1 − α − β. Across four US indices these assumed parameters still forecast the trend correctly — proof that exact parameters matter less than the structure.

Contextualise the forecast too. A vol number alone is like saying "I scored 14" without naming the sport. Rank your forecast against history over several windows (20/40/60/80/100 days). If a 22% forecast sits at the 89th percentile of the last 100 days, you are forecasting a high-volatility regime; if it sits at the 25th percentile, a calm one.

3. Three kinds of volatility

Before trading, distinguish three labels:

  • Historical (HV) — the past. Definite, known, undisputed.
  • Implied (IV) — the future. It isn't forecast directly; it is backed out of an option's traded premium by inverting Black-Scholes. When someone bids a premium, they are implying how volatile they expect the underlying to be.
  • Realized (RV) — the volatility actually realized over a contract's life. It is a range/time-period concept, not "present" volatility; books often use it interchangeably with historical, but strictly it lives inside the context of a specific contract.

4. The variance premium and the one equation

Here is why volatility trading makes money: implied volatility exceeds realized volatility most of the time (about 77% of months in the simulation). People overpay for insurance against the unknown. So you sell the option rich at the implied volatility and let it settle on the cheaper realized volatility — pocketing the gap, the variance premium.

A volatility trade's profit is premium(IV) minus premium(RV). A Taylor expansion collapses that messy difference into the equation you will see again and again:

PnL = Vega × (IV − RV)

Vega is the Greek measuring how premium responds to a change in volatility, so the formula simply says: your average profit equals your position's Vega times the gap between implied and realized volatility. In the practice notebook, for a one-year ATM call the Vega is 0.394 per vol point, so a six-point gap (IV 30% vs RV 24%) predicts a profit of 2.37 — and the exact premium difference is 2.37 too, confirming the approximation.

From hedging to a pure vol trade

How does an option become a "volatility" position? Through delta hedging. Take a one-year 100-strike call on a $100 stock at 30% volatility: it is worth $11.92 with a delta of 0.56, so you sell 0.56 shares to neutralise price. When the stock jumps to 110, the call becomes $18.14 with delta 0.68, and you re-hedge the extra 0.12. Keep replicating and the price exposure cancels out, leaving a position whose profit is driven purely by volatility. In practice continuous re-hedging is costly — slippage and brokerage — which is why later lectures look for optimal hedging schedules.

5. Structuring the trades

Covered call — the retail staple

Hold the underlying and sell a call against it (buy the stock at 100, sell the 100-call for $3). Your upside is capped above the strike, but you keep the premium as passive income, and half your edge comes from the option, half from the stock. The BXM ETF runs this on the S&P 500: from 1993 to 2022 it returned about 7.1% versus the index's 9.4% — lower, but with less volatility and a better Sharpe ratio and drawdown.

"Vega wounds, gamma kills." A wrong directional (gamma) short can run to infinity — short at 241 and watch it spike to 500 or beyond. A wrong volatility (Vega) short merely wounds: because volatility mean-reverts, an adverse spike tends to reverse, and your loss heals. That is comforting, not a licence to short forever — you still must close by the contract's expiry.

Harvesting the weekend premium — four iterations

The session models how a real strategy is built and refined. The idea: IV exceeds RV, so sell an at-the-money straddle; weekends concentrate the premium because traders fear weekend news on Friday and relax on Monday.

iterationoutcome
1 · sell weekend SPY straddle Friday, close Mondayworked until COVID, then crashed; data only exists from 2018
2 · sell weekly SPY (expiring Friday), close Mondayhistory back to 2011; lost money at the 2016 election and COVID
3 · same, but only when VIX < 98th percentilethe first healthy equity curve — skip trading when the market is extremely volatile
4 · hold to next Friday instead of closing Mondaydecays — anything can happen over a full week

The winner is iteration 3: roughly 29% return, 27% volatility, and a 71% win rate. The lesson is the process — start from a theoretically sound idea, then back-test variations across market conditions to learn when to switch the strategy on and off.

Trading earnings — the implied jump

Implied volatility rises into an earnings date (opinion splits on a good or bad number) and crashes the moment the result is out. Two ways to play it: buy a straddle about ten days before and close the day before earnings (a modest 43% win rate, but winners about 1.7× losers); or capture the IV jump itself using a front-week contract (expiring just after earnings) against a second-week contract.

The implied-jump formula. Total variance equals normal variance plus event variance, and the expected event move is √(2/π)·σ_E. With a one-day implied vol of 50% and a one-week implied vol of 20%, the implied jump works out to about 2.5-2.6%. You then compare it to history: if the average past jump is ~3% and you're forecasting 2.6% (near the top of the range), the options are rich and worth selling.

Choosing the strike scientifically

Don't just "buy the cheap option" or "sell the expensive one" — both are wrong. For each candidate strike, compute the premium at its implied volatility (its live market price) and the premium at the realized volatility (Black-Scholes at, say, 30%), and sell the strike with the largest gap — that is where the variance premium is biggest. In the worked grid the 105 strike's gap of 1.295 beat all others. One execution caveat: the last traded price isn't the price you can actually trade, and settlement can flip an at-the-money option from in- to out-of-the-money, so don't blindly hold to expiry.

6. The closing caution: zero only on average

To drive home that PnL = Vega × (IV − RV) is an expectation, the lecture simulates a straddle when IV = RV = 30%, where there should be no edge. The Monte-Carlo mean P&L is indeed ≈ 0 — yet individual trades swing from −54 to +8. Never judge a single volatility trade; the equation only holds when you average over many.

7. The takeaways

  • There is no true volatility — only estimators. Pick one (Parkinson) and contextualise it against history.
  • Parkinson is more precise than close-to-close but biased low (~0.81) because it misses overnight gaps; Garman-Klass/Rogers-Satchell/Yang-Zhang add more inputs.
  • Forecast with care: 30-day rolling jumps-and-crashes; EWMA smooths it; GARCH(1,1) encodes clustering (β), recent returns (α) and mean-reversion (γ), with α+β+γ = 1.
  • HV / IV / RV are distinct; the variance premium (IV > RV) is the edge, harvested via PnL = Vega × (IV − RV).
  • Delta-hedging turns options into pure vol trades; covered calls give retail passive income; "Vega wounds, gamma kills".
  • Structure trades where premium concentrates — weekends (sell only when VIX is below the 98th percentile) and earnings (the implied jump) — and select strikes by the largest IV-vs-RV gap.
