General Trading Theory

You are a trader first, an options trader second. There is nothing magical about options.
Euan Sinclair
options since 1995 · PhD physics
THE PROBLEM
Beginners think edge is everywhere (the moron) or that one clever model beats the market forever (the theorist). Both blow up.
YOUR GOAL
Master the trading pyramid — edge, then risk, then psychology — and start every idea from an observation, not a method.
THE EDGE
Two kinds: fleeting inefficiencies (trade big) and persistent risk premia (selling options/insurance — a career).
THE PYRAMID THREADING THE WHOLE SESSION
1 · EDGE
Positive expected value. The ONLY thing that makes money. Without it nothing else matters.
2 · RISK
Everything that isn't edge. Keeps you alive long enough to reach the long run.
3 · PSYCHOLOGY
The smallest sliver. Can't save you if you have no edge or risk control.

1 · Edge = expected value

Edge = positive expected value = the probability-weighted average outcome:

EV = Σ pᵢ · xᵢ

The canonical example — a coin that wins 55%, ±\$1:

EV = 0.55(+1) + 0.45(−1) = +0.10

10¢ is the average; a single toss is only +1 or −1. The edge appears only in the long run — so survive to get there.

Tip: the magic is in the observation ("stocks rise the last 5 min of the month"), not the method. Start simple — Jim Simons' most-used tool is OLS.

Short runs are dominated by luck; only the long run reveals the +0.10 edge.

Warning — win-rate ≠ edge: a die that wins ⅔ of the time (+\$0.40 / −\$1) has EV −0.067. Betting favourites & selling far-OTM options "win" often yet lose long-run.

2 · Same edge, different risk · sampling error

CoinPayoffWin-rateEVStd/100
A±\$155%+0.109.8
B+\$10 / −\$110%+0.1032.5

Identical EV, but B is ~3.3× riskier. EV alone never tells you the whole story — you must add risk.

Sampling error in finance is inverted. A physicist's instrument is imprecise; in finance you know exactly you made \$30, but you don't know the true edge behind it — and it keeps changing.

A batsman scores 100 (exact result) but that's not his average — Bradman's career average was 99.94, the next-best ever ~60. One result ≠ true ability. Over 30 trades a true 0.10 edge can read anywhere in [−0.20, +0.40].

3 · Risk = everything that isn't edge

One thing can go right (your edge); everything else works against you. Even a good strategy loses over short windows from pure luck:

SharpeWindowP(loss)
1.51 year6.7%
1.52 years1.7%
5 (HFT)1 month7.5%
5 (HFT)1 year~0%

A Sharpe-1.5 strategy loses ~7% of years on luck alone. Knowing your expected Sharpe lets you tell luck from a broken strategy.

Model risk: maybe the edge was never there / decayed. Need a reason it works; stop when the reason goes (end-of-month drift = US payroll inflows).
Unpredicted factors: options traders predict volatility; stock/FX/rates/dividends are risk → isolate edge, hedge the rest.
Meta-risks & fraud: 9/11 shut the market; biggest blow-ups are theft/fraud (Leeson, Kerviel, Madoff, FTX). Leeson was trader and risk manager → need an independent risk layer. Don't put all eggs in one basket.
Black swans: unimaginable events (an index restructuring changing dividend risk), not merely big ones. Hedge the consequences, not the probabilities — but don't over-hedge your edge away.

4 · EMH & the two kinds of edge

EMH = you can't profit from given information. Not literally true, but a great approximation ("the earth is a sphere" vs old "flat"). You live in the small exceptions — the pilot flies a great circle but still avoids the mountains. EMH tells you where to look: constrained markets (early crypto), info asymmetries (FX front-running, commodities), participants with different goals.

Tip: options tend to be overpriced — a 150-year-old effect (noted in an 1905 book). Selling them earns a risk premium, but un-hedged tails ruin you: 97% of short-premium trades profit, yet the worst is −\$19. Hedge the consequence.

5 · Don't be a hero — sizing turns edge into survival

Even with a real edge, betting too big destroys you. The growth-optimal bet on a 55/45 coin is the Kelly fraction f* = p − q = 0.10 — tiny. Make lots of small bets and let the edge play out over a career.

Bet sizeMedian wealth (×, start 1)P(ruin)
All-in (f=1.0)0.000100%
Full-Kelly (0.10)4.490%
Half-Kelly (0.05)3.080%

All-in has the edge yet almost surely goes broke. Sensible sizing = survive to the long run.

30-second glossary

Edge — positive expected value (probability-weighted average). The only thing that makes money.
Expected value (EV) — Σ pᵢxᵢ; realized only over many trials.
Win-rate — how often you win; NOT edge (can be high with negative EV).
Sharpe ratio — return per unit risk; sets how often luck makes you lose.
Inefficiency — arbitrage/mispricing; transient → trade big.
Risk premium — paid to bear a risk others avoid (insurance, selling options); persistent.
Black swan — unimaginable event; hedge the consequence, not the probability.
Kelly fraction — growth-optimal bet size (p−q for even-money); use half-Kelly in practice.
Meta-risk — outside the game (market closure, fraud, restructuring).
