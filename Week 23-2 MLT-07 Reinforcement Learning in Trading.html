Reinforcement Learning in Trading

The dream: a machine that invents its own strategy by trial-and-error — reward and punishment, like a gamer learning Pong. Deep RL = optimal control (the Bellman equation) + deep neural networks. An agent reads a market state, takes an action (buy/sell/hold), earns a reward, and learns the value of each move. Its superpower over greedy ML is delayed gratification — foregoing a small reward now for a bigger one later.

1 · Bias–variance: the over-fitting trap  (step table)

Truth is a quadratic + noise. Fit it three ways and judge on an unseen right-hand tail. More parameters fit the noise, not the signal — and detonate out-of-sample. RL / deep nets live on the high-variance end, so finance favours simple models.

ModelParametersTrain R²Out-of-sample RMSEVerdict
degree 1 (line)2+0.3812.8under-fit (bias)
degree 2 (quadratic)3+0.803.3well-fit ✓
degree 1516+0.861,076,237OVER-fit (variance)

Higher train R² ≠ better. Degree-15 wins on training and loses catastrophically on data it never saw. There is no universal "right" parameter count — match it to the data's true complexity (a PCA scree plot can hint at it).

2 · Why finance is hard #1 — fat tails

Black–Scholes assumes Gaussian returns. Real markets have fat left tails — crashes are orders of magnitude more likely than a normal curve allows. Worse, returns behave like Cauchy / Lévy-stable laws whose mean & variance never converge.

QuantityValueMeaning
P(|move| > 5σ) Gaussian5.73 × 10⁻⁷"impossible"

ratio~219,000×more likely than normal predicts

Neural nets assume stable distributions (cats stay cats); markets are a moving target — "kind of a cat, maybe a fox". Be cautious.

3 · Why finance is hard #2 — perception bias

Tom shows two charts; the eye cannot tell which is predictable. A random walk is full of "patterns" (head-and-shoulders, support/resistance) yet the next tick is a coin toss. Only autocorrelation makes a chart tradable — and every technical indicator is just a flavour of it.

Serieslag-1 autocorrelation of returnsvs band ±0.031Verdict

If you trade on price action, your inputs must carry some autocorrelation/cross-correlation — otherwise it is gambling. Pros often use only simple indicators (EMA, a volatility measure).

4 · Why finance is hard #3 — predict returns, not prices

A viral blog "perfectly predicted" prices — by accidentally predicting yesterday's price for today. The price fit looks like genius; the direction is a coin toss. Only direction makes money. Always model R = (P_t − P_t-1)/P_t-1.

5 · The Bellman equation & the Q-table

Q(s, a) = r + γ · maxa′ Q(s′, a′)

The Q-value is the unrealised reward of taking action a in state s. Training steps backward in time (a look-ahead that is fine because it's training, not trading). For each state pick the action with the highest Q.

State (momentum bucket)Q[buy]Q[hold]Q[sell]→ action
strong-up0.420.18−0.30BUY
flat0.050.110.04HOLD
strong-down−0.280.120.39SELL

Our engine: 6 momentum buckets × 3 positions × 3 actions; reward = new_position × next_return − cost·|Δposition|; update Q ← Q + α(r + γ·maxQ′ − Q). The notebook also reproduces the lecture's marshmallow Q-table exactly (Wait = 1.06, 1.18, 1.31, 1.46, 1.62, 1.80, 2.00 with γ = 0.9 — Wait beats Eat at every step). Deep RL only swaps this table for a neural network when the state space explodes to quadrillions of cells.

6 · The testing ladder — never train on price first

The cardinal discipline: validate on toy problems before the wild. A correctly-built agent must learn a pure sine wave; if it can't, the plumbing is broken. Then add noise, then let it loose on the real trending market. Numbers below are the notebook's real out-of-sample results.

Test seriesWhat the agent should doResult (out-of-sample)Reading

Profit only appears where there's genuine predictability. On the real market the agent is long 62% of the time — it learned the trend is up — but its in-and-out timing trails plain buy-and-hold. RL is not a silver bullet.

On a strong trend a pure-P&L reward collapses to buy-and-hold (Buffett's advice — dumb, or smart?). Other levers from the lecture: profit-per-tick, Sharpe ratio, punish long hold-times, punish drawdowns.

8 · What "deep" adds — a neural net from scratch

When the state has thousands of features the Q-table is astronomically large → replace it with a network. Inputs → weights (dot products) → hidden layer (sum + ReLU) → output (softmax → probabilities summing to 1). Train by nudging weights down the steepest gradient (SGD / Adam).

ReLU isn't strictly differentiable at the kink — engineers just set that point's derivative to 0 and it works beautifully. Adam = SGD with an auto-adapting learning rate.

Lessons from the trenches

  1. RL over-fits easily — minimise parameters; a small net is more robust than a giant one.
  2. It learns basic patterns first (momentum, mean-reversion); compare its P&L to simple strategies to check it isn't just rediscovering them.
  3. A zoo of hyper-parameters (activation, loss, reward, optimizer, network size) — config is part skill, part proprietary.
  4. Reward design is the real lever — simple rewards drift to buy-and-hold; richer rewards give interesting behaviour.
  5. Not a silver bullet: in Tom's experience RL has never outperformed a well-built traditional strategy.
  6. Explainability is the deployment blocker — you can't tell clients "the machine did it" after a drawdown.
  7. Get market experience first — data scientists often make rigid traders; markets evolve (cats won't, markets will).
