When Simple Rules Stop Working

Week 10-1 · Advanced Statistics for Quant Strategies · ASQ-01 (Part 1)

This lesson is a doorway into time-series modelling — a subject universities spread across a whole semester, compressed here into the part that actually matters for trading. The goal isn't to memorise the mathematics (we happily trust the statisticians who built the foundations); it's to grip the key ideas: why simple rules fail, what an autoregressive model really says, why we obsess over "stationarity", and how the ARIMA family ties it all together.

1. The motivation: a moving-average rule that disappoints

Moving-average crossovers are a lovely place to start trading. They're simple, intuitive, and often make decent money. But every few years markets go through a crazy period — a crash, a pandemic, a war, a rate-hike cycle — and that's exactly when these rules fall apart. The instructor calls these turning points regime shifts: something fundamental changes how the whole market behaves, and a rule tuned to yesterday's world suddenly bleeds money.

To feel it, we run a 10-day / 30-day crossover on a liquid index spanning the COVID crash. Go long when the short average is above the long one, short otherwise. The result is humbling: the crossover returned about +26% while simply buying and holding returned about +47%. If you're going to the trouble of building a strategy, you'd like it to beat doing nothing — and here it didn't.

Look at the returns, not just the price. Prices trend upward over time, but the daily returns hover around zero — with a twist. The wiggles cluster: stormy stretches of big swings, then calm stretches of small ones. That volatility clustering is the first clue that markets aren't purely random, which is precisely why a smarter, more adaptive model might have an edge.

2. The autoregressive idea

Many traders start with a hunch: if it went up, it'll probably keep going up. An autoregressive (AR) model turns that hunch into something you can measure. Ordinary regression predicts y from other variables x1, x2, x3. Autoregression is the same machinery pointed at the variable's own past:

r_t = α + β · r_(t-1) + ε_t        # AR(1): today's return from yesterday's

Here α is the baseline return when there's no momentum, and β measures how strongly yesterday carries into today. The sign of β tells a story: between 0 and 1 the series is trending (up tends to follow up); between 0 and −1 it's mean-reverting (up tends to follow down); near 0, yesterday tells you nothing at all. An AR(2) adds the day before that, AR(p) the last p days — same idea, more memory.

3. Stationarity: the quiet assumption behind everything

Before we trust any of this, we must ask whether the series is stationary. Strip away the formal definitions and stationarity just means stability: the mean stays roughly the same through the years, and so does the standard deviation. Compute the average return in 2018 and again in 2024 and they'll look alike — that's stationary. Do the same for prices and they're wildly different, because prices trend.

This is the punchline: prices are non-stationary, returns are (almost always) stationary. That's why every model in this lecture is built on returns — or, equivalently, on differenced prices (today's price minus yesterday's). The statistics we lean on only behave when the process underneath is stable.

Confirming it with a test. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test exists for the part of us that wants a number, not just a chart. Its null hypothesis is "non-stationary", so you read the p-value: below 0.05 means stationary. On our weekly data the prices came back at p = 0.099 (non-stationary, as expected) and the returns at p = 0.0001 (emphatically stationary). Textbook.

4. ACF and PACF: which lags actually matter

If returns depend on their past, which past — yesterday, last week, last month? Two diagnostic plots answer this. The autocorrelation function (ACF) measures how today's return correlates with the return k steps ago, capturing both the direct link and all the indirect ones that travel through the days in between. The partial autocorrelation function (PACF) isolates the direct relationship only, stripping out the chain.

Both plots come with a shaded blue band — the significance shadow, roughly ±1.96/√N. Bars that poke outside the band signal genuine autocorrelation worth modelling; bars inside are just noise. As a rule of thumb, the PACF suggests how many AR terms (p) you need and the ACF suggests how many MA terms (q). For a nearly-efficient return series, most bars sit demurely inside the band — a reminder that easy edges are rare.

5. A rolling AR(1) strategy — and an honest scorecard

Real systematic trading is dynamic. Rather than fitting one model once, we slide a window (here 100 weeks), re-fit an AR(1) at every step, and forecast just one week ahead: long if the forecast is positive, short if negative. Then we step forward and do it again. This is a rolling forecast, and it mirrors how professionals actually operate — always re-estimating, never assuming a model fitted years ago still holds.

The trap in one line: right 54% of the time, yet it still lost money. How? The losing weeks were simply bigger than the winning ones — asymmetric payoffs. Worse, the instructor noted the very same model outperformed buy-and-hold on one data pull and underperformed on another: the result is unstable. The logic is sound and the engine is genuinely professional, but a single lag is too simple for production. AR(1) quietly assumes next week will behave like the last hundred — and that assumption shatters at every regime shift.

6. ARIMA: adding the missing pieces

The fix is to enrich the model, and that's what ARIMA(p, d, q) does. The acronym is three ideas bolted together:

PartNameWhat it contributes
AR(p)AutoRegressiveuses the last p values of the series
I(d)Integrateddifferences the series d times to force stationarity
MA(q)Moving-average of errorsmodels the last q forecast errors to self-correct
Don't confuse the two "moving averages". The MA in ARIMA has nothing to do with the SMA/LMA price averages from the crossover. Here, "moving average" means the moving average of the model's errors — the gap between what it predicted and what actually happened.

Why would past errors help? Picture a class of students predicting their own exam scores each week. One student consistently guesses two marks low; another consistently guesses high. Once you spot that recurring bias, you can correct their next prediction by it. ARIMA's MA term does exactly this for returns: it watches the pattern in past mistakes and folds the correction into the next forecast. The I term, meanwhile, handles non-stationary inputs by differencing — turning stubborn prices into well-behaved changes. Choosing the order (p, d, q) — with ADF guiding d, the PACF guiding p, and the ACF guiding q — is the craft we build on in Part 2.

The one-paragraph version

Simple moving-average rules underperform because they can't adapt to regime shifts, so we move to statistical models. An AR(1) makes the "momentum hunch" measurable — r_t = α + β·r_(t-1) + ε_t — but only works on stationary data, which is why we model returns (ADF p < 0.05), not prices. The ACF/PACF plots reveal which lags carry real signal. A rolling AR(1) that re-fits each week and forecasts one step ahead is the professional pattern, yet on our data it was right 54% of the time and still lost money — a single lag is too simple and the result is unstable. The remedy is ARIMA(p, d, q): autoregression on past values, Integration (differencing) for stationarity, and a moving average of past errors to self-correct — the foundation for the order-selection work that follows.
