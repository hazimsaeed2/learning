General Trading Theory

Week 13-2 · OTS-01 — the opening lecture of the options module, taught by Euan Sinclair, who has traded options since 1995 as a market maker, prop trader, and at hedge funds and investment banks.

This is the first lecture of a course about options, and yet the instructor spends the entire session refusing to talk about options. His reason is the most important idea you will take from the whole module: there are two words in the title, option and trading, and by far the more important is trading. You cannot be an options trader without first being a trader. Nothing about an instrument makes you money simply because you discovered it. So before any Greeks or volatility surfaces, you need a general philosophy of how trading actually works — and that philosophy is what this lesson delivers.

The two beginners, and the sweet spot between them

People arrive at the markets making one of two opposite mistakes. The first is the moron, who thinks edge is everywhere — watch a YouTube video, follow three people online, and easy money awaits. He fails because he tries to monetize advantages that don't exist, racking up transaction costs. The second is the theorist, usually from a science or engineering background, who is certain he can out-think the market with one brilliant model that will pay him for life. He fails too, because the market is not a fixed puzzle; it is a competitive, adaptive system made of humans, and any idea that works has to keep adapting. The truth sits in the middle: making money is hard but not impossible, and you survive by finding robust, obvious observations and trading them in an adaptive way.

The trading pyramid

Sinclair organizes everything into a pyramid, drawn deliberately with the font size of each layer matching its importance.

LayerWhat it isWhy it ranks there
1. EdgeAn unfair advantage — positive expected value.The only thing that makes money. Without it, nothing else matters.
2. Risk managementEverything that isn't edge.Done badly it loses money; done well it merely keeps you alive long enough to reach the long run.
3. PsychologyEmotional discipline.The smallest layer. A Zen master with no edge still loses, like buying lottery tickets calmly.

The ranking is strict: if you have no edge, your risk management and psychology are irrelevant; if you have edge but no risk management, your composure won't save you. This is why, in any bank or fund, the traders who find edge are paid the most — finding edge is the hardest thing.

What edge really is

Edge means a trade has positive expected value — the probability-weighted average of all outcomes. The textbook example is a loaded coin that lands heads 55% of the time. You call heads, winning a dollar or losing a dollar, so the expected value is 0.55 × (+1) + 0.45 × (−1) = +10 cents. But notice: ten cents is never a result you actually get on any single toss — you get +1 or −1. The edge is an average, realized only over many trials.

Number of tossesRealized edge per toss% of runs that profit
1+0.1156%
10+0.0949%
100+0.1081%
1000+0.10100%

This is why a recurring theme of the lecture is surviving to the long run. Averages only mean something over many trials. Over ten tosses you might well be down; over a thousand the edge is almost certain to show. Sinclair's boss — a member of the Blackjack Hall of Fame who counted cards alongside Ed Thorp — distilled it to: find an edge, then stay in the game.

Winning often is not an edge. Consider a six-sided die where landing on 1–4 (two-thirds of the time) pays \$0.40 but landing on 5–6 loses \$1. You win two times out of three, yet the expected value is −6.7 cents. A high win-rate with poor payoffs is a long-term loser. This is exactly the trap of selling far out-of-the-money options: you win almost every time, but that says nothing about whether you have edge.

Two bets, same edge, very different risk

Expected value alone never tells the whole story. Take two coins with identical EV of +10 cents: coin A pays ±\$1 and wins 55% of the time; coin B pays +\$10 or −\$1 and wins only 10% of the time. Over a hundred trials, coin A's results have a standard deviation around 9.8, while coin B's is about 32.5 — roughly three times as risky for the same edge. So edge is the first gate, but risk is the second, and the two together decide whether a trade is good.

Sampling error means something different in finance

When a physicist reports a measurement "within error", he means his instrument is imprecise — the true value is exact, his reading is fuzzy. In finance it is the reverse. You know exactly that you made thirty dollars; what you don't know is how representative that thirty dollars is of your true edge, which is itself always changing. Sinclair's analogy is cricket: a batsman scores exactly 100 in an innings, but that single score tells you little about his true average. Don Bradman's career test average was 99.94 — and the next-highest in history is around 60 — yet no innings ever is the average. One result is never the underlying ability.

Risk is everything that isn't edge

In trading, only one thing can go right — the edge you predicted — while everything else in the world can go wrong. Sinclair walks through the categories.

Variance, or plain luck

Even a genuinely good strategy loses over short windows purely by chance. A strategy with a Sharpe ratio of 1.5 — which is excellent; Warren Buffett's best 20 years scored about 0.8 — still has a losing year roughly 7% of the time. That doesn't mean the edge is gone; it's just luck. Knowing your expected Sharpe is what lets you tell the difference: a Sharpe-5 high-frequency shop losing money over a whole month is suspicious and probably overfit, whereas a Sharpe-1.5 strategy having a bad year is simply normal.

Model risk, unpredicted factors, and meta-risks

The edge may never have been there, or may have decayed — and the statistics of your own results are a terrible early-warning system, because proving something stopped working needs as many observations as proving it worked. The fix is to know why an edge exists (US month-end stock drift comes from payroll and retirement inflows) so you can stop the moment the reason disappears, without waiting years. Then there are factors you simply don't predict: an options trader predicts volatility, while stock moves, currencies, interest rates and dividends are risks to be hedged or isolated away. And beyond the game itself lie the meta-risks — 9/11 closed the market entirely; wars and inflation strike from outside.

The biggest blow-ups are fraud, not Greeks. Sumitomo, Jérôme Kerviel, Nick Leeson, Bernie Madoff, FTX — the truly enormous losses come from cheating and hidden risk, not from exotic option exposures. The lesson is organizational: Nick Leeson destroyed Barings because he was both the trader and his own risk manager. You always need an independent layer of risk oversight, and you never put all your eggs in one basket.

Black swans

A black swan is not merely a big event — a market crash is not a black swan, because crashes happen all the time and if one ruins you, you were doing something wrong. A true black swan is unimaginable: the name comes from Europeans who never thought to ask the probability of a black swan until they reached Australia and saw one. Sinclair was hit twice by index and ETF restructurings that suddenly changed the dividends his options book depended on — events he could not have imagined because an index is, after all, just a number someone made up and can change at will.

Hedge the consequence, not the probability. You can't predict a black swan, but you can limit what it does to you. Risk management is not about how likely an event is — with a true black swan you don't even know the event — it is about the consequences. That said, don't over-hedge: capping a loss too tightly can throw away so much edge that you no longer have positive expected value. You hedge against things that would ruin your life, not against everything unpleasant.

The efficient market hypothesis, and where money lives

A market is efficient with respect to some information if you cannot profit from that information. The EMH isn't literally true — even its authors never claimed that — but it is a very good approximation, the way "the earth is a sphere" is far better than "the earth is flat." You make your living in the small exceptions, like a pilot who flies a great-circle route but still has to avoid the mountains. Crucially, the EMH even tells you where to look: markets that are constrained (early crypto, when the big funds couldn't easily get in), markets with information asymmetries (FX desks can legally front-run; commodity giants know more than you), and situations where participants have different goals.

Out of these exceptions come the two kinds of edge that matter.

InefficiencyRisk premium
What it isAn arbitrage or mispricing — buy gold in London, sell it dearer in New York.Someone pays you to take a risk they don't want — insurance, or selling options.
LifespanTransient — others notice and trade it away.Persistent — insurance demand never goes away.
How to play itTrade it as big as you practically can, because it won't last.Build a career on it; it can be the basis of a business.

The risk premium is the heart of options trading. Just as house insurance is a deliberately bad bet for you and a sound business for the insurer, options tend to be overpriced — a risk premium paid to whoever is willing to be short and bear the danger of a blow-up. This is not a new discovery: Sinclair owns a trading book from 1905 in which the author treats the overpricing of options as already-old common knowledge. An effect that has persisted for 150 years is a reasonable bet to persist through your career.

The edge is easy to find; the risk is hard to manage. Selling options has clear positive expected value, and in a simulation 97% of short-premium trades are profitable — but the rare losing trade is enormous, and if you are simply short options when the market crashes, you blow up. The edge is real; surviving it requires hedging the consequences. Trading options safely is like defusing a bomb: perfectly doable if you know the wrinkles, lethal if you don't.

Don't be a hero

Even a real edge will destroy you if you bet too big. Betting everything you own on a 55% coin still has positive expected value — and is still a terrible idea. The growth-optimal stake on that coin is the Kelly fraction, here just p − q = 0.10, a tiny slice of your capital. The simulation makes the point brutally: betting all-in has the edge yet goes broke essentially every time, while a full-Kelly or half-Kelly bettor compounds steadily upward.

Bet sizeMedian ending wealth (start = 1)Probability of ruin

You are not paid more for playing on hard mode. Make many small bets, let the edge compound, and resist the urge to take one giant shot — the people who make billions in a single trade rarely do it twice.

The one-paragraph version. You are a trader first and an options trader second; nothing about an instrument makes money on its own. Trading is a pyramid: edge (positive expected value — the only thing that pays), then risk management (everything that isn't edge, which keeps you alive to reach the long run), then a sliver of psychology. A 55% coin has EV +10¢ realized only over many trials, so survive the long run and never bet like a hero (Kelly is tiny). Win-rate is not edge, and equal-EV bets can differ wildly in risk. Risk includes model decay, luck (a Sharpe-1.5 strategy loses ~7% of years), unpredicted factors, fraud (have an independent risk manager), and black swans (hedge the consequences, not the probabilities). The EMH is a great approximation; you live in its exceptions — fleeting inefficiencies (arbitrage, trade big) and persistent risk premia (selling options, a career). Always start from an observation and quantify it as simply as possible: the magic is in the observation, never in the method.

Continuation addendum: compliance and the trading process

The source packet adds an important control layer before the trading theory even begins. The option examples are educational, not recommendations, and derivatives can create large losses quickly. More practically, Sinclair emphasizes that market rules and ethical norms differ across venues, so a trader should not infer legality from first principles. If you change a strategy, order type, execution route, or operating workflow, get manager or compliance approval in writing. That written chain is part of risk management.

The larger process rule is: do not start from a method. Start from an observation. The observation can be simple, such as an unusual month-end move or a persistent volatility premium. Then quantify it with the simplest test that can disprove it. Only after that do you split edge from risk, test costs and adverse selection, and ask whether the strategy can become a live business.

StepQuestionFailure mode
ObservationWhat phenomenon did you notice?Starting with a model and searching for a story.

ImplementationCan it survive costs, liquidity, mandate limits, compliance, and execution?Stopping at a backtest.
ReviewIs live evidence statistical, or just a tempting story?Changing a 1000-observation idea after a 20-observation sample.

Continuation addendum: edge depends on who is trading

The source summary makes the edge/risk distinction concrete by participant. A directional trader may be paid for a direction forecast, while volatility, rates, liquidity, and execution are risks. A market maker may be paid for bid/ask spread and flow information, while inventory and adverse selection are risks. An options volatility trader may be paid for a volatility forecast or risk premium, while stock direction, dividends, rates, skew, jumps, and liquidity are risks. The same instrument can therefore contain different edges for different participants.

Continuation addendum: review, automation, and psychology

Trade review is meant to keep you immersed in the market, not to provide an excuse to rewrite rules from a tiny live sample. The addendum notebook simulates this trap: a strategy with 1000 observations can have a 20-day live window where a small cluster of losses appears on one weekday. That story feels meaningful because you lived through it, but it is not enough evidence to change the strategy.

The automation rule is similarly practical: automate needs, not wants. If a task is cheap, informative, and keeps you in contact with the market, doing it manually may be better than hiding it behind a report that you stop reading. Psychology sits in the same place as risk management: it is a guardrail, not edge. It can stop you from overtrading, staring at P&L, staying in a position only because it is down, or setting impossible outcome goals. It cannot make a strategy profitable if the edge is not there.
