Strategy Building in Equities — is it ready to go live?

Week 4-2 · EFS-01 · Equity, FX & Futures Strategies · a plain-language walkthrough

Every back-test that loses money is easy to reject. The dangerous ones are the back-tests that make money — because a profit number alone tells you almost nothing about whether the strategy will survive contact with the real market. This lecture is the instructor's personal checklist: the handful of metrics he runs, and the thumb-rules he applies, before he is willing to risk capital. We walk through each one on his own example — a moving-average crossover on the Nifty index from 2000 to 2016, 4,235 trading days of real data — and we let the numbers deliver an honest verdict.

1. What even counts as a trading system?

A complete trading system must answer four questions automatically: what to buy, when to buy, how much to buy, and when to exit — and crucially, it must be testable on historical data. Tips from a friend or a TV anchor fail the last requirement: there is no accountability, no record, no way to know if the "edge" was ever real. The instructor's deeper point is that you are the weakest link. Emotions, fat-finger typos, hesitation — these are what algorithmic trading removes. Remove the weakest link, and the weakest link is usually the human.

Our example system: compute a fast moving average (SMA, 15 days) and a slow one (LMA, 40 days). Go long when the fast crosses above the slow; exit when it crosses back below. Run on the Nifty, it produces 48 completed trades and grows capital 3.95×.

Result on Nifty 2000-2016Value
completed trades48
strategy growth3.95×
buy & hold the index5.14×
The first lesson, immediately. The strategy "made money" — but plain buy-and-hold made more (5.14× vs 3.95×). Total profit is the metric that lies most. It tells you nothing about the risk you took or whether you simply rode a rising market less efficiently than doing nothing.

2. The overfitting trap — in-sample and out-of-sample

The temptation is to try every (SMA, LMA) combination and keep the best. But with enough parameters you can always find a combination that looks brilliant on past data purely by chance — like drawing a line through ten points with a ten-parameter curve. It fits the past perfectly and predicts nothing. The discipline that defends you: split the history. Optimise on a training slice (in-sample); then test the chosen parameters exactly once on held-out data (out-of-sample). If the two broadly agree, the edge is probably real.

We split the Nifty 80/20. The best combination in the training years was SMA 5 / LMA 40, growing 5.79×. Then we tested that single winner on the years it had never seen:

SMA 5 / LMA 40ResultRead
in-sample (training)5.79×looks superb
out-of-sample (held out)1.19×vs buy & hold 1.34×
conclusionthe winner underperformed buy & hold live — a classic curve-fit
A second safeguard: look at the neighbourhood of your best parameter. If the cell next door gives wildly different results, your "winner" is a lone spike — a fluke. A robust choice sits on a smooth plateau where nearby parameters all behave similarly. The instructor also divides history into market phases (bull, bear, consolidation, distribution) and insists a strategy work across all of them, because you never know which phase you are entering live.

3. Hit ratio, and why it needs normalising

The hit ratio is simply the fraction of trades that made money. But it hides the most important thing: when you win, do you win big? A strategy can win only 30% of the time and still be excellent if those wins dwarf the losses. The instructor's fix is the normalised hit ratio: the share of favourable money in the total of all absolute moves.

NHR = Σ(positive returns) / [ Σ(positive) + Σ|negative| ]

His worked example: 30 winning trades averaging +10%, and 70 losing trades averaging −2%.

StepCalculationValue
plain hit ratio30 ÷ 10030%
total positive money30 × 10%3.0
total negative money70 × 2%1.0
normalised hit ratio3 ÷ (3 + 1)68%

Only three trades in ten win — yet on a normalised basis this is a strong strategy, because the wins are five times the size of the losses. Our own Nifty strategy comes out at a plain hit ratio of 45.8% (22 wins, 26 losses) but a normalised hit ratio of 69.8%, because the average win (+15.2%) is far larger than the average loss (−5.6%). By the thumb-rule that puts it in the "tradeable" band (63-75%), though short of the 75% "pure gold" line.

The thumb-rules. Normalised hit ratio above 75% is pure gold — trade it and don't overthink. Around 63-66% is the instructor's comfortable working range. Below 58% he won't touch a medium-frequency strategy; at 50% you are just tossing a coin.

4. How much to bet — the Kelly fraction

Suppose you have a genuine edge. How much of your capital do you stake each time? Too little and you barely grow; too much and a normal losing streak wipes you out. The Kelly fraction is the bet size that maximises long-run compounded wealth. The clean textbook case the worksheet uses: a 50/50 bet that pays 2:1 — a win pays twice your stake, a loss forfeits it.

f* = (b·p − q) / b = (2 × 0.5 − 0.5) / 2 = 0.25

We can confirm it by brute force — sweeping the bet fraction from 1% to 100% and computing the final wealth after 50 wins and 50 losses:

Bet fractionFinal wealth (start = 10)
10%469
20%2,890
25% ← Kelly optimum3,611
30%2,890
50%10 — right back where you started

The curve rises to a peak at 25% and then collapses; bet 50% and a balanced run of wins and losses leaves you exactly where you began. Notice how betting more than Kelly is actively destructive.

In practice, bet less. The instructor trades only half to 60% of full Kelly, and only after the strategy has settled past its teething problems. Real edges are estimated with error, and full Kelly is brutally volatile — a touch of caution costs little growth and buys a lot of survival.

5. Drawdown — the question investors ask first

Drawdown is how far the equity curve has fallen from its running peak. It is always measured from the historical high — not from where you happened to enter — because you can never know your future starting point. Unsophisticated investors often ask only two questions: what is the profit, and what is the drawdown. The reason drawdown terrifies them is its cruel asymmetry:

If you lose……you must gain just to break even
10%+11.1%
25%+33.3%
50%+100%
90%+900%

Our Nifty strategy hits a maximum drawdown of −40.2%, against an annualised return of only 8.5%. The ratio of return to drawdown is 0.21 — far below the instructor's "comfortable above 2" rule. Yet another red flag.

Drawdown thumb-rules. Below 5% is gold. He likes annual-return-to-drawdown above 2 (above 3 is great). Past 25% drawdown, investors get jittery and start redeeming — often at the worst possible moment.

6. Sharpe ratio — return per unit of risk

If the instructor could keep only one number, it would be the Sharpe ratio: excess return divided by its own volatility. Like a dimensionless number in engineering, it strips away scale and asks the only question that matters — how much return did you earn for the risk you bore? Because returns add over time but volatility grows with the square root of time, you annualise by multiplying the daily Sharpe by √252.

Sharpe(annual) = (mean daily return − daily risk-free) / daily std × √252

StepValue
mean daily strategy return0.00038
minus daily risk-free (~6%/yr)0.00024
divide by daily std (the risk)0.01017
daily Sharpe0.0136
× √252 = annualised Sharpe0.22

A Sharpe of 0.22 is far below the 1.5 floor. Combined with the lost-to-buy-and-hold profit, the 40% drawdown, and the failed out-of-sample test, the verdict is clear and honest: this particular MA strategy is not ready to go live. That is exactly what the metrics are for — to say no before your money does.

Sharpe thumb-rules. Above 3 is pure gold, execute immediately. Around 2-2.5 is the sweet spot. Below 1.5 he doesn't bother. (Hedge funds will run 1.4-1.5; he prefers more cushion.)

7. Stops, leverage and slippage

To choose a stop loss, bucket the losing trades and watch where the cumulative loss piles up. In our strategy about half of all loss money accumulates by the −7% to −8% bucket, which makes that zone a sensible starting point for a stop — you cut the fat tail without being stopped out by ordinary noise. A trailing stop takes this further, tracking a percentage below the position's running peak rather than the entry price.

Leverage is a double-edged sword: borrow to double your position and you double both gains and losses. The instructor only adds leverage once a strategy's Sharpe is in double digits. And never forget slippage — the gap between the price you back-tested and the price you actually get — typically 4-5 basis points per trade for medium-frequency strategies. Subtract brokerage, taxes and slippage before believing any back-test.

8. Reading the market: open interest, cost of carry, and factor models

The second half of the lecture introduces derivatives data you can turn into signals. Open interest is the number of outstanding futures agreements between participants — not the volume traded. (If I buy a contract and later sell it to someone else, volume rises but open interest may not.) Cost of carry is the futures price minus the spot price, which follows from the fair-futures formula F = S·e^(rT).

Pairing open interest with price tells you what the crowd is doing:

Price risingPrice falling
OI risingfresh long build-up (new buyers)fresh shorting (new sellers)
OI fallingshort coveringprofit booking

Finally, a factor model blends several such factors — change in open interest, change in cost of carry, change in delivery volume — into one signal, with weights that sum to one. The catch is scale: if one factor swings over a wide range and another barely moves, the wide one dominates the signal by accident. The fix is to convert every factor to a z-score first — how many standard deviations it sits from its own mean — so all factors speak the same language:

z = (value − mean) / standard deviation

With three factors carrying weights 0.4, 0.5 and 0.1, a day's signal is just the weighted sum of the three z-scores; you buy above a positive threshold and sell below a negative one, optimising both the weights and the thresholds on in-sample data (and, the instructor notes, doing it in Python rather than Excel because the search is computationally heavy).

The go-live checklist

  1. Profit must beat the index or at least your cost of capital — necessary, never sufficient.
  2. Normalised hit ratio: >75% gold, 63-66% tradeable, <58% avoid.
  3. Sharpe (×√252): >3 gold, ~2-2.5 sweet spot, <1.5 skip.
  4. Drawdown: <5% gold, return/DD >2 comfortable, >25% investors flee.
  5. Always split in-sample / out-of-sample; prefer a smooth plateau over a lone spike.
  6. Size with Kelly (the 2:1 flip → 25%), but trade about half-Kelly in reality.
  7. Set stops from the loss distribution; respect leverage and slippage.
  8. Mine derivatives data — open interest, cost of carry, delivery volume — into a z-scored factor model.
The thread running through it all: a profitable back-test is a question, not an answer. The metrics — and the honest out-of-sample test — are how you turn that question into a confident yes or, just as valuably, a disciplined no.

9. Resource addendum - worksheet and expiry mechanics

The official files add a practical layer around the lecture metrics. They show how the Excel sheet is made dynamic, what the assignment expects from an EMA version of the crossover model, how to read derivatives data with quadrant rules, and how the expiry-day VWAP/cash-unwind setup is framed.

Excel OFFSET and data tables

The lecture spends time on the Excel OFFSET function because a back-test sheet should not be hard-coded to one SMA/LMA pair. If the input cells change from 5/40 to 10/50, the moving averages, signals, P&L, CAGR, hit ratio, and drawdown should all update. The Python equivalent is a parameter surface: scan many SMA/LMA combinations and check whether the winner is part of a stable neighborhood, not just a lonely overfit spike.

Derivative data quadrant maps

The official summary separates two diagnostic maps. Pair price with open interest to infer whether new longs, new shorts, short covering, or long profit-taking dominate. Pair price with cost of carry to distinguish strong buying, topping out, futures selling, and cash selling. These are not automatic trades; they are inputs to the factor model.

Delivery volume completes the factor set. The resource note says to compare delivery volume with its own moving average, then use it together with OI and price change. The important engineering point is the same as the notebook's z-score section: put every factor on comparable scale before assigning weights.

Expiry-day VWAP/cash-unwind setup

The in-class VWAP note describes traders who buy cash and short futures to capture cost of carry. If OI stays high and cost of carry does not compress into expiry, those traders may need to unwind cash positions near the end of expiry day. The proposed idea is to short cash around 3:00pm, buy back around 3:20pm after the expected dip, and treat the last 5-10 minutes carefully because institutional buying can appear at lower cash prices.
