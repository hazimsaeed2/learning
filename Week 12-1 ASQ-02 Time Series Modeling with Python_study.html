Time Series Modeling, Part 2: When the Machine Picks, Costs Bite, and Risk Refuses to Sit Still

Week 12-1 · Advanced Statistics for Quant Strategies · ASQ-02 (the conclusion of the two-part series)

Two weeks ago we built ARIMA models by hand, learned what stationarity means, and read ACF and PACF plots to pick an order. That left four loose ends, and this session ties them all up: can we get the machine to pick the ARIMA order for us? What happens to our pretty returns once we pay to trade? Is risk really constant the way our backtests quietly assumed? And if it isn't, how do we forecast it? A reminder before we start: this is intermediate-to-advanced material compressed from a semester into a few hours, so the goal is to understand the ideas and read the results — not to memorise code. And the recurring refrain: there are no silver bullets.

1. A one-minute refresher on ARIMA(p, d, q)

ARIMA stands for AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average, and its three numbers each control one piece:

PartParameterWhat it controls (model on prices)
AR — autoregressivephow many past price terms enter the model
I — integrateddhow many times we difference the series to make it stationary (nothing to do with calculus)
MA — moving averageqhow many past error terms — errors, not a price moving average

And why are there errors at all? Because a model is only ever an approximation. The instructor's favourite analogy: a model is like a map. A political map of India shows you the states; a topographical map shows you the mountains; neither tells you about the open manhole on the street where you're standing at 11 p.m. Every map keeps the features that matter and discards the rest, so reality = model + error by design. ARIMA captures what it can of a price series; the leftover is the error term.

2. Does one hand-picked order fit every asset? No.

Last time we chose (2,1,2) from ACF/PACF plots on a single asset. The obvious question: will that same order work everywhere? The lecture forced (2,1,2) onto five very different US names and found it profitable on only one. We can't download those here, so we built five NIFTY-derived "assets" (weekly, two-weekly, monthly, and the two halves of the weekly series) and ran a rolling one-step ARIMA strategy: forecast next bar, go long if the forecast is above today's price, else short, and compare to buy-and-hold.

The fixed (2,1,2) beat buy-and-hold on none of the five. The point lands exactly as intended: each asset may have its own optimal order, so you can't willy-nilly reuse one combination. That is the motivation for letting an algorithm choose.

3. Automated selection, and why "automated" ≠ "better"

The professional tools are pmdarima.auto_arima and sktime; both run a stepwise search and pick the order that minimises an information criterion. They're offline here, so we reproduced the same idea with a small grid search over statsmodels, choosing the order with the lowest AIC.

auto_arima(y, max_p=2, max_d=2, max_q=2,
           seasonal=False, stepwise=True,
           information_criterion="aic")

What is AIC actually doing? Every model-builder is caught between two failures. Underfitting means the model is too simple — it misses real structure and behaves the same dumb way in the future; its only virtue is simplicity. Overfitting means the model is so flexible it starts memorising noise as if it were signal, and then falls apart on data it hasn't seen. AIC is a scorecard that rewards good fit but charges a penalty for every extra parameter, trying to find the sweet spot between the two.

The honest result. When we let AIC choose, it improved on the manual guess for only one of the five assets — and it often picked simpler models (like a near random-walk). Why? Because AIC balances under- and over-fitting on the training data; it never once looks at profitability or compares against buy-and-hold. Automation is a genuinely useful tool, but it is not automatically superior. As one student asked: what's the point if it can't even rediscover our manual choice? The point is that across many assets it will help on some — and back-testing is precisely the business of discarding most ideas to keep the few durable ones.

4. Transaction costs: where the profits quietly leak out

Every real trade pays commission and crosses a spread. For a liquid US stock the instructor used about 0.03% per trade (0.0003) — and stressed it's both country- and broker-specific. We loaded that onto the NIFTY weekly ARIMA strategy, charging the cost every time the position actually changed:

NIFTY weekly ARIMA(2,1,2)Return
Before costs12.9%
After costs (0.03% per trade, 55 trades)9.3%
Buy & hold over the same window13.5%

The drag here was about 3.3% — modest, because (like the lecture's PayPal) the index is very liquid and we traded only a few dozen times. On a thinner instrument, or a strategy that flips positions constantly, the "net" equity line peels away from the "gross" line dramatically, and many backtests that looked profitable turn into losers.

Takeaway: a return figure with no cost assumption is fiction. Always backtest net of costs, and remember that the more you trade, the more the costs compound against you — the cumulative cost line is a staircase that only ever climbs.

5. Risk does not sit still — volatility clusters

Until now every backtest quietly assumed risk was constant: when the model said "long," we went all-in, the same size every time. But look at any long stretch of returns and you'll see calm spells and stormy spells. We measured it with a rolling 30-day volatility, annualised by multiplying by √252:

PeriodAverage annual volatility
2017 — calm9%
2018 — wobble12%
2019 — pre-COVID14%
2020 — COVID26% (spiking past 75%)

Across the whole sample, volatility swung from about 6% to 75% annualised. This bunching has a name — volatility clustering — and a mouthful of a technical term, heteroscedasticity ("different variance"). The key insight: unlike returns, which are nearly random from one day to the next, volatility has positive autocorrelation. When it's high it tends to stay high for a while; when it's low it lingers low.

Sizing by volatility

If volatility quadruples between regimes, a fixed position size means you're risking four times as much in the storm as in the calm. Better: fix the dollar risk and let the position size flex. Using daily risk = size × vol ÷ √252:

Regime$10,000 position risks/dayTo risk a fixed $250/day, hold…
Low vol (20%)$126$19,843
High vol (100%)$630$3,969

Same dollar of risk, very different position sizes. But this only works if we can predict tomorrow's volatility — which is the cue for ARCH and GARCH.

6. ARCH and GARCH — forecasting the volatility itself

ARCH — AutoRegressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity, the model that won Robert Engle a Nobel Prize — says today's variance depends on recent shocks:

σ²ₜ = ω + α · ε²ₜ₋₁

predicted variance = baseline ω + α × (yesterday's squared return)

Yesterday's squared return is a stand-in for yesterday's variance. GARCH (Bollerslev's extension) adds a memory of past variance too:

σ²ₜ = ω + α · ε²ₜ₋₁ + β · σ²ₜ₋₁

That extra β term lets GARCH capture long clustering with very few parameters, which is why GARCH(1,1) is the everyday workhorse. We fit one on NIFTY daily returns with the arch library and got α ≈ 0.11, β ≈ 0.86, so α + β ≈ 0.97 — close to 1, confirming that volatility shocks are highly persistent. Its one-step forecast of next-day volatility was 15.0% against a realised 14.4% — a sensible, useful number to size a position with.

The cricket-ticket analogy. Imagine pricing tickets for a home team. On a winning run, fans flock in and demand stays high for a cluster of games; on a losing run, demand stays low for a stretch. ARCH and GARCH do the same thing for markets — they read which "phase" of high or low variance we're in and forecast accordingly.

Best of all, the two families combine: ARIMA forecasts the return while GARCH forecasts the risk around it. Feed the GARCH volatility into your position sizing and you get a return signal that automatically shrinks when markets turn dangerous — a common and powerful pairing. (Further cousins — EGARCH, GJR-GARCH — exist but are out of scope.)

The one-paragraph version

This second time-series session fixes four weaknesses in a naïve ARIMA backtest. Automated order selection via the AIC information criterion (which balances under- against over-fitting) replaces hand-picking (p,d,q) — but it optimises fit, not profit, so it improved on our manual guess for only one of five assets and is no silver bullet. Transaction costs (~0.03% per trade) must be subtracted from every result; here they shaved 12.9% down to 9.3% over 55 trades, and on illiquid or high-turnover strategies the damage is far worse. Risk is not constant — volatility clusters (heteroscedasticity), swinging from ~6% to 75% annualised, so we should fix the dollar risk and size inversely to volatility. To do that we forecast volatility with ARCH (variance from past shocks) and GARCH (also from past variance), whose α+β ≈ 0.97 confirms strong persistence. Finally, pair them: ARIMA predicts the return, GARCH predicts the risk. And above all — test everything, net of costs, on your own market.
