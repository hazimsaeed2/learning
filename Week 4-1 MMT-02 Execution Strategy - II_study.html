Execution Strategy II — placing the order intelligently

Week 4-1 · MMT-02 · Market Microstructure for Trading · a plain-language walkthrough

Imagine the phone rings at ten in the morning. A client says: "Buy me thirty thousand shares of Microsoft by three o'clock, at the best price you can get." That single sentence is the whole job of an execution desk. The previous lecture taught you the kinds of orders you can send. This one answers the harder question that follows: out of all the prices and all the moments between now and 3pm, where exactly should each slice of the order go, at what price, in what size — and how do you make sure nobody on the other side is quietly picking your pocket?

We will walk through it the way the lecture does, using its own numbers so you can check every step.

1. How will you be graded? VWAP

Before deciding anything, you need to know how the client will judge you afterwards. The standard yardstick is VWAP — the volume-weighted average price. It is simply the average price the whole market paid over the day, but with bigger-volume intervals counting more:

VWAP = Σ(price × volume) ÷ Σ(volume)

If you buy below the day's VWAP, you did better than the average participant — a good fill. If you buy above it, you overpaid. In the lecture's Microsoft session, adding up price times volume across the day gives 28,639,943,075, and total volume is 30,879,500. Divide one by the other and the day's VWAP is 927.47.

QuantityHow it's builtValue

The simple rule: buy when the price is below VWAP, sell when it is above. VWAP is the green line you are trying to stay on the right side of all day.

2. Where to place the order — think in expected value

Now the real decision. You could post your buy right at the top of the book (the "touch"), where it is most likely to fill — but the discount you capture there is tiny. Or you could post it several levels down, where the discount is large but a fill is rare. Which is better? Neither extreme; you want whichever maximises expected value:

EV = probability of fill × gain if filled

The "gain" is how far below fair price you bought. Here is the lecture's order book, and the result is the lesson: the best level is the second one, not the first.

3. Discretion orders — a sure small edge beats a risky big one

A discretion order is an ordinary limit order with a second, hidden, more aggressive price it is allowed to pay only if a matching order shows up. It lets you sit passively most of the time but pounce when the chance is real. Compare two ways of buying when fair price is 100.25:

CaseExpected valueResult
No discretion — rest at 100.00, 50% chance of a fill0.50 × (100.25 − 100.00)0.125
Discretion — lift to 100.10 with certainty when an ask appears1.00 × (100.25 − 100.10)0.150

The discretion order wins, 0.150 against 0.125. A certain smaller edge is worth more than a coin-flip larger one. That is the whole appeal of discretion: it converts patience into reliable fills without chasing.

4. Blending pegging and discretion — and the one guard you must never skip

Two knobs work together. Pegging keeps your buy automatically parked at, say, best bid plus one tick — whenever the bid moves, your order follows. Discretion then lets that pegged order lift a little further to grab a fill. Blended, they keep you competitive and opportunistic.

But there is a danger. If the bid keeps ticking up and your peg keeps chasing, and discretion keeps lifting, you can cascade into paying far more than the stock is worth. So the lecture insists on one final check before every single order leaves the desk:

The fair-price guard: never buy above fair price, and never sell below it. This is the last line of the algorithm, and it is non-negotiable.

Watch it work. You peg at best bid + 10 cents and allow a 20-cent discretion range. The bid is 100.2, so your peg sits at 100.30; an ask appears at 100.50, which is inside your reach.

StepScenario A — fair price 100.55Scenario B — fair price 100.45

Same cascade, two different fair prices. When the lift stays at or below fair, you take it. When it would push you above fair, the guard stops you cold — even though the order "could" have filled. Missing a fill is cheap; overpaying systematically is not.

5. Guerrilla bots and spoofing — the reason the guard exists

Now you see why that guard matters so much. A guerrilla bot exists to bait exactly the kind of pegging logic we just built. Suppose your pegger naively sits five cents above the best bid with no fair-price guard. The manipulator feeds you tiny bids to drag you upward, then springs the trap.

StepWhat the bot doesWhere you end up
1posts a tiny bid at 100.2you chase to 100.25
2posts a tiny bid at 100.8you chase to 100.85
3sells 1,000 shares to youyou buy at 100.85
4market snaps back; bot buys to coverat 100.50

The bot pockets (100.85 − 100.50) × 1,000 = $350, and you lost the same $350 by overpaying 35 cents a share against a fair price of 100.50. Multiply that across thousands of trades and you understand the Navinder Sarao case — a trader who spoofed markets for roughly forty million dollars and ended up convicted and jailed.

The defence is the guard you already have. A pegger that refuses to bid above fair price (100.50) simply will not chase the bait. The manipulator's tiny orders move the screen but not your behaviour, and the trap earns nothing from you.

6. Can the order even finish in time? Theoretical Time Horizon

Slicing only works if the market trades enough volume for you to get filled before the deadline. The Theoretical Time Horizon (TTH) is a quick sanity check:

TTH = order shares ÷ (vol_ratio × min_vol)

Take the lecture's AXISBANK order: buy 10,000 shares with high urgency. The market trades about 500 shares a minute, and your aggressive algorithm captures 15% of that flow.

StepReasoningValue
market volumegiven500 shares/min

Just over two hours. If the client's deadline were tighter than that, you would need a more aggressive algorithm (a higher capture rate) or you would have to accept worse prices. TTH tells you that before you start, not after you've run out of time.

Putting it all together

The whole lecture is one decision loop, run for every slice of the parent order:

  1. Compute fair price and the day's VWAP — your targets.
  2. Pick the level by expected value, not by what fills fastest.
  3. Use pegging to stay competitive and discretion to convert chances into fills.
  4. Run the fair-price guard before every order — never above fair to buy, never below to sell.
  5. Stay alert to spoofing; the guard plus size/spike checks is your defence.
  6. Confirm the TTH says the order can finish inside the client's window.
If you remember only one thing: the closest price is rarely the best price, and the fair-price guard is the line you never cross. Expected value tells you where to stand; the guard keeps you from being dragged off it.

7. Resource addendum - what the worksheet adds

The official notes and in-class workbook add one more layer: the execution system is not only choosing between VWAP, EV, pegging, and discretion. It first decides whether the order is suitable for automation, then schedules the parent order against expected volume, then runs book-level guards before every child order.

Order ticket before order logic

The algo should not start from a blank screen. It needs a complete order ticket and supporting market data.

VWAP strategy is also a schedule

The resource notes describe three VWAP-algo stages: filter incoming orders that are too illiquid or too large for automation, estimate the intraday volume distribution, and work child orders intelligently around that distribution. A 30,000-share Microsoft order should therefore trade more during heavy-volume intervals and slow down during thin periods.

Practical time horizon gate

The official formula adds one more rule to the TTH section:

Practical Time Horizon = min(time remaining, TTH)

If TTH / Practical Time Horizon > 2, the notes treat the time window as insufficient for normal automated completion. That is a pre-trade escalation, not an after-the-fact excuse.

Dynamic discretion and book guards

A static discretion range can become too passive when time is running out, or too aggressive when the market is moving against the order. The resource formula makes discretion a function of base range, expected spread, price trend, and opposite-side liquidity. The practical rule is simple: widen only when the market conditions justify it, and still respect client limit and fair-price guards.

GuardPurpose
Alone flagIf your order is alone at the inside, do not keep advertising intent blindly.
Peg thresholdsMove the peg only when the target has shifted enough to justify a correction.
Significant sizeUse max(500 shares, 50% of bid size) as a queue-depth sanity check.
Stub order logicWhen only a small remainder is left, stop treating it like the full parent order.
Final controlsApply fair-price guard, client limit, audit logs, and kill-switch discipline.

FAQ clarifications that change implementation

  • IOC orders execute immediately against available liquidity and cancel the unfilled remainder.
  • Iceberg liquidity means displayed book size can understate true interest; refilling size is a clue, not something you control.
  • TBT data is the evidence behind fill probability, queue position, and order-book reconstruction.
  • Pegging loops can happen when multiple algos chase the same reference; size and threshold checks reduce the risk.
  • Spoofing is not normal cancellation. Repeated orders intended to move price without intent to trade are illegal and should be blocked by compliance controls.
