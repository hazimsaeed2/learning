Beating the Market — and Why It's So Hard

Week 9-1 · Data Analysis & Modeling in Python · DMP-03 (Part 1) · with Dr. Yves Hilpisch

The lecture is titled "Object-Oriented Programming in Python", but it opens somewhere more fundamental. Before you write a single trading class, you need to understand what you are up against. So this first half is a journey: we meet the villain (the Efficient Market Hypothesis), we pick up the mathematics we'll need (returns, risk, regression), and we run our first vectorized back-test. Only once you have felt the friction of doing this the simple way does the case for object-oriented programming — coming in Part 2 — really land.

Hilpisch works with a ten-year EUR/USD file in class. We don't have that file, so every idea below is reproduced on the shipped eod_prices.csv — the NIFTY index, 962 daily bars from 2017 to 2020. The concepts carry over unchanged.

1. The villain: the Efficient Market Hypothesis

Algorithmic trading, if we mean an alpha-generating strategy, is about one thing: beating the market. And the single financial theory with the most empirical support is the one that says you mostly can't — the Efficient Market Hypothesis. It is our benchmark, the baseline against which everything else must be judged.

Three landmarks tell the story. Fama, in the 1960s, argued prices behave approximately like a random walk, so past price changes carry no systematic forecasting power — which makes the chartist's craft, in his words, essentially worthless. Jensen, in 1978, sharpened this: a market is efficient with respect to some information set if no trading rule using only that information can earn persistent, risk-adjusted excess profit. And Timmermann and Granger, in 2004, added the crucial twist that efficiency depends on the information set S, the search technology T, and the model class M available at a given time. In other words, the EMH is a moving target: what was unbeatable in 1965, before computers and modern data, may not be unbeatable today.

The blunt corollary. If you firmly believe the EMH, you should not be doing algorithmic trading at all — you should buy a broad, low-cost index fund and be done with it. We proceed only because we treat efficiency as a moving benchmark and go hunting for exploitable structure.

2. The random-walk model, and the best you can do

The simplest model of a price is a random walk: tomorrow's price equals today's price plus some zero-mean noise.

S(t+1) = S(t) + ε,      with   E[ε | information today] = 0

Work through the mathematics in a least-squares sense and you reach a humbling conclusion: the best possible forecast of tomorrow's price, given everything you know today, is simply today's price. There is no predictable alpha to be squeezed from past prices alone. Simulating a handful of random walks drives the point home — they wander up and down with no memory you could trade on.

3. Prices versus returns — the distinction that trips everyone

Here is the single most important habit in quant finance: always know whether you are looking at prices or returns. Hilpisch admits he himself has fallen into the trap too often. Prices are trivially autocorrelated — if a price is around 18,000 today it won't be 9,000 tomorrow, so today and yesterday correlate at about 0.99. That tells you nothing about efficiency.

px.corr(px.shift(1))            # ≈ 0.99   prices, trivially correlated
r = np.log(px / px.shift(1))    # log returns
r.corr(r.shift(1))              # ≈ 0      returns — the real test

The genuine test of efficiency lives on the return series. In an efficient market, returns should show no usable linear predictability from their own past — their autocorrelation should sit inside a band of statistical noise.

The autocorrelation test in practice

We compute the autocorrelation function (ACF) of returns out to fifteen lags and draw a 95% confidence band of roughly ±1.96/√N. Bars inside the band are noise; persistent bars outside hint at structure you might exploit. To see what inefficiency would actually look like, we manufacture a synthetic AR(1) series, r(t) = ρ·r(t−1) + noise, with ρ = 0.30.

What the data said. The NIFTY return autocorrelations scattered close to zero — a couple nudged just past the band, but with no persistent sign, which is exactly the noisy, roughly-efficient picture the theory predicts. The synthetic AR(1) series, by contrast, lit up clearly at lag 1 (autocorrelation ≈ 0.30). That is the difference between a market you can't forecast and a process you can.

Run this same test on EUR/USD, Apple, gold — almost any end-of-day series — and Hilpisch promises you'll see the same thing 99.9% of the time: returns look efficient. A student rightly noted that big shocks, regime changes and heavy tails can break a single correlation number; true, but this deliberately simple model is a thermometer, not a diagnosis. It tells you whether there might be a fever worth investigating.

4. The mathematics of returns and risk

Two flavours of return, and they combine differently over time. Simple returns compound multiplicatively — you take the cumulative product of (1 + R). Log returns simply add up — a cumulative sum. Over a single day the two are nearly identical; over long horizons, or in high-frequency settings with a million compoundings a day, log returns win because they are both analytically convenient and numerically stable.

For risk, end-of-day data is annualized with 252 trading days: multiply mean return by 252, and volatility by the square root of 252. The Sharpe ratio is annualized mean over annualized volatility. On our NIFTY window that gave roughly 12% annual return, 19% annual volatility, a Sharpe of about 0.63, and a maximum drawdown near −38%.

The curse of asymmetry

Now the idea Hilpisch most wants you to remember. Volatility is an average; it quietly hides what happened in between. Drawdowns don't. And recovering from a drawdown requires a larger percentage gain than the loss itself — the curse of asymmetry.

Drawdown5%10%20%50%90%
Gain to break even+5.3%+11.1%+25%+100%+900%

A 20% loss needs a 25% gain to recover — only five points more, no big deal. But the relationship is not linear: a 50% loss needs a full 100% gain, and a 90% loss needs 900%. It is the large drawdowns, not the day-to-day wobble, that quietly kill a strategy.

A subtle trap. "But I'll add stop-losses to avoid drawdowns." If a drawdown still appears in your back-test, your execution controls have not removed it — they can only stop you trading. Sitting inside a year-long drawdown in real time feels nothing like seeing the tidy end result of a back-test where, on average, everything turned out fine.

5. The first back-test: a vectorized OLS strategy

Now we try to beat the market. The vectorized style expresses the whole strategy as deterministic transformations on entire arrays — no for loop walking bar by bar. It is compact and fast, which makes sweeping a thousand parameter settings easy. Our example: build seven lagged return features, fit an ordinary-least-squares model, forecast the next return, and go long or short on the sign of that forecast.

for k in range(1, 8):
    df[f"l{k}"] = df["r"].shift(k)          # seven lag features
X = np.column_stack([np.ones(n)] + [df[f"l{k}"] for k in 1..7])  # constant + 7 lags
beta = np.linalg.lstsq(X, df["r"])[0]       # OLS: alpha + a beta vector
df["pred"] = X @ beta                       # forecast the return
df["pos"]  = np.sign(df["pred"]).shift(1)   # long/short — shifted!
df["strat"] = df["pos"] * df["r"]           # strategy = position × return
The cardinal sin: look-ahead bias. The position you take on day t may use only information available up to day t−1. That is why we .shift(1) the position. Forget it, and you let the strategy peek at the very return it is trying to predict — and your back-test will look spectacular for entirely fake reasons.
The honest verdict. Here is the striking part. This is an in-sample fit with no transaction costs — the most flattering possible setup. And the OLS sign rule still lost money: about −47%, against a buy-and-hold gain of about +54% over the same window (572 long days, 381 short). The EMH warned us returns were barely predictable; the autocorrelation test agreed; and the back-test delivered the verdict. An arbitrary signal, even fitted to the data, has no real edge.

6. The strategy quality checklist

Which is exactly why you need a checklist — a pilot's pre-flight before any capital is allocated. "Does it make money?" is far too low-dimensional a question.

GateThe question to ask
Statistical edgeDo returns or forecasts genuinely differ from noise?
Economic edgeIs it still profitable after financing and transaction costs?
Persistence / robustnessDoes it survive out-of-sample, not just one small set?
ImplementabilityAre there liquid, tradable instruments? (An index can't be traded directly.)
Benchmark & capital costDoes it beat the benchmark and your cost of capital?
Risk profileAre drawdowns, volatility and tail behaviour acceptable?

Hilpisch's favourite retail playground is foreign exchange: the major FX pairs are highly liquid with tight spreads, you can go long or short symmetrically (unlike many stocks for retail traders), the data and REST APIs are clean, and rates often mean-revert around macro and policy regimes. That is why EUR/USD is his running example.

Where this is heading. The vectorized back-test we just built is intuitive and fast, but it is unrealistic — it operates on the whole array at once, far from how a market actually delivers prices one tick at a time. Part 2 introduces object-oriented programming and an event-based back-testing framework that moves us closer to market reality. The friction you felt here — copy-pasted cells, the constant danger of look-ahead bias — is precisely the problem OOP is about to solve.

The one-paragraph version

The Efficient Market Hypothesis is the benchmark every algorithmic strategy must beat, and it is a moving target that depends on information, technology and models. A random walk implies the best forecast of tomorrow's price is today's price. The real test of efficiency is on returns, not prices: their autocorrelation should sit inside a noise band, as NIFTY's roughly did, while a synthetic AR(1) with ρ=0.30 shows what exploitable structure looks like. Simple returns compound (cumprod), log returns add (cumsum); risk is annualized with √252, and the curse of asymmetry means a 50% loss needs a 100% gain to recover. A vectorized OLS back-test — lag features, fit, sign, and a shift to avoid look-ahead — lost money even in-sample with no costs, a textbook EMH lesson. Judge every strategy against the quality checklist before risking a cent — and let that friction motivate the move to OOP and event-based back-testing.
