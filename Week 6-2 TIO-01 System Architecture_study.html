System Architecture for Automated Trading Systems

Week 6-2 · TIO-01, the opening lecture of the Trading Tech, Infra & Operations module — a guided tour of how a modern trading system is built, told as a story of evolution.

This lecture answers a deceptively simple question: when you press "go" on an algorithm, what actually happens between your idea and a trade printing on the exchange? The instructor builds the answer the way the industry built it — starting from the slow, manual, human-driven system of the past, then bolting on each innovation until you arrive at today's fully automated architecture. Understanding every block, and why it exists, is what lets a quant tune a system to a particular strategy. There is no single optimal design; there is only the design that fits your edge.

1. What every trading system must do

Strip away the jargon and any trading system, manual or automated, needs five capabilities:

  • a way to read market data from the exchange;
  • a storehouse of historical data for analysis;
  • a tool to analyse that history;
  • a place for the trader to input decisions;
  • a way to route orders to the exchange.

In the traditional system, all five revolved around a human at a screen. Diagrammatically the world splits into three zones, separated by two dotted lines: the exchange (the external world you don't build), the broker/server (everything you implement), and the trader (the client application). Keep that three-zone picture in mind — every block we add lands in one of them.

2. The traditional system, block by block

Market data arrives from the exchange in a format unique to that exchange — the first N bytes are an instrument ID, the next the best bid, the next the best ask, and so on. The first program you need is a market-data adapter that knows how to decipher that specific layout. Connect to a second exchange and you need a second adapter, because each exchange speaks its own language.

Once deciphered, data can be poured into a data warehouse for posterity (or you can buy clean history from a vendor like Bloomberg or Reuters). But you rarely care about the whole firehose, so you carve out an operational data store — a small local subset of just the instruments your strategy watches. The trader's application then displays live data, lets the trader compare it against historical patterns in that store, and generates orders. Those orders flow to the order manager, which performs risk checks and routes them to the exchange over a two-way TCP/IP link — because the exchange talks back, acknowledging, filling, or rejecting each order.

Why buy data if the exchange gives it free? Live data only accumulates from the day you start recording, and recording requires paid lease-lines and a co-located server. Vendors sell deep history across many exchanges in one consistent format, plus fundamental, news and economic data — so you can back-test a strategy on the 2020 COVID crash without having been connected back then.

3. Why the world went algorithmic

The traditional system has one fatal bottleneck: the human. People take vacations, lunch and toilet breaks, bring HR complexity, and — crucially — are slow. The instructor's favourite illustration is a conference game: thousands of people raced to tap a tablet the instant it changed from blue to red, and the fastest reaction across three days was about 0.15 seconds. That is roughly the theoretical floor for an alert human.

Beyond speed, an automated system can monitor tens of thousands of instruments in parallel for complex patterns (a human manages maybe 50, and only simple ones), and — if tested properly — can fire millions of logically sound orders a day without typos. The case for automation is overwhelming.

4. Hardware versus software: where the logic lives

When a packet hits your server it arrives at the LAN card (the network card handling the physical layer). From here there are two routes. In a software system, the data travels via PCI into the CPU, where your C++/Python logic runs. The CPU is general-purpose — it will execute anything you write — but the journey up through the operating system and application layers costs time, landing you in the single-digit-microsecond range.

In a hardware-only system, you burn the logic directly into the LAN card's FPGA (field- programmable gate array). The card itself deciphers the packet, checks your trigger, builds the order and sends it — never troubling the CPU — in tens of nanoseconds. The catch: hardware is rigid. An FPGA does exactly the one task you wired it for and nothing else (an ASIC card can't even be rewired; an FPGA at least can be re-burned).

The trade-off in one line: simple logic that is extremely latency-sensitive goes in hardware; complex or frequently-changing logic stays in flexible software. HFT firms push the market-data adapter, the RMS checks, even the entire order manager into FPGA. A classic hardware strategy is a pre-configured lookup table: "when crude month-1 future ticks up, copy the price, add an offset, and fire a buy in month-2" — assembled entirely on the card.

5. The automated architecture: new blocks

Automating the trader's job means moving "watch → decide → execute" into one engine: the Complex Event Processing (CEP) block. Market data feeds straight into the CEP, which talks two-way with the order manager (it needs feedback on fills, positions and P&L to set its parameters). Heavy maths that isn't time-critical — option Greeks, correlations — runs in a separate calculation block (often on a GPU), parallel to and outside the hot path, feeding the CEP when asked.

So what is left for the human? The trader's app becomes a control panel: it sets strategy parameters, displays orders/trades/positions/P&L for confidence, and runs a within-application risk check.

Risk management lives in two places

The app-level RMS is a fat-finger guard. Suppose a strategy buys a straddle when implied volatility crosses 30.01%, but the trader types 3.01% — since IV is almost always above that, everything fires at once. A logical range per parameter catches the typo:

IV trigger typedLogical rangeRMS verdict
30.01 (intended)10 – 80ACCEPT
3.01 (typo)10 – 80REJECT

But the main risk check belongs in the order manager, implemented once and properly, because the market-data adapter and order manager are standard adapters shared by every strategy, whereas each CEP is strategy-specific. The OMS-level RMS checks order value, distance from the market, total size, net accumulated position, outstanding orders, P&L per instrument, traded value per second, and so on. An admin monitor dashboard sits across all order managers, showing server health, exchange statistics and per-server profitability — peace of mind when everything runs automatically.

6. Connecting many exchanges, and the FIX protocol

Prices are interconnected: the same stock lists on several exchanges (State Bank of India on both NSE and BSE), and cross-asset links abound (a rise in rubber prices pressures a tyre-maker's shares; gold moves jewellery stocks). Traders therefore want feeds from many exchanges into one CEP. But every new exchange means re-writing adapter and order-manager logic for its unique language — friction the exchanges themselves want to remove. Hence standardized protocols like FIX.

FIX is a series of tag=value pairs. A defensive AAPL buy looks like this:

TagFieldValue
1AccountQ001
48SecurityIDAAPL
44Price265.01
54Side1 (buy)
38OrderQty100
35MsgTypeD = new (F = cancel, G = replace)

Any FIX-speaking exchange understands this. To make incoming feeds consistent, a data normalizer transforms each exchange's market data into one standard structure — for instance de-cumulating Exchange B's cumulative depth [700, 1900] back into per-level quantities [700, 1200] so it matches Exchange A's best-level feed.

Why HFT shuns FIX. FIX is text, so it must be parsed; and it is bloaty — the price "265.01" eats 6 bytes versus a 4-byte binary float. Worse, popular exchanges that support FIX run it through a FIX-to-native converter, adding a latency hop. Native binary is a clean, direct conversation, so latency-sensitive firms use native protocols and co-location and avoid FIX entirely.

7. The dispersion example — and why you store events

The instructor's richest detour answers "why bother storing computed event history rather than just raw market data?" The motivating case is dispersion trading. Index volatility is not the average of its components' volatilities — it depends on their correlation. If two 50/50 components are perfectly anti-correlated, the index is flat; if perfectly correlated, the index is as volatile as the parts. Formally, assuming a single constant pairwise correlation ρ:

ρ (constant pairwise correlation)Index volatility
+1.0020% (components in sync)
+0.28 (implied by option prices)16%
0.0014.1%
−1.000% (flat index)

You back out each instrument's implied volatility from its option prices (aggregating across strikes with a variance-swap-style 1/K² weighting), do the same for the index, then solve for the constant ρ that reconciles them — the market's implied correlation. If that implied correlation is low and you expect it to rise, index vol will rise relative to stock vol, so you buy index options and sell stock options; if you expect correlation to fall, you do the reverse. Notably, correlation spikes in panics (everything falls together) and eases in calm markets (stock-pickers diverge).

The point about storage: computing implied correlation each time means re-deriving every option's implied vol, re-weighting by 1/K², and re-matching historical expiry dates — an enormous recomputation. If you calculated it live, store it. Hence the architecture grows an event store, and storage requirements balloon.

8. Quantified news and external data

Inputs needn't be exchanges. Economic releases (CPI, non-farm payrolls, PMI, crude inventories) are now published in machine-readable format — journalists are sealed in an electronic room and the data is released to subscribers at a precise instant. Corporate earnings arrive the same way. And because news is the first-order driver of a stock's price, vendors (Thomson Reuters, RavenPack/Dow Jones, Bloomberg) attach scores to every article so machines can react. The four the instructor named:

ScoreWhat it captures
Sentimentpositive / neutral / negative tone toward each company
Relevancehow central the company is to the article (header? every paragraph? in passing?)
Noveltyis this new, or a repeat of an event already reported? (avoid acting twice)
Market impactthe category and likely price-impact of the news type

Novelty matters because one event is reported in waves — an alert, an update, a full article, then echoes across wires — and a naive system would trade the same event repeatedly. There are two uses for quantified news: reacting faster than a human reader, and aggregating thousands of articles into a broad sentiment view (e.g. how much "innovation" chatter tracks the NASDAQ).

9. Testing before you go live: the simulator

A new or modified strategy is never sent straight to the exchange — a bug could be ruinous, and the quant who designed it and the developer who coded it may have misunderstood each other (one meant buy, the other coded sell). Instead, orders go to an exchange simulator that mimics how the exchange would respond. And since you might finish coding at midnight when markets are shut, you need the ability to replay stored historical data — flat days, volatile days, gap-ups, gap-downs — to see how the strategy performs across regimes before deciding to go live. This is algorithmic paper trading.

Why simulators are hard (and proprietary). Predicting the fill for an order resting between bid and ask is genuinely uncertain — there may be hidden iceberg orders, the queue position matters, and exchange response time itself stretches under load, so by the time your order is processed the price may have moved away. The instructor notes a head of simulator design became head of trading, precisely because building a good simulator demands the deepest market-microstructure knowledge. Often the gap between a great firm and a mediocre one is the sophistication of its simulator.

Key takeaways

  • A trading system spans three zones — exchange (external), broker/server (you build it), trader (client) — chained as adapter → warehouse/operational store → CEP → order manager + RMS → exchange, with a 7-year back-office record.
  • Automation closes a roughly 4-million-to-one reaction-time gap (human 150 ms vs FPGA 35 ns) and scales from ~50 to tens of thousands of instruments.
  • Hardware (FPGA on the NIC) is fast but rigid; software (PCI→CPU) is flexible but slower — choose per block.
  • Each exchange speaks its own binary language; the adapter deciphers and the normalizer unifies; FIX standardizes but is text-based and bloaty, so HFT prefers native + co-location.
  • RMS sits in the app (fat-finger typo guard) and — more importantly — once before orders leave the order manager.
  • Dispersion: index variance = component variances + pairwise correlation; solving for the implied correlation tells you whether to buy or sell index vol vs stock vol — and the heavy recomputation is why you keep an event store.
  • Strategies are validated on a proprietary exchange simulator with replayed data before going live.
