WEEK 17-2 · OTS-03 · STUDY GUIDE

Options Pricing Models & the Greeks

A plain-English tour of Varun Kumar's third options session — what a premium is really made of, why combinations let you express any view, how Black-Scholes prices an option, and how the Greeks turn that one number into a dashboard of risk.

If the previous sessions were about what options are, this one is about price — the single most important thing in options literature. By the end you should be able to look at a premium and know what is driving it, build a position that expresses a nuanced market view, and read the Greeks the way a trader reads a cockpit.

1. What a premium is made of

Every option premium splits into two parts. Take an underlying at S = 100 and a 90-strike call quoted at $14. Exercise it right now and you pocket 100 − 90 = $10; that floor is the intrinsic value. The remaining $4 is the extrinsic — or time — value: the amount you pay for the optionality of having until expiry for things to go your way. Intrinsic value is rigid (it only moves when the price moves); the time value is the dynamic part, and it is exactly what the Greek theta erodes day by day.

The same idea gives us moneyness. A call is in the money when S > X, at the money when S = X, and out of the money when S < X (a put is the mirror image). This vocabulary lets you describe a contract's position the way "bullish" or "consolidating" describes a market.

Two structural facts. American options can be exercised any day, so they carry more optionality and cost more than European options (exercisable only at expiry) — and the labels have nothing to do with geography. Most listed and index options you will trade are European. Beyond these live exotics (Asian options settle on an average price, etc.), built case-by-case for institutions — interesting to know, rarely traded by retail.

2. Payoff is not P&L — and combinations

A payoff diagram ignores what you paid; a P&L diagram subtracts the premium, so it starts in the red until the move covers your cost. Keeping the two apart prevents a lot of confusion (a straddle's payoff looks like "win either way", but its P&L only wins after recovering both premiums).

The real power of options is that you can combine calls and puts to express almost any view — something equities simply cannot do.

The protective put

Own the stock at 90 and worried about a fall? Buy a 90-put for $10. Below 90 the put gains exactly what the stock loses, so your worst case is the $10 premium, while the upside stays fully open. Hedging caps losses; it can never erase them, because you always pay for the insurance.

The bull call spread — and a cautionary tale

Bullish, but you doubt the stock will run past 120? Buy a 90-call and sell a 120-call. The short call's premium subsidises the long call, cutting your cost; in return your profit is capped at 120 − 90 = $30. Even if the stock rockets to 200 you still only make 30 — a calculated trade-off.

The 1×2 trap. Tempted by the premium, you sell two 120-calls instead of one. You collect more cash up front — but the extra naked short means that once the stock pushes well past 120 you start losing money. You got the direction perfectly right and still lost. The lesson: combinations are powerful, but never over-collect premium without a clear objective; over-using them flips your payoff away from your thesis.

The same toolkit gives you the risk reversal (buy a put, sell a call — bearish), and, when you have no directional view but expect a big move, the straddle (ATM call + put) and the cheaper strangle (OTM call + put).

Put-call parity

A frequent misconception is that if a call's premium rises, the put's must fall. Not so — calls and puts are bound together by put-call parity:

C − P = S − X·e^(−rT)

The cleanest proof builds a portfolio of long put + short call + long stock. At expiry it is worth exactly the strike X whether the market finishes up or down. With S=100, X=105, r=0, σ=30%, T=30 days the shipped model gives a call of 1.566 and a put of 6.566, so C − P = −5, which equals S − X = −5. Parity holds. (It relies on a fixed holding period T, so it applies to European options, not American.) Among other things, parity lets you derive the strike at which a call and put cost the same, and lets you trade a put synthetically with a call.

4. Black-Scholes — turning inputs into a price

The exchange needs a way to quote a premium in the first place — that is what a pricing model does. The standard closed form for a call is:

C = S·N(d₁) − X·e^(−rT)·N(d₂)

where d₁ = [ln(S/X) + (r + σ²/2)T] / (σ√T) and d₂ = d₁ − σ√T. It looks intimidating but is just the familiar S − X structure, each term weighted by a cumulative-normal probability:

  • N(d₁) is the delta — the hedge ratio, how many shares to hold per option.
  • N(d₂) is the risk-neutral probability the option finishes in the money.

Feed in six inputs — spot, strike, rate, dividends, time and volatility. Notice what is absent: past returns or drift. Options look forward to expiry, and BSM prices in a risk-neutral world, so historical drift is not an input. (Some richer models do add drift; capturing everything would push you toward machine learning.)

Assumptions — and the big flaw. BSM assumes continuous trading, lognormal prices, a constant risk-free rate, European exercise, no dividends, no transaction costs — and, most controversially, constant volatility. Volatility is anything but constant, which is why stochastic-volatility models (e.g. Heston) exist; but without some assumption you never get a clean closed form. For a retail trader or an exchange, BSM is good enough. Note one hard limit: BSM prices European options only — for American options (which can be exercised early) you switch to numerical methods such as binomial trees.

A quick sensitivity check (ATM, 3-month, base premium 4.61): raising the spot, the volatility, or the time-to-expiry all lift the call (to 7.92, 6.57, 6.88 respectively); a higher rate nudges it up slightly to 5.01. The put follows from parity.

5. The Greeks — your risk dashboard

Why bother computing a premium the market already quotes? Because the same equation, differentiated, yields the Greeks — the partial derivatives that tell you how your premium responds to each input. They are the foundation of risk management, delta hedging, volatility trading and strategy design in the lectures to come.

Delta is an S-shaped curve in the spot; a "90-delta call" simply means a delta of 0.9 — a $1 rise lifts the premium about $0.90. Because of parity, delta_call − delta_put = 1 always. Gamma measures how unstable your delta is, and it peaks at the money and for the nearest expiry — so if you want a steady delta, trade deep ITM/OTM or longer-dated; if you want a lively delta, trade ATM near expiry. Theta (decay) is also fiercest at the money. Vega peaks at the money too, but grows with time — a one-year option is far more vol-sensitive (0.375) than a one-week one (0.055), which matters enormously for volatility trades around events like a budget.

Additivity & trader jargon. Greeks sum across a book. A position of "long 5000 delta" makes $5000 per $1 up-move; "short 500 theta" bleeds $500 a day; "long 1000 vega" gains $1000 per +1% vol. Our sample book (long 10 ATM calls, short 5 110-calls, ×100) nets +460 delta, −19 theta and +123 vega. Beyond the standard five sit second-order Greeks — Vanna (∂delta/∂σ), Veta (∂vega/∂t) — and you can define any derivative you need.

6. The takeaways

  • Premium = intrinsic + time value; theta eats the time value.
  • Payoff ignores cost; P&L doesn't. Combinations express nuanced views — but don't over-sell premium.
  • Put-call parity (C − P = S − X·e^(−rT)) ties calls and puts together; European only.
  • Black-Scholes prices with six inputs and no drift; its weakest assumption is constant volatility.
  • The Greeks are partial derivatives — Delta, Gamma, Theta, Vega, Rho — and Gamma/Theta/Vega all peak at the money.
  • Greeks are additive across a portfolio; they are the language of every risk and volatility lecture that follows.
