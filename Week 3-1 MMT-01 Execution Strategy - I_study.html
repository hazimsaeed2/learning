Execution Strategy I — How an Order Becomes a Trade

Week 3-1 · MMT-01 · Market Microstructure for Trading — a plain-English study guide

Before you can build a strategy that makes money, you have to understand the plumbing that spends it: the moment you decide to buy or sell, your order travels through brokers and exchanges, lands in an order book, and gets filled at a price you only partly control. This lecture is about that plumbing — orders, order books, and the first ideas of execution: getting your trade done at a good price without moving the market against yourself.

1. Why trade with a computer at all?

A human trader is slow, emotional, and can only watch a handful of things at once. An algorithm fixes all three problems, and that is the whole reason this field exists:

  • Speed. A program reacts to an opportunity in microseconds; you react in seconds. In fast markets, that gap is the difference between a good fill and a bad one.
  • No emotion, more discipline. The computer follows the rules exactly — it does not panic, hesitate, or "hope". It removes the fear and greed that wreck human execution.
  • Breadth. One program can watch many stocks, in many markets, at the same time, and place orders across all of them simultaneously.

Algorithmic trading is simply: a computer program following a defined set of rules to decide the time, price, and quantity of every order.

2. The cast of characters (the market ecosystem)

Your order does not jump straight onto the exchange. It passes through a chain:

  • Traders run strategies. Each strategy spits out buy/sell orders (also called bid and ask orders), which can be new, modified, or cancelled.
  • Brokers receive your orders and route them to the exchange. They sit between you and the market.
  • Clearing houses guarantee the trade settles (the buyer pays, the seller delivers).
  • Exchanges match buyers with sellers and publish the order book.
Why the broker matters — "broker alpha"

Imagine a hedge fund trading $200 million every week. A broker whose technology saves just one basis point per trade — one cent on every $100 — hands the fund free money. That is broker alpha: the same strategy, a faster pipe, more profit. Walk the arithmetic:

3. Order routing — the microsecond journey

Inside the broker, two pieces do the work. The order router decides which exchange or venue gets the order (some stocks trade in several places at once). The market adapter translates your order into the exact language that venue speaks. Every step adds latency — delay measured in microseconds — and in fast markets, latency costs money. Minimising it is a competitive sport.

4. The order book — the heart of the market

An order book is just a list of every resting limit order for a stock, sorted by price. Two simple rules govern it:

  • The highest bid sits at the top of the buy side — the best price a buyer offers.
  • The lowest ask (or offer) sits at the top of the sell side — the best price a seller accepts.

The gap between them is the bid–ask spread, and it is the price of impatience: if you want to trade right now, you pay it. Here is a small book (we will reuse these exact numbers throughout):

Bid sizeBid priceAsk priceAsk size
60049.90 ← best bid50.10 ← best ask100

Best bid 49.90, best ask 50.10, so the spread is 50.10 − 49.90 = 0.20. The mid-price is 50.00, and the spread is 0.20 / 50.00 = 40 basis points. Notice the depth (queue sizes) is thinnest right at the inside and grows as you move away — orders near the touch fill quickly and leave.

Price–time priority

When two orders compete, the exchange decides who trades first with a single rule: best price wins; if prices tie, the earliest order wins. So a more aggressive price jumps the queue, and among equal prices, whoever got there first is filled first. This is why being fast (and well-placed) matters.

5. The two fundamental orders — and the trade-off you can never escape

Every order type is built from two primitives, and they sit at opposite ends of a see-saw:

Market order — certain fill, uncertain price

A market order says "fill me now, whatever the price". It is fast and reliable, but you pay the spread and, for bigger sizes, you walk the book: you take the best price, then the next, then the next, until you are filled. Watch a buy 400 eat the ask side above:

You wanted 50.10 but paid 50.1375 on average — a slippage of 0.0375 per share, or $15 on 400 shares. The bigger your order relative to the book, the worse this gets. That is market impact, and avoiding it is the entire point of execution strategy.

Limit order — certain price, uncertain fill

A limit order says "trade me only at this price or better". A buy-limit at 49.90 will never pay more than 49.90 — but it might never fill if the market walks away. While it waits, it sits in the book and provides liquidity; if a market sell hits it, you actually earn the spread instead of paying it.

The one trade-off to remember

Market order = control over time, none over price. Limit order = control over price, none over time. Every other order type is a clever attempt to get a little of both.

6. The rest of the order zoo

  • Stop (stop-loss) order. Dormant until the price hits a stop price, then it becomes a market order. Used to cap losses or confirm a breakout. Caution: stops cluster, so when they trigger they demand liquidity when it is least available and add momentum.
  • Iceberg order. Shows only a small "tip" in the book and hides the rest, so the market cannot see your true size.
  • Fill-or-Kill (FOK). Fill the entire quantity at the price right now, or cancel the whole thing.
  • Immediate-or-Cancel (IOC). Like FOK, but it accepts a partial fill and cancels the rest.
  • Pegged order. Automatically tracks a benchmark — a pegged buy follows the best bid up and down, staying competitive without you babysitting it.
  • Post-only order. Designed to add liquidity (and earn the exchange's rebate); it refuses to cross the spread.
  • Discretion order. A limit order with a hidden second, more aggressive price it is willing to pay when conditions are good — patient most of the time, opportunistic when the spread narrows.

The two ideas behind all the fancy ones are hidden volume (don't show your hand) and how the order behaves inside the spread (when it becomes visible or active).

7. Liquidity, makers, takers and rebates

Liquidity is how easily you can trade without moving the price — lots of orders flowing in a tight spread. Two roles create it and consume it:

  • Liquidity makers post limit orders and wait; they earn the spread (and often a rebate) but risk the market running away.
  • Liquidity takers send market orders; they get certainty but pay the spread.

A market maker is a professional liquidity provider: quote both sides, earn the spread, and manage inventory by reading order flow to guess which way the market leans.

8. From orders to an execution strategy

Now the real problem: a fund needs to buy (say) a huge block of shares within a deadline, without screaming its intentions and without paying massive impact. One market order would be a disaster; one limit order might never fill. The answer is to slice the order over time and blend passive and aggressive behaviour.

VWAP — the benchmark that scores your execution

The Volume-Weighted Average Price is the desk's report card. It weights each trade's price by its volume, so it reflects where the bulk of the day actually traded. If you buy below VWAP, you did well; if you buy above it, you overpaid. Here is the exact four-trade example from the lecture's spreadsheet:

Is there enough time? The time-horizon rule

Before accepting a big order, the desk checks whether it can realistically be finished in the time available. The lecture's rule:

StepValue

If the theoretical time needed is more than twice the practical time available, there simply isn't enough liquidity to finish cleanly, and the desk hands the order back rather than wreck the price.

Blending passive and aggressive: pegging + discretion

A good execution algorithm runs two agents at once. The passive agent keeps a pegged limit order at the bid, fishing for market orders to hit it at a good price. The aggressive agent watches the spread and, when it is narrow (cheap to cross), uses a discretion price to grab liquidity and keep the order on schedule. If the passive side is filling nicely, the aggressive side stays quiet; if the clock is running out, the aggressive side steps in. The discretion range is the dial that sets how aggressive the whole thing is. The trade-off: too aggressive and you give yourself away (and add momentum that moves the price against you); too passive and you miss the deadline.

9. What really moves the price: order-flow imbalance

It is tempting to think "big volume moves prices". The in-class reading (Cont, Kukanov & Stoikov, 2011) shows the better explanation is order-flow imbalance (OFI) — the net change in the bid and ask queues. When buyers add to the bid faster than sellers add to the ask, the mid-price drifts up, and the relationship is roughly linear, explaining about 65% of short-term price moves. The slope (how much a unit of imbalance moves the price) is inversely proportional to depth: thin books move more per order.

10. The vocabulary you now own

  • Market impact — how much your own buying/selling pushes the price against you.
  • Slippage — the gap between the price you expected and the price you got (worse in volatile markets and with market orders).
  • Tick - the minimum allowed price increment for an instrument.
  • Level 2 / Level 3 data - broker views of multiple bid/ask levels; full event data is richer because it includes order events and timestamps.
  • Marketable price - the current ask for a buy order, or current bid for a sell order.
  • Depth ahead - quantity at more aggressive prices that must trade before your resting order can fill.
  • Alone at inside - your order is the only same-side quantity at the best bid or best ask.
  • Alpha — risk-adjusted return above a benchmark; beta — how much an asset moves with the market, estimated as Cov(asset, market) ÷ Var(market).
  • OHLC — the open/high/low/close summary of a bar; the basis of moving averages and Bollinger bands.
The big picture

A strategy's signal tells you what to trade. Execution decides how well you actually trade it. The order book is where these meet — and every basis point you save in execution drops straight to the bottom line.

11. Workbook mechanics that complete the lecture

The Excel workbook in the official exercise folder adds several practical order mechanics that were only touched briefly in the transcript. They matter because execution code has to decide what to do at the exact moment the book changes.

Marketable limit orders versus market orders

A marketable limit order behaves like a market order, but with a safety rail. If the best ask is 100.50, a buy-limit at 100.75 can trade immediately, but it refuses to pay above 100.75. A plain market buy has no such ceiling.

Price-time priority and the queue

If 3 shares are already bid at 99.50 and you add 10 more at the same price, your 10 shares sit behind the first 3. When an 8-share sell order reaches that price, the first 3 fill before you receive the next 5. The rule is not "largest order wins"; it is best price first, then earliest timestamp.

Stop-loss, take-profit, and bracket orders

If you bought at 100 and want to take profit at 105, a sell-limit at 105 can safely rest in the book. But if your stop loss is 95, a normal sell-limit at 95 would execute immediately because it is below the current bid. That is why a stop order waits until the market reaches the stop trigger. A bracket order, mentioned in the FAQ, combines the entry, stop-loss, and profit-taking order so only one exit remains active.

FAQ edge cases

If there is no opposite-side liquidity, the exchange does not take the other side for you. Depending on the order type, your order rests, partially fills, or cancels. True tick-by-tick data includes new orders, modifications, cancellations, executions, prices, quantities, and timestamps; a retail "top five levels" view is only a small window into the book.

12. Where the sources add alternatives or nuance

The transcript teaches the trading intuition: read the book, know which order type you are using, and control the price-time trade-off. The official resources add three important extensions.

Method taught in the lecture: most examples assume the usual price-time priority exchange. This is the right default for the workbook's main order-book exercises.

Alternative method found in the official resources: the pro-rata sheet shows a different matching convention, where eligible fills are allocated in proportion to resting size. Both are valid market designs. Use price-time when the venue's rulebook says earliest queue position matters; use pro-rata only when the contract or venue explicitly uses proportional allocation.

Method taught in the lecture: volume and VWAP are used as the natural way to score execution.

Alternative method found in the order-book impact paper: short-horizon price changes are better explained by order-flow imbalance (OFI), which combines market orders, limit orders, and cancellations at the best quotes. The paper reports that OFI explained price changes better than trade imbalance or raw traded volume. In practice, VWAP is still a benchmark; OFI is a diagnostic for why the book is moving.

Method taught in the lecture: simple pegging plus discretion can blend passive and aggressive execution.

More detailed resource workflow: later slides add operational controls: client price limits, reserve size, "stub" logic for small leftovers, "alone at inside" checks, significant-size-ahead checks, and a dynamic discretion range based on expected spread, trend, and opposite-side liquidity. Those controls keep the algorithm from blindly chasing the market.

Modern best practice

Treat execution logic as a risk system, not just an order sender. Before live use, test it with delayed fills, partial fills, stale quotes, exchange rejects, missing depth, and sudden spread widening. A backtest that assumes every limit order fills at the displayed price is usually too optimistic.
