From a Notebook to a Trading System

Week 9-2 · Data Analysis & Modeling in Python · DMP-04 (Part 2) · with Dr. Yves Hilpisch

In Part 1 we met the villain — the Efficient Market Hypothesis — and built a fast, intuitive vectorized back-test. It worked on whole arrays at once, with no loop, and it was perfect for sweeping a thousand parameter settings. But it was also unrealistic: real markets don't hand you the entire price history in one block; they deliver one event at a time. This second session refactors everything into object-oriented, event-driven code — the architecture that real trading systems actually use, where the very same code runs the back-test and the live strategy.

1. A reality check before we begin

Hilpisch opens with a splash of cold water, because the questions from the first session revealed a hunger for shortcuts. UK regulators force brokers to publish their loss rates, and the numbers are brutal: roughly 70 to 77 percent of retail accounts lose money — 76.6% at CMC, 71% at IG, 69% at FXCM — year after year, worse than a coin flip. And it isn't just amateurs: over ten- and twenty-year horizons, even hedge funds, with hundreds of millions in technology and armies of PhDs, generally fail to beat a simple SPY buy-and-hold. He calls it the curse of big operations — the costs, compliance and overhead they must earn back first.

The blunt message. There is no way around hard work. Finance, mathematics and programming are reinforcing layers you must learn together — you cannot skip the theory and copy a strategy off YouTube. The retail edge is not more resources; it is low costs and small niches the giants can't be bothered with.

2. Why object-oriented, and why event-driven

Object-oriented programming lets us encapsulate a concept — a price bar, a position, a whole strategy — into a self-contained object we can reuse and recombine. Hilpisch's favourite image is Lego bricks: instead of a single fixed toy, you get blocks that snap together however you need. Our back-test from Part 1 was a notebook of sequential cells; now we refactor it into classes.

And why event-driven rather than vectorized? Because the vectorized engine treats each bar independently. The moment you care about realistic details — order queues, partial fills, latency, path-dependent logic, several assets at once — you need to model a stream of events, updating the world one event at a time. Crucially, this is exactly how live systems work, so the same engine can run both your back-test and your production strategy.

The professional's insistence. Senior people at hedge funds will tell you their back-testing engine and their execution engine are the same code — not merely similar. The strategy you tested is, byte for byte, the strategy that trades. Event-driven design is what makes that possible, and it removes the friction (and bugs) of porting research to production.

3. The vocabulary: four kinds of event

Everything in the engine is an event, and every event has just two defining features: a timestamp ("when") and a description of what happened ("what"). Hilpisch's analogy: "I was born" is the description; the date is the timestamp; together they make an event. We define four, as Python dataclasses, and they always flow in the same order.

MarketEvent  — a new price bar arrived          (price)
SignalEvent  — the strategy decided long/short  (direction +1/-1/0)
OrderEvent   — the portfolio wants to trade      (quantity)
FillEvent    — execution confirms the trade done (quantity, price)

Market → Signal → Order → Fill

4. The four Lego bricks

Four classes do the work, and each one knows how to react to exactly one kind of event.

BrickWhat it does
DataHandlerStreams prices, emitting a MarketEvent per bar. (This is the brick we'll later swap for a live feed.)
StrategyConsumes MarketEvents and emits a SignalEvent when its trading condition is met.
PortfolioTurns signals into OrderEvents, and tracks position, cash and equity (marking to market on every bar).
ExecutionHandlerTurns OrderEvents into FillEvents. In a teaching engine we assume every order fills at the last price.

Because the bricks only talk through events, you can replace any one of them without touching the others — a different strategy, a different execution model, a different data source. That is the whole point of the architecture.

5. The engine: one queue, one loop

At the centre sits a single queue (a deque). The event loop pops the next event, looks at its type, and routes it to the right component — which may, in turn, append new events. The loop drains the queue and pulls the next bar, over and over, until the data runs out.

q = deque()
while bar := data.stream_next():        # pull next price
    q.append(bar)
    while q:                            # drain all events from this bar
        ev = q.popleft()
        if   isinstance(ev, MarketEvent): strategy.on_market(ev, q); portfolio.on_market(ev, q)
        elif isinstance(ev, SignalEvent): portfolio.on_signal(ev, q)
        elif isinstance(ev, OrderEvent):  execution.on_order(ev, q)
        elif isinstance(ev, FillEvent):   portfolio.on_fill(ev, q)

That tiny loop is the heartbeat of the system. A new price may trigger a signal; a signal may trigger an order; an order produces a fill; the fill updates the portfolio. Nothing else is needed to coordinate the four bricks.

6. The strategy, and its two knobs

Our example is time-series momentum: if the recent return is positive, assume the trend continues and go long; if negative, go short. This is intimately tied to the autocorrelation discussion from Part 1 — momentum only makes sense if returns carry some positive autocorrelation. Two parameters control it, and both matter enormously.

  • lags — how many days of momentum to average. One lag is the AR(1) case; you might try three, five or seven.
  • threshold — a minimum move below which we do nothing. Financial returns cluster around zero, so without a threshold you'd flip position constantly and bleed money on turnover. Hilpisch calls it a true hyper-parameter, "as important as the strategy idea itself" — like a high-jump bar the move must clear before the strategy acts.
The over-fitting warning, in one chart. Running the same engine with different knobs is sobering. A one-lag momentum strategy lost about 10% while buy-and-hold made about 59%. But push to seven lags with a 0.01 threshold and the same engine made about 86% — beating buy-and-hold. When the result swings that wildly with the parameters, you are looking straight at the danger of over-fitting. A single in-sample run flatters; honest work uses rolling or out-of-sample windows.

Reading the risk, honestly

One subtlety Hilpisch stresses: a long/short strategy on a single instrument has essentially the same volatility as the underlying — about 19% in both cases here — because volatility doesn't care whether you're long or short, only how far returns swing from their mean. So you cannot reduce volatility with a clever long/short rule on one asset. Judge it instead on total return and especially drawdown. A tidy-looking equity curve can still hide a drawdown that lasts nearly three years — and in real time, living through that feels nothing like reading the end result.

7. Going live: swap a single brick

Here is the elegant pay-off. To take this strategy live, you replace exactly one component — the DataHandler. The CSVDataHandler "streams" by reading a file row by row; a live handler streams by listening to a broker's websocket or REST feed (crypto markets, for instance, stream all day long). The Strategy, Portfolio and ExecutionHandler classes, and the event loop itself, stay completely unchanged.

class LiveDataHandler:                  # exactly the same interface
    def stream_next(self):
        tick = broker.get_next_tick()    # websocket / REST
        return MarketEvent(time=tick.time, price=tick.price)

A good learning path. This minimal engine is a framework, not a finished platform — a skeleton and a way of thinking, with named components you can grow. Hilpisch suggests starting from this momentum class, then swapping it for a simple moving-average strategy, then an OLS-prediction strategy — each time changing only the Strategy brick while the rest of the machine stays put.

The one-paragraph version

Most retail traders — and even most hedge funds — fail to beat a simple buy-and-hold, so there are no shortcuts: finance, math and programming must be learned together. To get realistic, we move from a vectorized back-test to an object-oriented, event-driven one that processes a stream of four events — Market → Signal → Order → Fill — through a single queue and event loop. Four reusable "Lego brick" classes (DataHandler, Strategy, Portfolio, ExecutionHandler) do the work, and a time-series momentum strategy with two hyper-parameters (lags and a turnover-cutting threshold) shows how wildly results swing with the knobs — a vivid over-fitting warning, and a reminder that a single-instrument long/short keeps the underlying's volatility. The architecture's reward is that going live means swapping just the DataHandler: the back-test code and the live code are one and the same.
