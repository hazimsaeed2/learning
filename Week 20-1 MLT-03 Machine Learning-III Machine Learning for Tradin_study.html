WEEK 20-1 · MLT-03 · STUDY GUIDE

Neural Networks & Text Mining: How Machines Learn Their Own Weights

A plain-English walk through the third machine-learning session — from a single brain-inspired neuron to a deep network that trains itself by gradient descent, tested on real EUR/USD data, and finishing with how to turn news text into numbers a network can read.

The first two ML sessions used algorithms where you engineer everything. Neural networks are different: you design the shape of the network, and the network discovers its own weights from the data. This session explains what a neuron is, how layers of them form a network, how that network learns by gradient descent and backpropagation, and how the same machinery reads text once you turn words into numbers.

1. From a brain cell to an artificial neuron

A biological neuron collects signals through its dendrites, adds them up in the nucleus, and — if the total is strong enough — fires a pulse down its axon to the next cell. An artificial neuron copies that story exactly. It receives inputs $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$, gives each its own weight $w_i$, adds them into a single number, and passes that through an activation function:

a = Σᵢ wᵢ·xᵢ + b   →   y = φ(a)

The weighted sum a is the "how loud is the combined signal" number; the activation φ decides what the neuron passes on. The simplest version is the Threshold Logic Unit: output 1 if a clears a threshold θ, else 0. That is nothing more than a linear classifier — it draws a straight decision line w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ = θ and calls everything on one side "1" — the very same idea as a support vector machine. Swap the threshold for a plain y = a and the neuron becomes a linear regression. So a single neuron already contains the two workhorses of classical ML; the magic comes from combining many of them.

2. Stacking neurons into a network

Connect neurons in layers and let the signal flow one direction — "forward" — and you get a deep feed-forward network, also called a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). It has three kinds of layer:

  • Input layer — one node per feature; this is where your data enters.
  • Hidden layer(s) — neurons that transform the signal. "Deep" simply means several hidden layers.
  • Output layer — the final prediction, a class (up/down) or a number (a price).

Each neuron in a layer feeds every neuron in the next, each connection carrying its own weight. That is a lot of knobs, which is exactly why networks are so flexible.

Neural networks are universal. A network with a single hidden layer can approximate any continuous, bounded function to arbitrary accuracy — it might just need a lot of hidden units. That power is also the danger: networks over-fit very easily, memorising the training set. The goal is always to minimise the generalisation error (on unseen data), not the training error.

3. Activation functions — and why deep networks used to stall

The activation function is the neuron's personality. The lecture surveys four shapes — linear, piecewise-linear, sigmoid, and threshold — plus the modern default, ReLU. The sigmoid squashes any input into the range (0, 1); in the notebook sigmoid(0) = 0.5 and sigmoid(2) = 0.881. Its zero-centred cousin tanh maps into (−1, 1). ReLU is simply max(0, a) — it leaves positive signals untouched and zeroes out negatives.

The vanishing-gradient problem. Learning relies on passing gradients (slopes) backwards through the network. But the sigmoid's slope peaks at just 0.25 — at a = 0 — and shrinks toward zero for large inputs (the notebook shows sigmoid′(6) = 0.0025). Multiply many such tiny slopes together across deep layers and the gradient effectively vanishes; the early layers stop learning. ReLU fixes this because its slope is a constant 1 for positive inputs. The mirror-image failure, exploding gradients (huge slopes piling up into an unstable network), is tamed with gradient clipping and weight regularisation.

4. How the network learns — gradient descent & backpropagation

You never pick the weights by hand. Instead you define a loss function that scores how wrong the network is — commonly the sum of squared errors Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)², comparing target y to prediction ŷ — and then search for the weights that make it smallest.

Gradient descent is that search. Recall from calculus that a function is minimised where its derivative is zero. Gradient descent walks toward that point: compute the slope of the loss with respect to each weight, then nudge every weight a small step in the negative-gradient (downhill) direction, and repeat. The notebook demonstrates it on a toy loss L(w) = (w − 3)², whose minimum is at w = 3:

Step by step the weight slides to 3 and the loss melts to zero — exactly where the derivative is zero. In a real network there are thousands of weights, so we need an efficient way to get all those slopes at once. That is backpropagation: the chain rule of calculus, applied layer by layer from the output backwards, so each weight receives its fair share of the blame for the final error. Forward pass to predict, backward pass to assign blame, nudge the weights, repeat — that loop is training.

5. A real neural network on EUR/USD

The shipped lab builds an actual network with scikit-learn's MLPClassifier on real EUR/USD daily data (2009-2018). It engineers 16 features — rolling standard deviations, moving averages, summed percentage changes, the intraday Close − Open, and three classic technical indicators from TA-Lib (Bollinger Bands, MACD, Parabolic SAR) — and asks the network to predict whether tomorrow's close is up or down. The data is scaled, split 80/20 without shuffling, and fed to a network with one hidden layer of five neurons using a tanh activation and the adam optimiser.

The result is humbling and honest. The network converged in 107 iterations and scored 52.7% accuracy on training and 50.4% on the test set. Those two numbers being close means the network did not badly over-fit — but both sit right on top of a coin toss, because next-day close-to-close direction is very nearly random, the identical lesson from ML-I and ML-II. The five neurons' weight matrices (16×5 into the hidden layer, 5×1 out to the decision) were discovered by backpropagation, not chosen by a human — which is the whole point of the exercise.

6. Backtesting — why accuracy is not the verdict

A 50% classifier sounds worthless, but accuracy is the wrong yardstick for a trading model. What matters is money. So the lab back-tests the network on out-of-sample EUR/USD (2019-2020, 307 days): go long when it predicts up, short when it predicts down, and compound the daily returns.

The strategy earned +11.9% while buy-and-hold lost 4.4%. How, on 50% accuracy? Because the network's correct calls happened to land on the larger price moves, and its wrong calls on the smaller ones. A model can be right only half the time and still make money if it is right when it counts. The takeaway is a rule for the whole course: judge a trading model by its profit-and-loss, not its classification score.

7. Text mining — turning words into numbers

The session closes by pointing the same machinery at text — news, tweets, filings — for sentiment analysis. A network only eats numbers, so the trick is converting language into a numeric vector. Two steps: first strip out stop-words (the filler words "the", "of", "that", "was" that carry no signal); then vectorise what remains with TF-IDF — term frequency × inverse document frequency. A word's weight grows with how often it appears in a document but shrinks by log(total documents / documents containing the word), so distinctive words dominate and ubiquitous ones fade.

Applied to the lecture's four toy reviews, TF-IDF produces one clean numeric vector per review:

reviewsurviving words and their TF-IDF weights
"I liked the service"liked 0.71, service 0.71 ("I", "the" dropped)
"It was horrible"horrible 1.00 (the only content word)
"Waiting area was not so clean"area 0.58, clean 0.58, waiting 0.58
"Wonderful experience"wonderful 0.71, experience 0.71

Each review is now a row of numbers indexed by the shared vocabulary — exactly the kind of feature matrix a neural network needs to learn "positive" versus "negative", ready to plug back into everything above.

8. The takeaways

  • A neuron is a = Σ wᵢxᵢ + b followed by an activation; a threshold neuron is a linear classifier, a linear one is regression.
  • Stack neurons into input / hidden / output layers to get a feed-forward MLP — a universal approximator that over-fits easily.
  • Activation functions: sigmoid/tanh saturate and cause vanishing gradients (max sigmoid slope 0.25); ReLU avoids it; clipping/regularisation handle exploding gradients.
  • Networks learn by gradient descent (step down the loss gradient, as the toy weight slid to 3) plus backpropagation (chain rule through the layers).
  • The real EUR/USD MLP scored ~50% accuracy yet its back-test beat buy-and-hold (+11.9% vs −4.4%) — judge trading models by P&L.
  • Text mining: remove stop-words, TF-IDF-vectorise, and feed the numbers to a network for sentiment.
