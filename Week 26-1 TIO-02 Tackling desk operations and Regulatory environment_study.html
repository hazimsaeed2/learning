How to Set Up Your Own Algo-Trading Desk

EPAT · Week 26-1 · TIO-02 — the business, infrastructure and regulation of running a trading operation

Most of EPAT teaches you to find an edge. This session is about everything that surrounds the edge: the company you register, the way you reach the market, the servers you rack, the regulators you answer to, and the money it all costs before you place a single profitable trade. The speaker, Nitesh, co-founded iRage — a private high-frequency firm that has been clearing and trading on Indian exchanges since around 2009, doing one to two million trades a day. He has set up trading infrastructure in Mumbai and Singapore, and he speaks from having done it, broken it, and fixed it. One disclaimer runs through everything: the numbers are deliberately crude and illustrative, and none of it is legal, tax or investment advice.

First, what kind of trader are you?

Before any of the operational decisions make sense, you have to know your trading paradigm. You've heard low-, mid- and high-frequency trading defined a dozen ways. The speaker throws out the textbook definitions and uses a business one, anchored to a single question: how quickly does your edge decay if you add delay?

If your strategy still works after you add a hundred milliseconds or more of latency, it is low-frequency and needs no special speed infrastructure. If a few hundred milliseconds would wreck it but a few milliseconds wouldn't, it is mid-frequency. And if every additional microsecond hurts, you are in the high-frequency world. The point of defining it this way is that the entity, the market access, the regulation, the hardware and the money you'll need all flow from where you land on this latency scale. Crucially, these are not rungs on a ladder — there is no natural progression from low to high. Each is a complete, separate business, and there are giant funds living happily in every one of them.

To feel the scale: a retail broker's API round-trips in roughly 30 ms — about 30,000 microseconds. A genuine high-frequency stack operates at 0.5 µs. That makes retail about 60,000× slower. It is why the word "HFT" should be used with care.

The entity — speed versus liability

You can start trading as an individual or in a partnership almost immediately, but both carry unlimited liability — business losses can reach beyond the money you put in. Centuries ago that danger produced the limited liability company, where the "corporate veil" caps your loss at your contribution, lifting only if there's fraud. Companies are powerful — they have shares, so you can seek a valuation or an IPO — but they are heavy to run.

That is why most trading firms choose the LLP, which marries a partnership's flexibility (capital is a contribution with a profit-sharing ratio, not shares) to limited liability. Distributing profits and changing course is easy; you simply can't list it. iRage itself is an LLP and an exchange member. India recently clarified that an LLP may carry on trading and investment up to about ₹2,000 crore (~$110M) without needing an NBFC licence. Setting an entity up is cheap and quick: roughly $300-500 in India, $600-800 for a Singapore local (more like $3-5k for an overseas-controlled one), and $1-3k in the US.

If you want to manage other people's money

There is a whole spectrum here, and it is governed by three switches: do you pool capital or keep each client segregated; can you market openly or only pitch in closed rooms; and what assets can you touch — long-only or long-short and derivatives? At the gentle end, a research analyst (an India-specific licence) just publishes tips for a fee and never touches client money; a registered investment advisor gives actual buy/sell advice for a profit share, still without holding the money. A managed account uses a power of attorney — the money stays in the client's account but you trade it, the model most global CTAs use. Then come the structures where the money actually comes to you: a PMS keeps each client segregated and is long-only-plus-hedging; a hedge fund (an AIF in India and much of Europe) pools capital, goes long and short, can't market openly, and demands a big ticket — around $110k in India, $250k in the US. At the far end, a mutual fund can pool, can advertise on billboards, but is long-only and the most heavily regulated of all, because it takes money from the ordinary public. Accredited investors — those who can prove net worth or qualification — may enter below the prescribed ticket.

Reaching the market — your own membership, or a broker?

You can become your own exchange member or go through a broker, and the pros of one are exactly the cons of the other. Owning the membership buys you lower latency, dedicated infrastructure, control, IP protection, and two subtler prizes. The first is removing counter-party risk: the speaker recalls a time when iRage's own top-tier clearer came close to insolvency — everything was contained, but it spooked them into taking their own clearing membership. The second is capital efficiency. A mere client posts cash or shares as margin; a member can fund margin with fixed deposits, government securities, even bank guarantees. Put up $1M of your own and add a $1M bank guarantee and you command $2M of margin — a 2× capital-efficiency multiplier that roughly doubles your return on capital. Even posting a fixed deposit as margin earns you a few percent of "free" alpha a pure client forgoes. Membership itself comes in tiers: a proprietary member (Prop/Alpha) trades only its own book with the lightest compliance; a trading member can take clients but must clear through someone else; a clearing member both trades and clears; and a professional clearing member (PCM) only clears, without trading.

The catch is a membership fee plus heavy compliance. The speaker's rule of thumb: only buy a membership when the financial benefit — better access, lower cost, capital efficiency — is about two-and-a-half to three times the extra burden. Otherwise a broker can usually arrange dedicated infrastructure at your volume anyway. The simple cost crossover makes it concrete: with a large fixed membership cost but a tiny marginal cost, against a broker's pure per-volume mark-up, the break-even sits near 2,069 million orders a year in our model. Below that, the broker is cheaper; above it, membership wins. Since a mid-tier HFT firm sends 10 to 100 million orders a day, it blows past break-even with room to spare.

Co-location — fairness, finiteness and power

Co-location means putting your server on the same network as the exchange's matching engine. Note the precision: a server in the building next door, or even the same building but on a different network, is only proximity hosting, not co-location. These halls are big — CME's Aurora data centre is hundreds of metres across — so a rack near the matching engine could, in principle, beat a rack far away. Exchanges neutralise that by giving every rack an identical length of cable. Distance in the room is irrelevant; cable length is everything.

Why does length matter so much? In optical fibre a signal travels at about 2×10⁸ m/s, which is roughly 5 nanoseconds per metre, or 1 microsecond per 200 metres. At HFT speeds that is an enormous, unfair edge — hence the equal cable. Three constraints shape what you can put there: space (a rack holds 42 standard "U" units, and global banks once tried to bid for all the space when Indian co-location opened), power (you can't actually fill a rack with forty power-hungry 1U servers — it's power-bound, not space-bound: a rack's default budget is about 6 kWh and a server draws ~0.5 kWh, so you fit only about 12 servers), and network access (many facilities forbid the internet, forcing point-to-point lines). And it is expensive: a full CME rack runs about $15,000 a month — some $180,000 a year — while a full rack in India costs under $15,000 a year, roughly 12× cheaper.

Order routing — you pay for messages

Exchanges don't throttle orders, they throttle messages — every new order, cancellation and modification counts. CME permits 1,500 messages in a rolling three-second window (500 per second per line); NSE permits up to 1,000 per second. Now do the arithmetic for a mid-tier firm sending 100 million orders a day: spread over a 6.5-hour session that is about 4,274 messages per second, which needs roughly 5 NSE lines or 9 CME lines. This is why order-routing connectivity is the big-ticket network expense for an HFT firm; market data, by contrast, is comparatively cheap — often around $1,000 a year for a member. (An exchange earns from three buckets: order fees, co-location or market-data fees, and connectivity.)

And connectivity is where the exponential cost of speed shows up most starkly. The lecture's representative leased lines run about $2,000/month for 82 ms, $5,000 for 78 ms, $45,000 for 71 ms and $125,000 for 68 ms — shaving just 14 milliseconds multiplies the bill roughly 60-fold (about $8,800 per millisecond saved at the fast end), so only genuinely latency-sensitive strategies pay for the last millisecond. The data itself is bulky: one day of tick-by-tick data is around 30-35 GB on NSE and a few hundred GB on CME, across instruments numbering in the ten-thousands.

The platform latency ladder

How you actually run your algorithms sits on a ladder where every rung down in latency costs exponentially more. A broker API for retail is free or a few dollars a month but lives in the tens of milliseconds. Mid-frequency vendors get you to a few milliseconds for a few hundred dollars. High-frequency vendors — rare, because anyone with real speed keeps it in-house — reach hundreds down to tens of microseconds for a few thousand. In-house systems hit single-digit microseconds. And to go below one microsecond, where the leading players live, you move to FPGA or ASIC: you strip out the operating system entirely and code the network adapters directly onto the hardware, because even an OS adds delay. From broker to FPGA is a span of about 60,000× in speed — the same "tens of thousands" gap that separates retail from the front of the pack.

What does it all cost to set up?

The speaker stresses, with feeling, that these numbers are crude and illustrative — and he assumes people cost zero, because at the start you partner rather than hire. With that caveat, the first-year capex to stand up the business spans two end-points. A high-frequency firm with its own membership in a developed market runs to about a quarter of a million dollars (~$260,000 in our breakdown: entity, office, membership lease, co-location, market data, network lines and hardware), and full HFT needs "a few more bundles" still. A mid-frequency firm with no membership in an emerging market needs only about $25-30k (~$24,500), because it skips membership, co-location and expensive market data entirely. The vital footnote: this is setup capex only — not trading capital, not AUM.

Regulation, compliance, and the risk that really kills firms

If you trade through a broker, the broker shoulders most compliance. The moment you become a member, it's yours. Taking a system live usually means either a conformance test (you run against the exchange's test environment — CME's CERT, for example — and submit your logs) or, in India and a few other markets, strategy empanelment, where each automated strategy is approved individually. India recently clarified the retail picture: send fewer than 10 orders per second from a static IP and you're exempt from approval. On top sits record-keeping — transaction logs kept four to eight years, plus saved control parameters that map each order back to its strategy — and ordinary system, password and audit policies. (In the US, the Pattern-Day-Trader rule adds its own twist: more than four intraday equity trades in five days forces a minimum $25,000 account.)

But the lesson the speaker most wants to leave you with is that in automation the deadliest risk is not market risk, it's operational risk. Picture a strategy arbitraging two exchanges. Some roadworks sever a fibre, so prices stop arriving from one exchange — yet your engine, none the wiser, keeps firing orders against stale data. That is a short road to bankruptcy, and the PRM case studies are full of it. The defence is layered: check for staleness in the strategy (no market-data change for N ticks → stop), check the socket connection in your own technology, and rely on the exchange heartbeat — when those periodic messages stop, you know the line is down, and you can pre-authorise Cancel-on-Disconnect so the exchange wipes your resting orders automatically. Any one guard tripping should halt trading and pull your orders.

The takeaways

Setting up a desk is mostly the unglamorous part. Know your paradigm, because latency sensitivity dictates everything downstream. Choose an LLP for limited liability without a company's weight. Weigh your own membership — for capital efficiency and removing counter-party risk — against a broker's lower fixed cost, and only buy in when the benefit clears the compliance burden two-or-three times over. Respect co-location's space, power and equal-cable rules, and remember that order-routing messages, not market data, drive the HFT bill. Climb the latency ladder only as far as your edge demands, knowing each rung costs exponentially more. Budget honestly — a quarter-million for HFT-with-membership in a developed market, twenty-five thousand for MFT through a broker in an emerging one — and never confuse setup capex with trading capital. And above all, engineer against operational failure, because the cut fibre and the stale tick will ruin you faster than any bad trade.
