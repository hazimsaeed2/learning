Your First Back-Test

Week 7-2 · DMP-01 — turning a trading idea into numbers you can trust

This is the lecture where coding finally meets trading. Up to now you have learned Python and you have learned a little about markets; here the two come together in the single most important skill an algo trader has: back-testing — taking a trading rule, running it over historical data, and asking the only question that matters, would this actually have made money? The instructor, Vivek Krishnamurthy, spends the first half on four small ideas that show up in every back-test you will ever write, and only then builds a strategy. We will follow the same path, using a real shipped NSE daily file (962 bars, 2017 to 2020) in place of the live download.

The shape of the work: seven steps

Before a single line of code, it helps to see the whole arc. A strategy is built in seven steps: you (1) have an idea — from forums, news, blogs, academic papers, or plain gut instinct; you (2) download the data — daily is easy, intraday usually comes from your broker; you (3) build the algorithm — calculate indicators, write entry and exit rules, and compute returns and profit in actual money, not just percentages. Then four habits keep you sane: (4) program step-wise and test each piece as you go, because debugging a whole script at once is misery; (5) comment your code so future-you understands it; (6) fragment it into functions — make the data download a function so you don't re-run it every time; and (7) build contingency plans — the simplest being to save your downloaded data to a CSV so a failed download never stops you.

Idea one: simple returns versus log returns

There are two ways to measure how much a price moved. A simple return is the one you learned in school: today's price minus yesterday's, divided by yesterday's. In pandas that is one call, px.pct_change(). A log return takes the natural logarithm of the price ratio, np.log(px / px.shift(1)). Run both on the same data and you'll see they are almost identical — on our NSE file the largest gap between them was about 0.009 — but they are not the same, and the gap widens as returns get larger.

The instructor's analogy is perfect: it's like measuring weight in kilograms versus pounds. The person doesn't change; only the unit does. A 70-kg person isn't 70 pounds. Likewise the price move is real and fixed; simple and log returns are just two units for describing it. (One difference from kg/lb: there the ratio is constant, whereas here the gap between the two grows as the move grows — and log returns always understate the simple return slightly.)

So why have two units at all? Because each is convenient in a different situation. Simple returns are intuitive when you want to talk in plain percentages. Log returns have a magical property — they are additive over time — and they behave nicely in statistical models. That additivity is the heart of the next idea.

Idea two: cumsum, cumprod, and the addition trap

Here is the trap that catches everyone once. Take the price path the lecture uses: 100 → 105 → 108 → 103 → 106. The honest answer to "what did I make?" is obvious: I went from 100 to 106, so I made 6%. Now compute the four daily simple returns — +5.000%, +2.857%, −4.630%, +2.913% — and naively add them up. You get 6.14%. Wrong. Close, but wrong, and on a longer series the error compounds badly.

The fix is to multiply, not add. You compound the (1 + r) factors together and subtract one at the end: (1 + r).cumprod() - 1. Multiply those four factors and you land exactly on 6%. This is what cumprod (cumulative product) does — at each row it multiplies in the new factor, tracing the growth of your money.

Log returns escape the trap entirely, because of a fact from school maths: ln(a) + ln(b) + ln(c) = ln(a·b·c). Add the four log returns and the inner prices cancel telescopically, leaving exactly ln(106/100) = 0.05827. So with log returns you simply add them — cumsum — and convert back at the end if you want a percentage.

The rule to memorise: simple returns go with cumprod (the product rule); log returns go with cumsum (the sum rule). You cannot mix them. Pick a unit and stay in it — measuring half your class in kilos and half in pounds makes the whole roster nonsense.

Idea three: the .shift() operator and look-ahead bias

This little operator does something simple and protects you from something serious. series.shift(1) moves every value down one row, so yesterday's price sits on today's row; shift(-1) pulls tomorrow's value up. That's all it does mechanically. Its purpose is to prevent look-ahead bias — the cardinal sin of back-testing, where your strategy uses information it could not possibly have known at the time.

Why it bites. When you compute a signal from a bar's close and then "trade" on that same bar, you are pretending you knew the close before the bar finished. Your back-test prints glorious, completely fake profits. Shifting the position vector down by one forces the trade onto the next bar — the first moment you could actually have acted. Most blown-up back-tests trace back to a missing or wrong shift.

Idea four: vectorized versus event-driven

There are two philosophies of back-testing. Vectorized — what this lecture uses — lays the whole dataset out as a matrix (rows are timestamps, columns are close, returns, indicators) and does all the calculations at once with NumPy's linear algebra. It is blisteringly fast. Its weakness is realism: it quietly assumes every order fills instantly at the price you wanted, with no slippage and no order-book friction. Event-driven walks through the data one candle at a time, like a for loop, re-running the rules at each "event" (each new candle). It can model unfilled and partially-filled orders, slippage, and the real dynamics of the book — but it is slow and far more work to write.

Use both, in order. Finding a profitable strategy is like finding a needle in a (smallish) haystack — you might start with 15 ideas and end with 2 worth trading. Don't spend expensive event-driven effort on all 15. Use fast vectorized back-tests to winnow 15 down to a few promising survivors, then apply the realistic event-driven treatment to those. (Quantra's free Blueshift is one event-driven engine that also does live trading.)

Putting it together: a vectorized SMA strategy

Now the payoff. The idea is deliberately simple: go long when the price is above its 12-period simple moving average, exit when it falls below. Watch how cleanly the four building blocks slot in.

df["cc"]  = df["Close"].pct_change()           # 1. returns (simple)
df["SMA"] = df["Close"].rolling(12).mean()     # 2. the indicator
df["pos"] = np.where(df["Close"] > df["SMA"], 1, 0)   # 3. the rule: 1=long, 0=flat
df["pos"] = df["pos"].shift(1)                 # 4. shift -> no look-ahead
df["strat"] = df["pos"] * df["cc"]             # strategy return per bar
equity = (1 + df["strat"]).cumprod()           # compound it (simple returns!)
total  = equity.iloc[-1] - 1                   # final return

Read it line by line. rolling(12).mean() is the moving average — the Python equivalent of dragging an AVERAGE formula down a spreadsheet. np.where turns the rule into a column of 1s and 0s: a 1 means "be invested," a 0 means "stay out." A run of consecutive 1s simply means you hold; the instant it flips to 0 you sell. Then the crucial shift(1): because pct_change measures the return into each bar, the position must be lagged one step so that a buy signal earns the following bar's return, not the current one. Finally, multiplying position by returns zeroes out every bar you were flat, leaving only the returns you actually earned — and because these are simple returns, we compound them with cumprod.

The honest result

On the NSE data, after the 12-day warm-up we have 951 rows; the raw signal is long on 606 bars and flat on 345, and after the shift it's 605 long, 345 flat — invested roughly two-thirds of the time. The verdict:

ResultTotal return
Buy & Hold54.43%
SMA-12 strategy54.46%

A dead heat. The strategy did about the same as simply holding the index — no real edge, despite all that activity. In the lecture's own live Nvidia run the SMA strategy actually trailed buy-and-hold (16% versus 29%). Either way the lesson is the same, and it is the whole reason back-testing exists: most simple ideas do not beat the benchmark. The point of a back-test is not to admire a strategy but to let the numbers tell you the unflattering truth before you risk real money.

The one-paragraph version. Measure moves in simple returns (pct_change) or log returns (np.log(P/P.shift(1))) — and combine them correctly: simple with cumprod, log with cumsum, never mixed. Always shift your signal by one bar to avoid look-ahead bias. Screen many ideas fast with vectorized back-tests, then validate the survivors with realistic event-driven ones. And when you finally run your clean little strategy and it merely ties the benchmark — that isn't failure, that's the back-test doing its job.
