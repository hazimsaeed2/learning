Quantitative Portfolio Management

Put yourself in a portfolio manager's chair. Every PM answers three questions from the data: (1) which universe of assets, (2) how many constituents, (3) in what proportion (weights)? The path: a single stock → a two-stock portfolio → Harry Markowitz's covariance insight (1952 Nobel, Modern Portfolio Theory) → the diversification limit → the efficient frontier and weight optimisation → an out-of-sample backtest. Every number below is computed in the practice notebook from the real Indian-equity data that ships with the lecture — TCS, Maruti, Marico, the Nifty 50 benchmark and a 49-stock Nifty matrix (Jan-2015–Aug-2020).

1 · There is no single "best" portfolio

The best portfolio depends on the objective. Decide the purpose first — it determines the asset universe.

InvestorObjectiveTypical universe
Pension / endowmentPreserve capitalBonds, low-vol assets
Hedge / mutual fundCapital appreciation / alphaEquities, riskier assets

Our goal for the class: minimum risk and maximum return. We chase it concretely, analysing every instrument from two angles — returns and risk.

4 · Portfolio return = weighted sum

For weights w and returns r:

rₙ = wₐrₐ + wₛrₛ + … + wₙrₙ = Σ wᵢ rᵢ

Worked step (50/50 TCS+Maruti):

StepValue
w·r = 0.5·16.16 + 0.5·19.42= 8.08 + 9.71
Portfolio return17.79 %

Scales to N constituents unchanged.

5 · Markowitz — portfolio variance needs covariance

Before 1952 people summed weighted variances. Markowitz showed that's incomplete — the co-movement of assets matters. If one falls while another rises, combined risk drops.

σ²ₙ = wₐ²σₐ² + wₛ²σₛ² + 2·wₐwₛ·cov(A,B)

Scalable matrix form (any N):

σ²ₙ = w · Σ · wᵀ  ;  σₙ = √(σ²ₙ)

Σ = variance–covariance matrix. Its diagonal holds the variances because cov(x,x)=var(x).

Worked step (50/50 TCS+Maruti):

TermValue
σ²ₐ (TCS var)0.0623
σ²ₛ (Maruti var)0.0921
cov(A,B)0.0189
0.25·0.0623 + 0.25·0.0921 + 2·0.25·0.01890.04805
σₙ = √0.0480521.92 %

21.9 % is BELOW both 24.96 % and 30.35 % — that's diversification, the free lunch.

6 · Lower correlation → lower risk

Covariance is unbounded; correlation standardises it to [−1, 1]:

corr(x,y) = cov(x,y) / (σₓ·σₐ)
Portfolio (50/50)CorrelationVolatility
TCS + Maruti0.2521.9 %
TCS + Marico0.1219.2 %

Key takeaway: hold uncorrelated securities — lower correlation pushes portfolio volatility down further.

9 · Out-of-sample backtest

Take the 6 names into the held-out final ~30 % of the sample (an unseen window running through the 2020 COVID crash). Compare equal-weight vs max-Sharpe optimised:

WeightsCumulative (out-of-sample)
Equal-weight8.9 %
Max-Sharpe optimised28.1 %

The optimised weights led out-of-sample here, but the in-sample edge (Sharpe 1.05) is no guarantee — markets shift regime, so PMs rebalance periodically to stay close to the backtest.

VaR is the loss threshold; CVaR is the average loss once you breach it (it also reveals the tail's size). Kelly > 1 implies leverage — most traders bet a fraction of it because it ignores drawdowns.

11 · The PM's checklist

  1. Define the objective first — it sets the universe.
  2. Analyse returns (arith for analysis, CAGR to publish) and risk (variance for analysis, vol to publish).
  3. A single stock is risky (~25 % vol for ~16 %).
  4. Return = weighted sum; variance = w·Σ·wᵀ (covariance matters).
  5. Hold uncorrelated securities — lower correlation, lower risk.
  6. Diversification has a limit (a systematic floor ~18 %); it can only be hedged, not diversified.
  7. Optimise weights on the efficient frontier (min-vol / max-Sharpe / max-return).
  8. Backtest out-of-sample & rebalance — the edge lives in universe choice and quantamental constraints.
  9. Judge with risk-adjusted ratios (Sharpe/Sortino/Treynor/Calmar/Information), watch tail risk (VaR/CVaR), size with Kelly.
