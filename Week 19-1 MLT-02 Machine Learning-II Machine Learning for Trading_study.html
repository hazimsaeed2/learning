Machine Learning-II: Evaluating Models, Support Vector Machines & Clustering

A plain-English walk through Jay's second ML session — how to tell a good model from one that's only pretending, the confusion matrix and the metrics that survive imbalanced data, the maximum-margin idea behind Support Vector Machines, unsupervised grouping with K-means, and a single change of target that quietly lifts the edge from a coin toss to ~80%.

Lecture 1 built a model; lecture 2 asks the harder question — is it any good? Along the way it introduces two new algorithms — a supervised one (the Support Vector Machine) and an unsupervised one (K-means clustering) — but the real lesson is a way of thinking: most of the time the algorithm is not your problem, your data and target are. The session is structured to make you feel that conclusion rather than just hear it.

1. Why training is called .fit

Training a model means making it fit — represent — the shape of the data. The instructor shows a wiggling cloud of points and a line that tries to pass through it. Tune the model and three things can happen. An under-fitted model is too simple to capture the shape (a straight line through a curve). A well-fitted model tracks the true pattern and generalises. An over-fitted model wiggles even more than the data, chasing every noisy point — it has memorised the training set and will stumble on anything new. We always aim for the middle one.

2. The two sources of error: bias and variance

Why do models drift toward under- or over-fitting? Two errors creep in.

Bias is the model's inability to learn the association between features and target — train it as many times as you like and it still can't. It produces under-fitting. The cause might be too simple an algorithm, too little data, or features with no predictive power. Variance is the opposite: an over-reliance on the training data. Change the training set and the model falls apart, because it memorised rather than learned. It produces over-fitting.

How to quantify them (for classification). A model with training accuracy below 50% is biased — it does worse than a coin toss. A model whose train-minus-test accuracy gap exceeds about 8–10% has high variance. The instructor's bullseye image: a biased thrower lands systematically off-centre; a high-variance thrower scatters all over the moment the dartboard changes. We want low bias and low variance.

3. The confusion matrix and the metrics that matter

To evaluate a classifier we tabulate its predictions against reality. By convention class 1 is the positive class (in trading, "take a long") and class 0 is negative ("no position"). Every prediction lands in one of four cells:

predicted 0predicted 1
actual 0True Negative (TN)False Positive (FP)
actual 1False Negative (FN)True Positive (TP)

We apply this to the SVM's real test set — 372 days, of which 175 were truly class 0 (131 TN, 44 FP) and 197 truly class 1 (29 FN, 168 TP). From these four numbers everything follows:

  • Accuracy = (TP + TN) / total = 299/372 = 0.804. Fraction predicted correctly, regardless of class.
  • Precision = TP / (TP + FP) = 168/212 = 0.792. Of every "up" the model called, how many actually rose.
  • Recall = TP / (TP + FN) = 168/197 = 0.853. Of every real up-day, how many the model caught.
  • F1-score = the harmonic mean of precision and recall = 0.822. One number for when you care about both.

Why accuracy lies

Imbalanced data wrecks accuracy. If only 70 of 1000 records are positive, a lazy model that always predicts 0 scores 93% accuracy — while never identifying a single positive (recall 0.00). This is exactly what a "next day up 5%" target produces: a handful of positive days in 1500, so a do-nothing model looks 90%+ accurate yet never trades. Since in trading we only act on the positive class, we lean on precision and recall, both centred on the TP cell. The instructor prefers these over artificially rebalancing the data.

4. Support Vector Machines

The first new algorithm is the Support Vector Machine (SVM), a supervised classifier with an elegant idea. Between two classes you can usually draw many boundaries; the SVM insists on the single boundary — the separating hyperplane — that sits as far as possible from both classes. The width of that no-man's-land is the margin, and the handful of points that touch its edges are the support vectors. They alone define the boundary; move any other point and nothing changes.

A little vocabulary from the slides. A supporting hyperplane has the entire dataset on one side with at least one point resting on it. A separating hyperplane divides the two classes — and because infinitely many separate the same data, the SVM chooses the maximum-margin one. In equation form a hyperplane is a₁x₁ + a₂x₂ + … + b = 0; the distance from a point to it follows the usual point-to-line formula; and the margin between the two supporting planes works out to 2 / ‖w‖. Making the margin as wide as possible is therefore the same as making ½·wᵀw as small as possible, subject to every point being on its correct side (yᵢ(wᵀxᵢ + b) ≥ 1). That is the entire optimisation.

Kernels — bending a straight boundary

Real data is rarely separable by a straight line. The kernel trick quietly projects the data into a higher-dimensional space where a straight boundary does exist, without ever computing those coordinates explicitly. The common kernels are linear (w·x), polynomial ((w·x+1)ᵖ) and Gaussian / RBF (exp(−‖w−x‖²/2σ²)). Like logistic regression, an SVM needs its features scaled.

Soft margin vs hard margin. A hard-margin SVM permits no misclassifications at all — which sounds ideal but forces the boundary to contort around every awkward point, i.e. it over-fits. A soft-margin SVM adds a penalty term, ½wᵀw + C·Σeᵢ, that lets a few points sit inside the margin. The constant C is the dial: a large C punishes errors heavily and pushes toward a hard margin (more variance); a small C is more forgiving (more bias). It is the bias–variance trade-off from section 2, now a single knob.

But notice the headline number — every kernel clears ~80% on the test set. That is startling after MLT-01, where every algorithm hovered near a coin toss. The reason is the next section.

5. The one change that lifts the edge — the target variable

In MLT-01 the target was "will tomorrow's close be higher than today's?", and no algorithm could beat ~50%. Here the shipped SVM asks a subtly different question: "will tomorrow's open be higher than today's open?" — and it also lags the volume so only yesterday's volume feeds the model. Same feature toolkit, same split, but the accuracy leaps to about 86.5% train / 80.4% test.

Why the leap? Not the algorithm — the target. Every feature is built from the close, so the model is meant to be run at the close (16:00), by which point the entire trading day is known. That closing information largely anchors tomorrow's open — the overnight gap is small — whereas tomorrow's close is a whole fresh day of randomness away. The open-to-open question is simply more answerable. The implied trade is buy at today's close, sell at tomorrow's open (a "buy today, sell tomorrow", BTST). Crucially there is no look-ahead bias: everything the model needs is known the moment it runs, and lagging the volume keeps same-day information from leaking in.

This is the lecture's real punch-line, and it is a habit more than a result: when a model suddenly performs well, find out why before you trust it. Here the entire gain came from a smarter, more logical target — proof that thoughtful target and feature design beats algorithm-hopping every time.

6. K-Means Clustering — learning with no answer key

Everything so far has been supervised: we had a target column and asked the model to predict it. Clustering is unsupervised — there is no target at all. We simply ask the machine to group objects that resemble one another, and the most common method is K-means. Its loop is almost embarrassingly simple:

  • choose the number of groups, k, and scatter k random centroids;
  • assign every point to its nearest centroid;
  • move each centroid to the mean of the points now assigned to it;
  • repeat the last two steps until the centroids stop moving.

"Nearest" depends on a distance measure: the straight-line Euclidean distance is standard, but Manhattan (summed absolute differences, as if walking city blocks) and Mahalanobis (which accounts for how the features co-vary) also appear. Because it is distance-based, K-means must have scaled features — otherwise a large-magnitude column silently dominates. K-means is not the only way to cluster: hierarchical methods build a tree of groups, either bottom-up by repeatedly merging the closest pair (agglomerative, "AGNES") or top-down by splitting one big cluster (divisive, "DIANA").

The slides warm up with ten customers plotted on two attributes. Run K-means with k=2 and the points fall cleanly into two segments (labels 0,0,0,0,0 and 1,1,1,1,1) around centroids at roughly (3.2, 6.0) and (7.2, 3.4) — two natural marketing groups discovered with no labels. The shipped notebook then does the real thing: it clusters 368 companies described by six fundamental ratios (forward P/E, price-to-book, forward EPS, beta, quick ratio, debt-to-equity) into k=3 groups of sizes 132, 213 and 23.

Choosing k — the elbow method

How many clusters is right? K-means can't tell you, so we use the elbow method. For each k we compute the inertia — the total squared distance from every point to its centroid — and plot it:

In trading, clustering a universe like this drives portfolio construction, groups open positions by shared risk, and surfaces candidate pairs for statistical arbitrage — all without a single label.

7. The takeaways

  • .fit = represent the data's shape; avoid under-fit (bias) and over-fit (variance).
  • Bias = train accuracy < 50%; variance = train−test gap > ~8–10%. Want both low.
  • Confusion matrix → accuracy, precision = TP/(TP+FP), recall = TP/(TP+FN), F1 = their harmonic mean. Accuracy lies on imbalanced data — the real SVM scored 0.804 accuracy, 0.792 precision, 0.853 recall.
  • SVM draws the maximum-margin hyperplane; kernels (linear/poly/RBF) bend it; a soft margin's C trades bias for variance. A more flexible kernel widened the train−test gap.
  • The real edge came from a smarter target — open-to-open, run at the close, volume lagged — lifting the SVM to ~80% test.
  • K-means is unsupervised (no target); it groups by similarity, and the elbow of the inertia curve picks k (here k=3 for 368 firms).
