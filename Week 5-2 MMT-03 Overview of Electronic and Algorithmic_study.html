Overview of Electronic & Algorithmic Trading

Week 5-2 · MMT-03 (instructor Rob Cassell) — the first of two sessions, written as a lesson you can read end to end.

This lecture is the bridge from "what is a market" to "how do I actually trade in one." It is deliberately about process and intuition — the equations for market impact and timing risk arrive in the second session (MMT-04). The single thread running through everything is one hard trade-off, and once you see it, the rest of the lecture is a tour of the tools built to manage it.

1. Why algorithms exist: the price-impact problem

Electronic trading simply means sending orders over a computer network; algorithmic trading is the computerised execution of orders following pre-specified rules. Algorithms became essential because institutional orders are large — far more than the 100 shares a retail investor might buy. A large order moves the price: buying pressure pushes prices up, selling pressure pushes them down. This is market impact, and it is one of the costliest, most unavoidable components of trading.

The defence is to slice the order — break a 100,000-share parent into, say, ten 10,000-share children and trade them over time so the market never sees the whole thing at once.

2. Market impact: temporary plus permanent

When you buy aggressively, the price rises while you trade and then reverts once you stop — but it does not return all the way. That gap is the heart of market-impact analysis.

StagePriceInterpretation
Undisturbed$30.00where the stock sat before you arrived
Peak (while buying)$30.25total impact = 25¢ a share
New equilibrium$30.05permanent impact = 5¢ (information the market inferred)
Revert amount30.25 → 30.05temporary impact = 20¢ (the liquidity premium you paid)

Temporary impact is the premium you offered to attract sellers to trade with you right now; it evaporates after you finish. Permanent impact is the market's belief that your order carried information ("why is that party buying so much? maybe it's undervalued"), so the price settles higher than where it started. Sell orders behave as a mirror image.

3. The trader's dilemma

Now the central tension. Market impact falls the slower you trade — leaking the order out gently disturbs the price less. But timing risk rises the slower you trade — the longer you are exposed, the more the price can drift against you on news, volatility, or other traders catching on. Timing risk itself has three pieces: price volatility, liquidity risk, and parameter-estimation error in the impact model.

Rob Cassell's one-line summary: "Trade too fast and you move the market; trade too slow and the market moves you." Your job is to balance the two.

Formally you minimise a combined cost:

Cost(t) = MarketImpact(t) + λ × TimingRisk(t)

where λ (lambda) is your level of risk aversion. With impact falling like 1/√t and risk rising like √t, the optimum has a clean closed form, t* = a/(λ·b). Calibrated to the lecture:

λInvestor typeOptimal trade horizon
0.25risk-neutral (indifferent to risk)3.40 days — trade slowly
1.0balanced (equal concern)0.85 day — the lecture's answer
3.0risk-averse (worried about risk)0.28 day — trade quickly
Why would you be risk-neutral? Because you might be trading 50 buys and 50 sells at once — if the market moves, your buys and sells offset, so you are naturally hedged and can afford to take your time. A single urgent order, by contrast, makes you risk-averse: get it done before the edge disappears.

4. Slicing schedules: VWAP, TWAP, POV

Having decided how long to trade, you choose how many shares per interval. Three classic schedules split our 100,000-share order across the day:

  • TWAP (time-weighted) — equal shares in every bucket, a flat uniform schedule (about 7,143 shares across 14 half-hour buckets).
  • VWAP (volume-weighted) — follow the day's intraday volume profile, a J-curve that is heavy at the open and heaviest into the close, light at midday. Trading in proportion to volume hides your order in the crowd.
  • POV (percentage of volume) — trade a fixed share of whatever prints, e.g. 10%. If you are 10% of volume and you have 100,000 shares, the day must print about 1,000,000 shares for you to finish.

Orders can be sliced by time, by volume (POV), or by price (trade harder at favourable prices). And any algorithm is either static (parameters fixed unless you change them) or dynamic (it revises its own schedule in response to live market signals).

5. Agency vs principal bid — and why frequency decides

There are two ways a broker fills your order. An agency execution charges a commission and works the order on a best-efforts basis — you bear all the market risk and don't know the final price in advance. A principal bid (capital commitment) charges a fat premium but guarantees a price — the broker takes all the risk. Which is better? Work the numbers from the lecture:

On the surface a ~70% chance says "do the agency execution." But Rob Cassell's deeper point is that trading frequency changes the answer. If you trade daily, a 70% edge compounds in your favour and a bad day is quickly recovered. If you trade once a year, the 30% chance of a worse fill — with a twelve-month wait to make it back — may not be worth it. The right decision blends the probability with how often you get another shot.

6. Where trades happen: exchanges, ATSs, and dark pools

The modern market is not one exchange but dozens of trading venues:

  • Exchanges — displayed order books, and they must maintain a fair and orderly market (provide liquidity, run closing auctions, halt on extreme volatility).
  • ATSs / ECNs — also displayed, but with no obligation to provide liquidity; if there's no counterparty, your order simply doesn't fill.
  • Dark pools — the order book is hidden. You can place a large order without showing your hand, which limits immediate impact, but you can't see the queue and don't know why you're not filling. Dark pools do roughly 30–35% of US volume and match at the midpoint of the NBBO.

Every displayed venue keeps an order book: bids sorted highest-to-lowest, asks lowest-to-highest, executed under price–time priority (best price first; ties broken by who arrived earliest). Across all venues, the NBBO (national best bid and offer) is the highest bid and lowest ask anywhere. Worked example over three venues:

VenueBest bid × sizeBest ask × size
A30.00 × 80030.10 × 300
B30.02 × 20030.15 × 600
C29.90 × 160030.06 × 100

The NBBO bid is 30.02 (venue B) and the NBBO ask is 30.06 (venue C), so a dark pool would print at the midpoint, (30.02 + 30.06) / 2 = $30.04. We know the price a dark trade would occur at, even though we can't see the hidden orders behind it.

7. Smart order routing: the longest queue can be fastest

Suppose you want to buy 100 shares at $30. Under price–time priority you wait behind everyone already queued at that price. The naïve move is to pick the shortest queue — but the smarter move weighs how fast each venue trades. Watch what happens when venue A trades ten times the volume of B and C:

VenueShares aheadRelative speedExpected wait (queue ÷ speed)
A260010×260 — fastest
B8001×800
C14001×1400
The grocery-store analogy: you don't just count the people in each line — you also look at how big their carts are and how fast the cashier is. The longest line can check out first if its cashier is ten times quicker. Smart order routers do exactly this calculation, in real time, across every venue.

8. How venues get paid: maker–taker economics

Finally, the plumbing. Venue fees are quoted in mills — one mill is 1/100 of a penny ($0.0001). There are three revenue models:

ModelWho pays / who is rebatedVenue keeps
Commission (typical dark pool)both sides pay 10 mills20 mills
Maker–takertaker (market order) pays 30; maker (limit order) gets 10 rebate20 mills
Inverted maker–takermaker pays 30; taker gets 10 rebate20 mills

A maker adds liquidity by posting a limit order and is rewarded with a rebate; a taker removes liquidity with a market order and pays a fee. The inverted model flips this — it's a "pay to cut the line" arrangement, like buying a fast-pass at an amusement park to jump the queue instead of waiting for the orders behind you to clear. High-frequency firms build whole strategies around harvesting these rebates.

9. The decision-making process, in five steps

Pulling it together, executing an institutional order is a repeatable process: (1) select a broker; (2) select an algorithm and its parameters (the macro-level schedule); (3) transact using a Limit Order Model and Smart Order Router (the micro-level placement); (4) revise the strategy if conditions change; (5) measure cost and evaluate performance. The overarching goal is to minimise total transaction cost while keeping the trade consistent with the fund's investment objective.

Cassell's parting warning: brokers keep their market-impact models secret partly so you can't grade their execution quality. Always verify what a broker tells you — the second session (MMT-04) is devoted to exactly that, with the math and the Python.

10. Resource addendum - landscape, taxonomy, and intraday profiles

The official lecture objectives and summary add a broader map around the worked examples: where execution sits in the investment cycle, who participates in the market, how algorithm families differ, and why intraday volume, volatility, and spreads matter to execution logic.

ParticipantRole in the execution ecosystem
BrokersFacilitate client trades through agency execution, principal commitment, algorithms, DMA, and venue access.
InstitutionsGenerate large parent orders from portfolio construction and rebalancing decisions.
Liquidity providersPost bids and asks, support displayed liquidity, and seek spread/rebate economics.
Hedge funds / prop / HFTUse profit-seeking, market-making, liquidity, or pattern-recognition strategies.
Retail investorsTypically submit smaller orders through brokers or wealth-management channels.

The algorithm taxonomy below is useful because "algorithmic trading" is not one strategy. Some algorithms only execute a portfolio manager's order; others make investment decisions, provide liquidity, or simply give clients direct venue access through broker infrastructure.

Strategy familyExecution meaning
Arrival Price / Implementation ShortfallTrades faster or slower relative to the order's arrival price and the cost-risk objective; behavior differs by broker.
Front-Load / Back-LoadFront-load trades more at the beginning to stay close to arrival price; back-load waits for a future benchmark such as the close.
Liquidity SeekingUses displayed and dark venues to capture liquidity at a specified price or better, often aggressively near order completion.
Optimal StrategyChooses schedule or POV rate by optimizing market impact versus timing risk for a chosen risk-aversion level.
Portfolio / BasketCoordinates many buy and sell orders together so risk, hedge, and schedule constraints stay aligned.
DynamicRevises participation rate or schedule from live prices, volumes, fills, and re-optimization rules.

Finally, execution models use intraday market microstructure. The lecture notes and Q&A highlight that volume is typically J-shaped, volatility is closer to L-shaped in modern US data, and spreads are widest near the open and close. A VWAP, POV, or dynamic algorithm needs those curves to decide how much to trade and how aggressively to place orders.

Key takeaways

  • Market impact = temporary (liquidity premium, reverts) + permanent (information, stays): 25¢ = 20¢ + 5¢.
  • Trader's dilemma: minimise MI(t) + λ·TR(t); impact falls and risk rises with time; balanced λ → 0.85 day.
  • Slicing: TWAP (uniform), VWAP (follow the J-curve), POV (fixed % of volume); static vs dynamic.
  • Agency vs principal: $30.12 ± 20¢ vs guaranteed $30.25 → ~70–74% chance agency wins, but frequency decides.
  • Venues: exchanges (must provide liquidity) and ATSs are displayed; dark pools hide their book and print at the NBBO midpoint ($30.04).
  • Smart order routing: balance queue depth and venue speed — the longest queue can fill fastest.
  • Maker–taker: makers earn rebates, takers pay fees, mills = $0.0001.
