1 · The two questions this lecture answers

Every single thing in this 3-hour session circles back to two very practical questions a real trader asks:

  1. "Can I trust this backtest?" — A backtest is a simulation of your strategy on past data. It's easy to produce a gorgeous equity curve that means nothing. We need to know when to believe it.
  2. "Can I actually make money — and how much?" — Once we trust the method, we want a realistic profit number, then a path to running it live.

Keep these two questions in your head; they are the thread through the whole lecture.

2 · The model we use the whole time: Buy-Low-Sell-High on SPY

To make everything concrete, the instructor uses one tiny strategy throughout. SPY is the ETF that tracks the S&P 500 — think of it as "the US stock market in one ticker." The rule is:

Buy-Low-Sell-High (a mean-reversion strategy):
At the end of each trading day, look at today's close vs yesterday's close.
• If today's close < yesterday's close (price dropped) → buy SPY with all your cash (go long).
• Otherwise (price rose or flat) → sell everything (hold cash), or in the long/short version, short it.

The bet is that a down day tends to be followed by a bounce — prices "revert to the mean." That's the entire alpha idea. Notice how few inputs it uses: just two numbers — today's close and yesterday's close. We'll come back to that; it matters enormously.

Plain-English analogy: it's like buying an umbrella the day after a storm because you think sunshine is "due." Sometimes right, sometimes not — the job of backtesting is to find out how often, and whether it pays.

3 · The same rule as a machine-learning model

Here's the part that surprises people: the instructor doesn't just assert that SPY bounces back. He proves it with machine learning — and it's the gentlest possible introduction to ML, so don't be intimidated.

Step 1 — turn prices into "yesterday's move" and "tomorrow's move"

For every day we compute two percentage changes:

yield_yesterday = (close_today - close_yesterday) / close_yesterday   # how it moved INTO today
yield_tomorrow  = yield_yesterday shifted up by one row               # how it moves the NEXT day

That little shift is the whole trick: it lines up "the move I can see now" next to "the move I want to predict." Now we can ask a precise question: does yesterday's move predict tomorrow's?

Step 2 — fit a straight line (linear regression)

Linear regression just finds the best straight line through a cloud of points. We feed it yield_yesterday (the input, x) and yield_tomorrow (what we want to predict, y). The line's slope — its "coefficient" — tells the whole story:

  • slope < 0 → a down day leans toward an up day = mean reversion (good for buy-low-sell-high)
  • slope > 0 → moves continue in the same direction = momentum
  • slope ≈ 0 → yesterday tells you nothing
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
model = LinearRegression().fit(hist[['yield_yesterday']], hist['yield_tomorrow'])
print(model.coef_)        # the slope
The result on the real course data (SPY, 2001–2021): slope = −0.10. It's negative, so SPY genuinely mean-reverts. The "buy when it fell" idea isn't a hunch — the data confirms it. (A scatter plot of the two columns with this line drawn through it tilts gently downward; you'll see exactly that chart in the practice notebook.)

Step 3 — turn the model into a stock screener

If the slope measures how strongly a stock reverts, we can compute it for many stocks and rank them. Wrap the regression in a function, loop over tickers, collect the slopes:

TickerSlope (mean-reversion coefficient)Reads as
SPY−0.095strong reversion (best fit for this strategy)
QQQ−0.063reverts
AAPL−0.049reverts
GOOG−0.022weak reversion
TSLA+0.003momentum — buy-low-sell-high would lose here

This is a real, useful insight: broad index ETFs revert; a single hot growth stock like TSLA trends. So buy-low-sell-high belongs on an index, not on a momentum name. The screener just told us where the strategy works — that's research, done with one number.

A richer feature — MACD. Instead of yesterday's raw move, you can feed the regression a momentum indicator like MACD = (fast moving average − slow moving average) ÷ price. Same machinery, a different (and often cleaner) input. This is exactly how you'd "add a good parameter" later when we find the model is slightly underfit.

Step 4 — the threshold turns the model into trades

The model says "down days tend to bounce," but how far down should it fall before we buy? We add one tuning knob, rate: go long if yield_yesterday < rate, otherwise go short. Sweeping that rate is what later produces the famous "best ≈ 1.1%" — and, as we'll see, also the over-fitting trap.

4 · Why a beautiful backtest is often a lie

The instructor showed this model producing a portfolio curve that crushes "buy and hold SPY." Then he asked the class: what's wrong with this picture? This is the most useful checklist in the lecture. A backtest looks too good when it quietly ignores real-world frictions:

Hidden problemWhat it means in plain words
Transaction costEvery buy/sell costs money (brokerage + exchange + regulator fees + taxes). Rough feel: ~0.5% round-trip in some markets, much lower in the US, ~0.01–0.05% intraday in India. The lecturer's target assumption: 0.5%. "1% is definitely too high." If your sim assumes zero cost, profits are inflated.
SlippageThe price you see isn't the price you get. By the time your order hits the market, it has moved a little against you. Real, and the basic sim ignores it.
Fractional sharesYou have \$100, SPY costs \$60 → you can really only buy 1 share, leaving \$40 idle. But the sim assumes it can deploy 100% of cash every day, i.e. buy fractional shares most brokers don't allow. That quietly boosts returns.
No train/test splitHe tuned the model on the same data he tested it on. Good practice is to split data into a training part and an out-of-sample test part (covered in the backtesting course). He didn't here — though, fairly, he's used this exact model unchanged for ~8 years, so it has effectively survived a long out-of-sample period.
Just as important — what is NOT the problem here:
  • Look-ahead (forward) bias? No. Look-ahead bias means using future information you wouldn't have had at decision time. This model only uses yesterday's and today's close — no future data leaks in. So this common suspect is innocent for this strategy.
  • Too many transactions? No — it actually trades quite rarely.
Knowing what isn't wrong is as valuable as knowing what is. Don't reflexively blame look-ahead bias.
See it for yourself in the notebook. The practice notebook charges a small cost every time the position flips. At 0% cost the strategy turns \$1 into ~\$19; at 0.5% per flip it ends at \$0.12 — a −88% loss. The strategy trades almost daily, so even a tiny cost compounds into ruin. That single chart is the lesson.

5 · The big idea: overfitting vs underfitting, measured properly

This is the gold of the lecture. Most people "feel" whether a model is overfit. The instructor insists on a number.

The definitions (memorize these)

  • Overfitting = too many parameters, too little data. The model memorizes the training data instead of learning the pattern, so it predicts badly on new, real data. (Great backtest, horrible live.)
  • Underfitting = the model is too simple to capture the true nature of the data. Performance is mediocre and unstable because it missed the main driver. The fix: add parameters.
Common myth, busted: overfitting is NOT "great in backtest, bad in real life." That's a symptom, not a definition, and you can't measure it. Overfitting/underfitting is decided by how much data you have per parameter — nothing else.

The measuring stick: degrees of freedom

A degree of freedom is, roughly, one parameter (one "knob") your model's decision depends on. Count the knobs, then compute:

Data points per degree of freedom = (number of rows of data) ÷ (number of parameters). The bigger this number, the safer you are from overfitting.

Worked example — our SPY model

How many degrees of freedom does Buy-Low-Sell-High use? The class guessed 2; the real answer is 3:

  1. Today's close price — parameter 1.
  2. Yesterday's close price — parameter 2. (Even though we got it by shifting today's close up by one row, yesterday and today are genuinely independent values, so they count as two.)
  3. The threshold "rate" (he used 1.1%) — parameter 3. Why does a fixed number count? Because he tuned it: he swept the rate from −0.02 to +0.02 and picked the best. Tuning a value spends a degree of freedom — you used extra information to choose it.

Now the data: ~20 years of trading days ≈ 5,000 rows. So:

5000 rows ÷ 3 parameters ≈ 1,666 data points per degree of freedom

Is 1,666 good?

Use caseRule-of-thumb data per parameter
A typical, well-behaved linear regression~20 to 100 is fine
Stock price prediction (very noisy, not normally distributed)aim for 1,000+ — prices are wild, so you need much more data per knob
ChatGPT (for perspective)~175B parameters, ~0.5–1T training tokens → only ~5 data per parameter — yet fine for its use case

So our 1,666 is above the 1,000 bar for prices, but not by a huge margin. Verdict: slightly underfitting, but close to a healthy fit — not a great model, not a terrible one. And since it's underfitting, the prescribed fix is to add more (good) parameters — which means doing research to find features that genuinely help (e.g. the MACD feature from §3).

The takeaway you'll reuse forever: before trusting any model, count its tuned parameters and divide your data by that count. For price strategies, want ≥1,000 per parameter. This single habit catches most overfitting disasters.
The honest test in the notebook. When we tune the threshold on the first half of the data and then trade the second half we never looked at, the magic mostly evaporates: in-sample the strategy made ~×19, but out-of-sample it barely clears ×1 while buy-and-hold nearly quadruples. That gap is over-fitting, made visible.

6 · When do we trade? The 3:59 PM trick

The US market closes at 4:00 PM Eastern. Our rule needs today's close to decide — but at 4:00 the market's gone and you can't trade. So the robot makes its decision at 3:59 PM, one minute before close.

But wait — at 3:59 there is no official close price yet. Why is this okay?

The assumption: the spot price at 3:59 is statistically almost equal to today's official close. So we use the 3:59 price as a stand-in for the close, decide, and place a market order for near-instant execution.

How would you prove that assumption? Take historical data, compute the difference between the 3:59 price and the actual close each day, and run a Student's t-test to check the difference is statistically indistinguishable from zero. That's the kind of rigor he wants — don't assume, test.

8 · Anatomy of a trading robot

In IBridgePy the robot is surprisingly short. Two built-in functions do the work:

def initialize(context):
    # runs ONCE at the start — set up everything
    context.security = symbol('SPY')              # what to trade
    schedule_function(buy_low_sell_high,          # run this function...
                      date_rule=date_rules.every_day(),       # ...every trading day...
                      time_rule=time_rules.market_close(minutes=1))  # ...at 3:59 PM

def buy_low_sell_high(context, data):
    # runs every day at 3:59 PM
    hist = request_historical_data(context.security, '1 day', '2 D')  # 2 daily bars
    yesterday_close = hist['close'][-2]
    today_close     = hist['close'][-1]
    if today_close > yesterday_close:
        order_target_percent(context.security, 0.0)    # price rose -> hold 0% (sell all)
    else:
        order_target_percent(context.security, 1.0)    # price fell -> hold 100% (all-in long)
  • initialize(context) runs once — it claims your variables (stored on context), defines the contract via symbol(), and sets the schedule.
  • handle_data(context, data) (or a scheduled function) is where decisions are made. context holds the variables you set in initialize; data holds account info and near-real-time quotes.
  • order_target_percent is "rebalance to a target weight." 0.0 clears the position; 1.0 puts 100% of the portfolio into SPY. You state the destination, the engine works out the buy/sell.

10 · The guessing game — what real returns look like

He ran the long-only model over different periods and had the class guess the return. Here are real numbers computed on the actual SPY data file from the course (SPY daily, 2001-01-03 → 2021-04-09), so you see honest figures, not hand-waving:

Period (long-only Buy-Low-Sell-High)Total returnAnnualised Sharpe (daily)

2016-01 → Apr 2021+82.1%0.86
Full 2001 → 2021+476.7%0.66
Buy & hold SPY, full period+348.4%~0.4

Read these honestly:

  • The strategy beats buy-and-hold over the long run (+477% vs +348%) and roughly doubles the Sharpe ratio (~0.8 vs ~0.4). The Sharpe ratio is return per unit of risk — higher is better; doubling it is meaningful.
  • But a Sharpe of ~0.8 is "good, not spectacular." That's consistent with our earlier verdict: the model is only mildly underfit — decent, improvable.
  • Over short windows the numbers swing a lot (the long/short variant even underperformed in one year). That variability is exactly why you don't trust a single pretty period.
  • And remember §4: these are before transaction costs. Add realistic costs and this daily strategy turns negative — the honest version, not the brochure version.

11 · Taking it live: holidays, early closes, emails, crashes

A backtest that works is only half the job. Live trading throws real-world curveballs. He adapted a Moving Average Crossover strategy (buy when a short moving average crosses above a long one — MA(5) vs MA(60)) and improved it one step at a time. Each improvement adds the one thing live trading needs that a backtest never told you about:

ImprovementLive issue it solvesHow
1. Handle holidaysMarkets close on holidaysif not isTradingDay(): return — do nothing on closed days.
2. Handle early close~3–4 days/year the US market shuts at 1:00 PMisEarlyClose() is expensive, so check once at 10 AM, set a flag, and decide at 12:59 instead of 15:59 on those days.
3. Email notificationYou want to know what the robot didschedule_function(send_email, ...) at 16:30 (after the broker finalises the close) to email your account balance.
4. Handle exceptionsAn order fails while you're awayWrap orders in try/except: email the error and keep the robot running instead of crashing.
5. Minute-bar versionYou want signals every minute, not once a daySet repBarFreq: 60 so handle_data fires every minute; read the clock yourself to define your market window (e.g. 9:30–16:00).

Errors you will hit live (and what they mean)

  • No data permission — you must buy the broker's data subscription for that market; call the broker's rep.
  • Market not open — you can still get a price, but it may be stale. Always check the price's timestamp is fresh enough.
  • Requesting historical data too often — brokers rate-limit history requests to protect their servers. Optimize your code to ask less.
  • Not enough margin — after selling you may need a day for cash to settle before you can buy again. Catch it with try/except.
  • Order status unknown — a limit order may or may not have filled; use order_status_monitor to confirm before continuing.

When stuck, his answer is always the same: the IBridgePy YouTube channel, tutorials, documentation, and blog have a worked solution for each of these.

12 · A second strategy, straight from an academic paper: VIX predicts returns

To show how research becomes code, he implements a published paper (Giot, On the relationships between implied volatility indices and stock index returns). The VIX is the market's "fear gauge" — high VIX = panic, low VIX = calm. The paper's finding: extreme fear tends to precede bounces, extreme calm tends to precede dips.

The rule: keep a rolling 2-year window of VIX. Go long the index for one day if today's VIX is the highest in that window (or in the top 2 percentile boxes). Go short for one day if it's the lowest (or in the bottom 2 boxes). Otherwise stand flat.

The point isn't this specific strategy — it's the workflow: read a paper → restate its rule precisely → fetch the data (here a second instrument, the VIX index, via superSymbol) → schedule the decision near the close → place the order. That is how a real quant turns literature into a robot.

13 · The pro toolbox (one breath each)

  • Stock screener — e.g. "hold the 10 most active US stocks, 10% each." You pass a scan universe + a scan code (most active) + filters; it returns a ranked DataFrame you feed into orders.
  • Options — get the option chain, then filter to the expiry closest to a target date and the strike closest to a target price — those are the liquid, active contracts. Search them in IB's security database or via IBridgePy.
  • Greeks — call get_option_info with the names you want (Delta, Gamma, Vega, Theta, implied volatility) and get them back in a dictionary. (Greeks measure how an option's price reacts to moves, time, and volatility.)
  • Multiple accounts — fund managers generate one signal but route different sizes to different linked accounts by passing an account number to each order. Great tooling for managing many clients from one signal.
  • High-frequency trading — don't, as a retail trader. It needs internet latency near zero, a high-performance CPU, lots of memory, a fast algorithm, stable tick-by-tick data, and rock-bottom transaction cost. The hurdles are too high.
  • Smart Routing — IB's software continually scans competing exchanges and routes your order to the best venue on price and availability of automatic execution. Specify an exchange yourself only if you have a reason to.
  • Deployment: local vs cloud — local is cheaper long-term, full resources, full privacy, but you manage power, internet, backups. Cloud gives ~99.99% uptime and easy "push code to remote," but you pay for storage/compute and get limited OS choices. Develop locally; deploy to the cloud when reliability matters.

14 · From an idea to a live robot — the roadmap

Zooming out, every strategy in this lecture follows the same nine-step path. This is the checklist to reuse for any new idea:

  1. Come up with an idea — e.g. "if price drops vs yesterday, buy; else sell." Trade daily.
  2. Collect related data — SPY daily bars.
  3. Build a mathematical model to test it — linear regression.
  4. Draw graphs to visualise — matplotlib scatter + equity curve.
  5. Program it in IBridgePy — a strategy .py file with initialize + handle_data.
  6. Backtest the strategy — and run the overfitting check.
  7. Forward-test / paper-trade on data the model never saw.
  8. Go live small with real money to get a true feel.
  9. Track, monitor, and improve — handle the live-trading issues from §11.

This is also, in miniature, how an algo hedge fund is built: research an edge → translate it into a robot → run it on a server → keep a verifiable track record → raise outside accounts → manage them from one signal (multiple accounts) → keep improving.

15 · The mindset that actually makes money

"If you torture the data long enough, it will confess to anything." — Ronald Coase. Keep tweaking parameters and you can fit any past. That's overfitting in one sentence; resist it.

The instructor's honest process for turning a model into money:

  1. Do fundamental or quantitative research; collect data to support the idea.
  2. Turn it into a trading robot.
  3. Backtest it (trust it only after the overfitting check).
  4. Forward-test (paper trade) on data the model never saw.
  5. Run a small real-money account to get a true feel.
The baseline rule: if your strategy can't make money in the mathematical model, it will never make money in the real market. The model is the floor, not the ceiling. And judge models by risk-adjusted return (Sharpe), not raw return alone.

16 · Test yourself

Q1. We turned the "buy when it fell" rule into a machine-learning model. What single number proves SPY mean-reverts, and what sign must it have?

The linear-regression slope (coefficient) of yield_tomorrow on yield_yesterday. It must be negative (it's ≈ −0.10 for SPY). A positive slope, like TSLA's, means momentum.

Q2. The backtest looks amazing. Name three real-world frictions that could be inflating it — and one suspect that is not the problem here.

Transaction cost, slippage, fractional-share assumption (also: no train/test split). NOT the problem: look-ahead bias (the model uses only past closes).

Q3. Our SPY model has 3 parameters and ~5,000 rows. Compute data-per-parameter and judge over/underfit for a price strategy.

5000 ÷ 3 ≈ 1,666. For prices you want ≥1,000, so it's slightly underfitting but close to healthy. Fix = add good parameters (e.g. a MACD feature).

Q4. Why does tuning the "rate" threshold cost a degree of freedom?

Because you swept many values and chose the best — you used extra information to pick it, so it counts as a fitted parameter, not a free constant.

Q5. Why decide at 3:59 PM, and how would you justify it statistically?

You need today's close but can't trade at 4:00. The 3:59 spot price ≈ the close; justify with a t-test on historical (3:59 − close) differences being ~zero.

Q6. You need a 20-year backtest but have no minute data. Which data approach do you use and why?

"Simulated by daily bars" — it fakes the minute bar from the day's close, and daily bars are available everywhere while 20 years of minute bars usually aren't.

Q7. Name IBridgePy's three cornerstones and the live-trading improvement that stops one bad order from killing the robot.

Cornerstones: real-time price, historical data, place order. The improvement: wrap orders in try/except, email the error, and keep running.

Master rule: a backtest is only as honest as its frictions and its data-per-parameter. Make costs realistic, keep ≥1,000 data points per tuned knob for price strategies, then forward-test before risking real money.
