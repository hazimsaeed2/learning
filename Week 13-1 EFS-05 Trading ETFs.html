Trading ETFs

How an ETF is built — indices, baskets, in-kind creation/redemption, and the arbitrage that keeps price ≈ NAV.
Taught from practice
ex-Goldman ETF PM · 25y markets
THE PROBLEM
Mutual funds have two structural flaws: a free-rider/liquidity cost and a tax cost forced on every holder.
YOUR GOAL
Understand the ETF structure — index → basket → in-kind creation/redemption → secondary-market arbitrage.
THE EDGE
In-kind baskets push costs onto the AP and realize no gains — solving both flaws. Price hugs NAV via arbitrage.
CORE EXAMPLE THREADING THE WHOLE SESSION
A cap-weighted index of 2 stocks (MV ₹7.5 m) normalized to 1000 via a divisor of 7,500. Watch what corporate actions do to the divisor while the index stays 1000.
A 10-stock creation basket worth ₹3.50 m + ₹45,325 cash → 100,000 ETF shares ⇒ ≈ ₹35.52 cost per ETF. Redeem it back, sell, get ≈ ₹35.38. The 14-paise gap = the AP's round-trip cost.

1 · Cap-weighted indices & the divisor

Market value = price × shares. An index provider (MSCI, FTSE Russell, S&P, India's IISL) normalizes the messy total into a round number with a divisor:

Index = Total MV ÷ Divisor

When a corporate action changes shares/constituents but not real value, you re-solve the divisor so the index doesn't jump. Recomputed once a day.

Tip: Index providers are "super-powerful" — MSCI dropping a country moves billions in tracking money.
Corporate actionTotal MVDivisor
Base7,500,0007,500
2-for-1 split7,500,000 (same)7,500 (same)
$2 dividend7,400,000 ↓7,400
Constituent swap15,000,000 ↑15,000

Index stays 1000 in every row.

Split = no value change → nothing moves. Dividend / swap = MV changes → divisor re-solved.

2 · Fund structures & the two flaws of mutual funds

Open-endedClosed-ended
Units created/redeemed on demand; unit count floats; usually no secondary market; AUM game.Fixed pot; trades on a secondary market; price can drift far from NAV (no arbitrage path back).
NAV = Portfolio value ÷ Units

Modern funds price at end-of-day NAV ("NAV-next") — fair-looking but scandal-prone (late trading, market timing).

FLAW 1 · Free-rider / shared liquidity

A redeemer triggers a sell costing ₹2,000, charged at fund level → split across all 3 (₹667 each). The redeemer gets ≈ ₹999,333 (more than ₹998,000): long-term holders subsidize the trader (~1.5–2% CAGR drag).

FLAW 2 · Tax

Active manager sells a ₹500k winner → ₹75,000 tax event distributed to all, even holders who didn't sell.

History: Hakansson (1976) named the flaws → Nathan Most (a commodity trader) built SPDR, the first ETF, in 1993.

3 · Primary market — the ETF as a warehouse

The ETF manager is a warehouse manager, not a stock-picker. You transact in lots (a creation unit, e.g. 100,000 shares), and it's in kind — no cash for the stocks.

  • Creation: AP assembles the published creation basket + cash component → hands it in → gets ETF shares.
  • Redemption: AP returns ETF shares → gets a basket of stocks back (sells them itself for cash).
  • Authorized Participants = special, deep-pocketed firms (≈30+ for early SPDR; today often HFTs).
Tip: the cash component funds rebalances & corporate actions, since the fund never takes cash for stocks.

4 · How in-kind baskets solve both flaws

Replay the 3-investor story as an ETF: investors bring baskets (not cash); the manager hands back ETF shares. A redemption hands back a basket and extinguishes the units.

AssetsUnitsNAV
Day 1 (3 baskets)7,500,000300,00025.00
Day 2 (1 redeems)5,000,000200,00025.00
FLAW 1 solved: NAV unchanged (25→25). The AP carries the transaction burden, not remaining holders.
FLAW 2 solved: in-kind delivery realizes no capital gain → no tax forced on holders.
Baskets track the index: constituents change at rebalances (NIFTY 50 ≈ 4 changes in ~15y). So an ETF manager barely trades — opposite of an active manager.

5 · Secondary market — iNAV, premium/discount, arbitrage

ETFs trade all day on a lit order book. An indicative NAV (iNAV) is published live = basket value ÷ units. APs uniquely see both markets, so they arbitrage the gap and pin price ≈ NAV.

SignalMeaningAction
Premiummarket > iNAV (35.45)SELL
Discountmarket < iNAVBUY
In bandwithin 35 bpsno trade
Tip: require a markup (e.g. 35 bps; up to 100–200 bps on illiquid counters) before bothering. This linkage is absent in closed-ended funds — that's why ETF price hugs NAV.

30-second glossary

Divisor — number that turns total MV into a clean index level; re-solved on corporate actions.
NAV — portfolio value ÷ units. iNAV — live intraday estimate of it.
Creation unit — the lot size (e.g. 100,000 ETF shares) you must transact in.
Creation basket — the exact stocks+cash you assemble to get ETF shares.
In kind — swap basket ↔ shares with no cash for the stocks (the magic that fixes tax + free-rider).
Authorized Participant (AP) — special firm allowed in the primary market (often an HFT).
Premium / Discount — secondary price above / below iNAV → sell / buy.
Free-rider problem — in mutual funds, in/out traders' costs fall on long-term holders.
SPDR — first ETF (1993), by Nathan Most, a commodity trader (hence warehouse/basket lingo).
