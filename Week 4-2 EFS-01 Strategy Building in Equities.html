② In-sample vs out-of-sample — the overfitting trap

Optimise on a training slice, then test the winner once on held-out data. If it doesn't carry over, you curve-fitted noise. Nifty split 80/20:

④ Position sizing — the Kelly fraction

How much to bet each time to maximise long-run compounded wealth. For a 50/50 bet paying 2:1 (win pays 2× stake, loss forfeits stake):

f* = (b·p − q) / b = (2×0.5 − 0.5) / 2 = 0.25

Bet fractionFinal wealth (50W/50L, start 10)
10%469
20%2,890
25% ← Kelly3,611 (peak)
30%2,890
50%10 (back to start!)
TIP: trade only half-Kelly (~12-15%) in practice — real edges are estimated with error and full Kelly is brutally volatile.

⑤ Drawdown — the number investors ask first

Drop from the running peak of the equity curve (measured from the historical high, not your entry). The brutal asymmetry:

Lose……need to recover
10%+11.1%
25%+33.3%
50%+100%
90%+900%

Our strategy: max DD −40.2%, annual return 8.5%, return/DD = 0.21 → fails the >2 rule. Another red flag this strategy isn't live-ready.

⑧ Market parameters & the z-score factor model

Open interest (OI) = outstanding futures agreements (not volume). Cost of carry = futures − spot (from F = S·e^(rT)). Read the OI × price quadrants:

Price ↑Price ↓
OI ↑fresh long build-upfresh shorting
OI ↓short coveringprofit booking

Factor model: blend factors with weights summing to 1. Convert each to a z-score first (z = (x−μ)/σ) so a wide-scale factor doesn't dominate by accident.

⌛ 30-second glossary

  • In/out-of-sample — train on one slice, test on a held-out slice.
  • Overfitting — a fit that shines on the past but fails live.
  • Hit ratio — fraction of trades that profit.
  • Normalised hit ratio — share of favourable money; weights wins by size.
  • Kelly fraction — bet size that maximises compounded growth.
  • Drawdown — drop from the equity-curve peak.
  • Sharpe — return per unit of risk, annualised ×√252.
  • Open interest — outstanding futures agreements.
  • Cost of carry — futures price minus spot.
  • Leverage / slippage — multiplies P&L / ~4-5 bps execution loss.
