① Core Example — Weekend Premium Strategy (4 Iterations)

WHAT

Idea: IV > RV most of the time → sell straddles and capture premium. Deploy around weekends when fear of overnight/weekend risk can lift premiums. Sell on Friday, buy back Monday. Tested four iterations — in this lecture's backtest, the version with a VIX filter was the first one that looked reasonably viable.

WHY

People may overpay for insurance before weekends because they fear gap-ups or gap-downs from weekend news. The lecture's framing is that Friday premiums can be richer and may ease by Monday once uncertainty passes. But selling blindly failed in crisis periods such as COVID and election shocks, when Monday premiums stayed elevated.

HOW — Strategy Evolution

Iteration 1: Weekend SPY Straddle

Sell weekend spy ATM straddle Friday → expire Monday.

❌ Lost heavily during COVID. Only data from 2018 — too short.

Iteration 2: Weekly SPY (Longer History)

Sell weekly contract (expires next Friday) on Friday → close Monday.

❌ Worked 2011–2017 but crashed during US elections & COVID.

Iteration 3: Weekly + VIX Filter ✓

Same as #2, but sell only when VIX is below the 98th percentile.

✅ First viable strategy — 29 % return, 71 % win rate, 27 % vol.

Iteration 4: Hold Till Friday (Not Monday)

Sell Friday → hold entire week → expire next Friday.

❌ Too long in this test — midweek events hurt positions. The lecture treats weekend premium as a short-lived window rather than a full-week hold.

WORKED EXAMPLE
SPY at 450. Sell ATM straddle Friday: sell 450C + 450P = collect $8 premium. If Monday fear eases and the premium decays to $5, buy back → $3 profit. In the lecture's backtest, this was filtered to periods where VIX was below the 98th percentile.
TERMINOLOGY
Weekend spy = contracts expiring Monday (new since 2018).
ATM straddle = sell call + put at same strike = current price.
VIX = CBOE Volatility Index (implied vol of S&P 500, next 30 days).
GOTCHA
The lecture presents weekend premium as real but narrow in this example. Friday→Monday held up better than a full-week hold, and the VIX filter improved the backtest by avoiding panic regimes where Monday premiums stayed high. Process: theoretical idea → backtest → diagnose failures → add filters → re-test.

② Volatility Measurement — Estimators, Not Truth

WHAT

There is NO true measurement of volatility — only estimators. Standard deviation of log returns uses only close prices (sample, not population). Range-based estimators (Parkinson, Garman-Klass) use more data points per day for less error. All are biased in different ways.

WHY

Stock prices are always a sample, never a full population (future prices unknown). More data points per day = less estimation error. But the key insight: it doesn't matter WHICH method — stick to ONE and use it consistently for comparison. Like measuring temperature — the unit doesn't matter, the context does.

Standard Deviation

σ = std(log returns). Uses only close prices (1 data point/day). Simple but highest error.

Apple example: 1.252 %

Parkinson Estimator

Uses High & Low (2 data points/day). Inputs: N (total days), H (high), L (low).

Apple example: 1.12 % — biased LOW because the formula only sees the intraday High-Low range; overnight gaps are outside its input. With 10 days: captures ~81 % of true vol. More days → closer to true, but this overnight limitation never disappears.

Other Estimators

Garman-Klass: OHLC (4 points/day).
Rogers-Satchell: includes drift.
Yang-Zhang: includes jumps.
More data points = less error, but NO perfect method.

# --- Standard deviation method ---
def calculate_volatility(symbol, start, end):
    data = yf.download(symbol, start=start, end=end)   # Yahoo Finance
    returns = np.log(data['Close']).diff().dropna()      # log returns
    vol = returns.std() * np.sqrt(252)                   # annualize: √252 trading days
    return vol                                           # e.g., 0.30 = 30%

# --- Parkinson estimator ---
def calculate_parkinson_volatility(symbol, start, end):
    data = yf.download(symbol, start=start, end=end)
    N = len(data)
    hl = np.log(data['High'] / data['Low']) ** 2         # ln(H/L) squared
    vol = np.sqrt((1 / (4 * N * np.log(2))) * hl.sum())  # Parkinson formula
    return vol * np.sqrt(252)                             # annualize
WORKED EXAMPLE — Day vs Night Vol
Day vol: use High & Low → measures intraday range.
Night vol: use prev Close → current Open → measures gap.
Total variance = weighted sum: (T₀/D)×σ²_night + (Tᵢ/D)×σ²_day.
D=24h, T₀=18h (closed), Tᵢ=6h (open).
TERMINOLOGY
Estimator = approximate measurement (not exact).
Range-based = uses price range (H−L) vs close-only.
Biased low = systematically underestimates true value.
Population vs sample = all possible vs observed data.
GOTCHA
High-frequency data does NOT solve the overnight problem — ticks only exist when market is open. A minute-by-minute AAPL dataset still misses 18h of overnight risk. More granular ≠ more accurate; match data frequency to your trading horizon.

③ Volatility Forecasting — EWMA & GARCH

WHAT

Rolling window (30-day std): spikes/crashes from single events entering/leaving window — untradeable. EWMA (Exponential Weighted Moving Average): weights recent data more, smooth transitions. GARCH(1,1) (Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity): models all three volatility characteristics.

WHY

Options trading needs FORWARD-LOOKING volatility. Rolling windows fail (Netflix example: 15 % price spike → vol doubles, sustains 30 days, then crashes). GARCH captures clustering, mean-reversion, and return-dependence — the three key volatility behaviors.

Three Volatility Characteristics (→ GARCH terms)

CharacteristicMeaningGARCH Term
ClustersHigh-vol stays high, low stays low for extended periodsβ × σ²ₜ₋₁ (prev vol)
Mean-revertsTends to drift back toward a long-term average more than prices doγ × V (long-term var)
Depends on recent returnsRecent price crashes change vol behaviorα × r²ₜ₋₁ (prev return²)

GARCH(1,1) Equation

σ²ₜ = γV + α·r²ₜ₋₁ + β·σ²ₜ₋₁

Constraint: α + β + γ = 1

Trader assumptions: α ∈ [0.01, 0.1], β ∈ [0.9, 1.0], then γ = 1 − α − β.

Parameters: properly found via Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE), but traders assume practical ranges — forecasts still work.

WORKED EXAMPLE — Netflix
30-day rolling vol: price jumps 15% → vol doubles (0.3→0.6), sustains 30 days, crashes day 31 when spike leaves window. EWMA (λ=0.94): same spike but smooth decay over 30 days. GARCH: captures both the spike AND the mean-reversion back to long-run vol.
TERMINOLOGY
EWMA: σ²ₜ = λσ²ₜ₋₁ + (1−λ)r²ₜ₋₁, λ∈[0.9, 0.99].
GARCH = Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity.
MLE = Maximum Likelihood Estimation (find α,β,γ that maximize likelihood function).
GOTCHA
GARCH forecasts must be contextualized! A vol of 0.9 means nothing alone. Compare to percentiles across 20/40/60/80/100-day windows. If forecast is at 25th percentile → low vol phase. If near 100th percentile → extreme vol. Same number, different context = different trade.

④ Historical, Implied & Realized Volatility — The Variance Premium

WHAT

Historical = past volatility (backward). Implied = future expected vol derived from options premiums via Black-Scholes (forward). Realized = actual vol within a specific contract period (NOT = historical — tied to contract timeframe). Key equation: P&L = Vega × (IV − RV), works on average.

WHY

IV > RV most of the time across many asset classes and volatility regimes discussed in the lecture. People pay an "insurance" premium for uncertainty. This gap is the variance premium. But it only works on average — individual trades can lose even when the broader theory is sound.

Historical Volatility

What happened in the past. Known, definite. Uses price history.

Example: Apple's 30-day historical vol = 25 %.

Implied Volatility (IV)

What the MARKET expects in future. Derived from options bid/ask. If someone bids high → they expect high vol. Input premium into BSM, solve for σ → that's IV.

Realized Volatility (RV)

Actual vol within a CONTRACT's lifetime. NOT = historical. After contract expires, calculate what actually happened. If IV was 30 % but RV was 22 % → 8 % variance premium captured.

# --- The fundamental P&L equation for volatility trading ---
# P&L ≈ Vega × (IV − RV)        # works ON AVERAGE across many trades
#
# Example: Vega = $500, IV = 30%, RV = 22%
# Expected P&L = 500 × (0.30 − 0.22) = 500 × 0.08 = $40 per unit
#
# Monte Carlo simulation: even when IV = RV = 30%,
#   individual trades show non-zero P&L (both profits and losses)
#   but AVERAGE across many trades → $0 (as equation predicts)

# --- Variance premium across asset classes ---
# Equities:   IV > RV most times. INDEX options > single stock (more hedging demand)
# Commodities: exists but VARIES by commodity (crude ≠ sugar ≠ copper)
# Bonds:      exists, less volatile than equities
# VIX:        even VIX futures have variance premium!
WORKED EXAMPLE
S&P 500: IV consistently above RV across low, medium, and high vol phases. Even at VIX=15 (low), IV premium exists. Small-cap stocks show ~2× the variance premium of large-caps (more uncertainty).
TERMINOLOGY
Variance premium = IV − RV (the "insurance" gap).
Vega = ∂Premium/∂Vol (dollars per 1% vol change).
Black swan = rare, extreme event that can wipe out a year of premium income.
GOTCHA
P&L = Vega×(IV−RV) is an AVERAGE — don't expect it every trade. Monte Carlo shows wide distribution of outcomes. Short vol = consistent small gains + rare catastrophic losses. One black swan can erase months of premium collection. Always size positions accordingly.

⑤ Covered Call Strategy & Asset-Class Volatility Nuances

WHAT

Covered call: long underlying + sell OTM (Out of The Money) call at resistance. Collect premium as passive income. Widely used by retail traders. BXM ETF tracks this on S&P 500: 7.1 % annual return (vs 9.4 % SPX), lower volatility, better Sharpe, better max drawdown.

WHY

Simplest form of volatility trading — delta partially hedged by stock ownership. Premium decays in your favor if stock stays flat or rises slightly. Caps upside at strike price but provides income buffer. Not a pure vol trade but an entry point for retail traders.

Covered Call Mechanics

1. Own 100 shares of stock (long position).
2. Sell 1 call at resistance level.
3. Collect premium → income.
4. If stock stays below strike → keep premium + stock.
5. If stock rises above strike → profit capped, shares called away.

Asset-Class Vol Trading

Equities: Index > single stock premium. Small-cap > large-cap.
Commodities: Can't generalize — crude ≠ sugar ≠ copper. Need domain knowledge (inventory reports, weather, currency).
Bonds: Premium exists, less volatile.
VIX: VIX futures also have variance premium!

Commodity Trading Insight

"You can trade equity with statistics, but you can't trade commodities with statistics."

Crude: DXY + inventory reports. Sugar: weather + crop reports. Copper: DXY + LME inventory. Each commodity has unique volatility drivers — statistical methods alone won't work.

⑥ Earnings Volatility Trading — The Implied Jump

WHAT

Common pattern: IV often gradually increases before earnings and then drops sharply right after release. Two strategies: (A) Buy straddle 10 days before, close day before release (ride the IV ramp). (B) Capture the implied jump using two contracts at different expiries. The lecture says similar logic can apply to FOMC, inventory reports, and employment data.

WHY

Before earnings: uncertainty → split opinions → IV rises. After earnings: information released → uncertainty resolved → IV crashes. This IV pattern is one of the most reliable in options markets. Also: Post-Earnings Announcement Drift (PEAD) — positive earnings → momentum continues 1–2 months.

Strategy A: Ride the IV Ramp

Buy ATM straddle 10 days before earnings. Close 1 day before release.

Capture the gradual IV increase without the binary earnings risk.

Results: 43 % win rate, 1.7× win/loss ratio (wins are bigger than losses).

Strategy B: Capture the Implied Jump

Use two contracts: T₁ (front week, expires during/before earnings) and T₂ (back week, expires after).

Implied jump = (2/π) × σ_E. Compare to historical max jump. If forecast ≈ max → sell front-end straddle.

Results: 66 % win rate when jump ≈ historical max.

WORKED EXAMPLE — Implied Jump
σ₁ (1-day contract) = 50 %, σ₂ (1-week contract) = 20 %.
T₁ = 1 day, T₂ = 7 days.
Implied jump = 2.6 %. Historical max = 3 %.
2.6 % ≈ max → high conviction → sell front-end straddle.
TERMINOLOGY
PEAD = Post-Earnings Announcement Drift (momentum after good earnings).
Front week = contract expiring during earnings.
Back week = contract expiring after earnings.
Earnings reversal = stock falls after positive earnings (already priced in).
GOTCHA
Earnings reversal is common — stock rises BEFORE earnings (anticipation), then sells off on "buy the rumor, sell the news." Don't confuse positive earnings with positive price action. The vol CRASH after earnings is the reliable pattern, not the price direction.

⑦ Scientific Strike Price Selection

WHAT

Don't buy cheapest or sell most expensive options. Instead: for each strike, calculate premium at IV (current market price) AND premium at RV (using BSM with realized vol). Select the strike where the gap (IV premium − RV premium) is LARGEST — that's where variance premium is greatest.

WHY

The most common retail mistake is buying cheap OTM options or selling the most expensive ones. But the optimal strike is where IV − RV gap in premium terms is maximum. This mathematical approach removes guessing and aligns your trade with the variance premium thesis.

WORKED EXAMPLE — Four Strikes Compared

StrikeIV Premium (Market)RV Premium (at 30 %)GapPick?
$95$8.50$8.31$0.19No
$100$5.80$2.56$3.24✓ Best
$105$3.40$0.99$2.41No

$100 strike has the highest gap ($3.24) between premium at IV and at RV → sell this strike to capture maximum variance premium.

HOW — Step by Step
1. Get current market premiums for each strike (this is at IV).
2. Calculate current realized vol (Parkinson or GARCH).
3. Use BSM: input RV as volatility → get "fair" premium per strike.
4. Gap = Market premium − BSM premium at RV.
5. Sell the strike with the largest gap.
TERMINOLOGY
Fair price = BSM premium at realized vol (not market price).
Variance premium gap = IV premium − RV premium.
Strike selection = finding optimal strike for max edge.
Resistance-based = alternative approach using chart resistance for covered calls.
GOTCHA
This method assumes RV stays stable — if RV spikes after you sell, the gap narrows or reverses. Best combined with GARCH forecast: if you forecast LOW future vol, the gap widens further. Settlement mechanism can change your P&L — close positions before expiry unless deep in the money.

⑧ "Vega Wounds, Gamma Kills" — Hedging & Risk

WHAT

"Vega wounds, Gamma kills" — the lecture's intuition is that volatility is more likely to mean-revert than price, so a wrong vol bet may recover over time, whereas a wrong directional bet can keep trending against you. Delta hedging isolates vol exposure: sell Δ shares to neutralize price sensitivity. But continuous hedging is expensive and impractical.

WHY

If you short vol and it spikes, the lecture argues that vol often mean-reverts, so the loss can be more temporary than a pure directional loss. If you short a stock and it trends up for a long time, your loss can keep compounding. This is why vol trading is framed as more forgiving than directional trading, but you still need position sizing and filters to survive the wound period.

Delta Hedging Example

1-year $100 call, stock at $100, vol 30 %, premium $11.92, Δ = 0.56.

Hedge: sell 0.56 shares → price-neutral.

Stock → $110: call → $18.14, Δ → 0.68. Already hedged 0.56 → rehedge +0.12 shares.

Repeat at every price change → pure vol exposure. But costly & impractical for retail.

Short Vol Risk Profile

Long options: limited loss (premium paid), unlimited profit.

Short options: limited profit (premium received), unlimited loss.

Short vol = consistent small gains + rare catastrophic losses.

→ In the lecture's examples, position sizing and a VIX-style filter matter a lot.

OTS-05 covers: optimal hedge frequency, at what price to hedge, when to stop.

WORKED EXAMPLE
Short AAPL straddle at $241. Stock drops to $226 → profit. Then spikes to $260, $280 (gamma exposure) → the directional loss can keep growing. But short vol: vol spikes from 20→35, you lose, and if vol later mean-reverts to 22 the loss can recover. Time can heal some vol shocks more easily than pure directional moves.
TERMINOLOGY
Delta hedging = selling Δ shares to neutralize price risk.
Gamma = rate of delta change (2nd derivative).
Implied skewness = put premiums > call premiums (crash protection costs more).
Slippage = execution cost of hedging trades.
GOTCHA
"Vega wounds" ≠ "Vega is safe." COVID March 2020: VIX hit 82 — short vol positions needed months to recover. If you're leveraged or undersized, the wound can still be fatal before vol reverts. The lecture's practical safeguards are: (1) a VIX filter, (2) position sizing for worst-case scenarios, (3) avoiding continuous hedging before studying better methods.

KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. No True Vol Measurement
All methods are estimators. Parkinson uses H&L and is biased low because its formula only measures intraday range, not overnight gaps. GARCH captures clustering, mean-reversion, return-dependence. Pick ONE method, stick to it, contextualize with historical percentiles. The number alone is meaningless.
2. IV > RV = Your Edge
Variance premium appears across the asset classes and volatility regimes discussed in the lecture. P&L ≈ Vega × (IV − RV) on average. But individual trades vary widely, so one trade can still be painful. Short vol often looks like many small wins with occasional severe losses.
3. Filter, Don't Trade Blindly
Selling straddles every day struggled during crisis periods in the lecture's tests. The weekend-premium example improved once a VIX < 98th percentile filter was added. The earnings example was framed as attractive when implied jump looked large relative to history. The strategy-iteration process (idea → backtest → diagnose → filter → re-test) is the real method.
4. Vega Wounds, Gamma Kills
Vol tends to mean-revert more than price, so wrong vol bets can recover more often than wrong directional bets. But "wounds" can still be fatal if overleveraged. The lecture's safeguards were a volatility filter, proper position size, and disciplined strike selection using the IV−RV gap.
