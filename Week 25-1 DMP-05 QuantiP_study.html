QuantiP — Building a Quant Strategy in Python

EPAT · Week 25-1 · DMP-05 — how a professional goes from idea to a tested strategy, and how to trade a real academic anomaly

Most people learning to trade with code start in the middle: they grab a notebook, write a moving-average rule, and run a backtest. Ishan — a quant researcher who has worked at Barclays and Merrill Lynch — wants you to zoom out. A professional follows a fixed set of steps, in order, and most of the value is in the steps beginners skip. This session walks through that workflow, then uses it to implement a famous strategy from a real research paper.

Why a workflow at all?

Whether you join a hedge fund, set up a prop desk, or just trade your own money, the discipline is the same: don't randomly start coding. There is a sequence — data, screening, alpha, backtest, performance analysis — and skipping a step is where strategies quietly die. At a big fund each step is a separate team; at a startup one person wears all the hats. Either way, the order matters.

Keep one sobering fact in mind: roughly 90 % of active fund managers underperform their benchmark. If your strategy can't beat a cheap index ETF, there is no reason for it to exist. That is the bar.

Step 1 — Data, and the one programming idea that matters

Alpha is built from data, and there are four families of it. Price data (open, high, low, close, volume) is where everyone starts. Fundamental data — revenue, profit, cash flow, and crucially the timing of earnings announcements — adds a second dimension. Sentiment data comes in three levels: the company itself (con-calls, management interviews), third parties (research houses, news agencies), and the public (social media — remember how Reddit drove GameStop). And legal, regulatory and economic data — wars, Fed decisions, even counting ships through the Strait of Hormuz to predict crude — rounds it out. Not all data has predictive power; the skill is choosing what does.

Now the programming lesson. When you fetch data, don't paste yf.download(...) into every notebook. Wrap it in a module — a function like get_stock_data(name, start, end) living in its own file. Textbooks say modular code "reduces lines" — but here the line count is identical. The real reason is code maintenance. Imagine you've used the data call in a hundred notebooks and Yahoo Finance suddenly stops providing data. With inline code you must edit a hundred files. With a module, you change one function and every notebook quietly switches to the new source. That is the genuine payoff of modular programming.

One aside worth remembering: auto_adjust=True does not average prices or smooth anything — it adjusts for splits, bonuses and other corporate actions, so a 1-for-1 split doesn't look like a 50 % crash.

Step 2 — Screening, and the step everyone forgets

Once you have data on many stocks, throw away the ones that don't fit. Drop penny stocks — they have a mind of their own, lock in circuits, and attract price-chasing beginners who think a ₹2 stock becoming ₹10 is easy money. Drop high-volatility names if your mandate forbids them (a pension fund cannot gamble retirees' money). Drop stocks in recent mergers, because the merged entity behaves nothing like the historical data you'd backtest on.

But the screen that ninety percent of people forget is liquidity. Here is the rule of thumb: you can realistically buy only about 1 % of a stock's daily dollar volume in a day without moving the price against yourself. Work the lecture's example. A stock trades 1.18 million shares a day at about ₹12.6, so its daily dollar volume is roughly $14,868,000. One percent of that is about $148,680 — and if you're a $100 million manager wanting to put $10 million into a single name, you simply cannot. It would take far too long and the slippage would be brutal. Verdict: avoid. People who ignore this get trapped in small-cap positions they cannot exit when the market turns.

Step 3 — Alpha, the hard part

Alpha is any edge that earns more than the market. Where do ideas come from? Four wells: your own experience (you've seen RSI or a moving average work), others' experience (a trader who can't code teaming with a developer who can), your network (EPAT seniors, juniors, faculty), and academic research (sites like SSRN are full of documented anomalies). Don't be intimidated by PhDs at big funds — they often over-engineer, reaching for neural networks where a moving-average crossover would do. Process and effort beat pedigree.

Whatever the idea, you must turn it into a testable hypothesis. "If the market rises two days it keeps rising" means nothing to a computer. Translate it: buy when the 2-day EMA is above the 7-day EMA. One alpha is rarely enough, so add a second — an ADX above 20 to confirm the trend has strength — and combine them. The simplest combination is "AND": multiply the two 0/1 signals so you only buy when both agree. With more alphas you'd switch to majority voting, or weighted voting where each alpha's weight reflects its own Sharpe or return. In our run on real data the raw EMA(2/7) crossover was long about 54 % of the time but flipped signal 507 times — it badly over-trades, which is exactly why a trend filter (and, next, diversification) matters.

Steps 4 and 5 — Backtest honestly, then judge from many angles

Backtesting is going back in time to ask "would this have worked?" Two disciplines matter. First, shift your signal by one bar — you trade tomorrow on a signal you see today, never on the same bar, or you've peeked at the future. Second, include transaction cost. In our single-stock run on RELIANCE the raw crossover returned +251 % gross — but simply buying and holding the stock made +673 %, so this fast rule actually trailed doing nothing. Charge the lecture's cost (0.03 % brokerage + 0.02 % tax = 0.05 % per side) and the trade log's net falls from 660 to 526 points — a 20 % drag. Costs routinely eat ten percent or more of gross, and they collapse marginal strategies entirely.

Then judge wisely. A single number — total return — lies. Look at the Sharpe ratio (only 0.64 for the crossover), the number of trades (507 signal changes, punishing after costs), and the volatility. Only the full picture tells you whether the strategy is worth your time and money.

The most powerful lever in momentum: diversification

Here is the punchline of Part 1. Beginners on day one suggest a dozen fixes for a weak strategy — stop-losses, position sizing, new indicators, parameter sweeps. All reasonable. But the simplest, most effective move is to diversify. Take the exact same strategy and run it across a basket of loosely-correlated stocks instead of one. Some names work in one period, others in another; they cover for each other, portfolio volatility falls sharply, and the Sharpe ratio jumps.

In our notebook the average single-stock Sharpe was about 0.51; the equally-weighted 47-stock portfolio's Sharpe was 1.28 — roughly two-and-a-half times higher, on a cumulative return of +272 % — and because volatility fell so much, you could even add a little leverage. Diversification works best across genuinely different assets: Nifty-50 names move together, but commodities like crude, gold and lean hogs barely correlate at all, so the benefit there is enormous.

Part 2 — Betting Against Beta

Now we use the workflow to implement a real paper: Betting Against Beta by Frazzini and Pedersen of AQR. First, two kinds of risk. Idiosyncratic (unsystematic) risk is firm-specific — a strike at one Maruti plant hurts Maruti, not Hyundai. You can diversify it away, so the market won't pay you for it. Systematic risk is market-wide — COVID, war, 2008, a Fed decision — and it hits everything at once. You can't diversify it, only hedge it, so it is compensated.

We measure systematic risk with beta: regress a stock's returns on the market's, and the slope is beta. A defensive name has a beta well below one — the shipped Beta notebook gets Nestle around 0.52, and on our own Nifty matrix TCS lands at exactly 0.52: when the market falls 14 %, it falls only about 7 %. Across the 48 names beta runs from 0.46 (Wipro) to 1.76 (Yes Bank). Beta above one means more volatile than the market; below one, less.

The anomaly

The Capital Asset Pricing Model says expected return equals the risk-free rate plus beta times the market's excess return. Set the risk-free rate to zero and it collapses to expected return ≈ beta × market return — so a higher beta promises a higher return. Everyone therefore piles into high-beta stocks to juice their performance. Why? Because they are leverage-constrained: a retail trader can't walk into a bank and borrow to triple their stock exposure, and mutual funds have mandates. Unable to use leverage, they reach for beta instead. That crowding makes high-beta stocks overvalued and low-beta stocks underpriced — nobody wants to buy boring Nestle in a bull market.

So the contrarian bet is: go long low-beta and short high-beta. You're buying exactly what the leverage-constrained crowd has abandoned.

When we built this on the real Nifty-50 data, the low-beta portfolio (the 12 names with beta below 0.7, held from 2018) returned +237 % — beating the Nifty's +134 % — and did it with a higher Sharpe (1.10 vs 0.76) and a shallower drawdown (−28 % vs −38 %). Sorting the universe by beta, the five lowest-beta names earned +241 % at Sharpe 0.89 while the five highest-beta names earned only +185 % at Sharpe 0.63 — a betting-against-beta spread of about +56 points, with the low-beta side taking far less risk. That is the anomaly in one line: lower beta, higher risk-adjusted return, the opposite of what CAPM predicts. The paper finds the same pattern in international equities, treasuries, credit, commodities and currencies.

Why does low beta win, especially in crises? Partly the arithmetic of recovery. A stock that falls 10 % needs an 11 % gain to get back to even; one that falls 50 % needs a full 100 %. Low-beta names fall less in a crash, so they climb back faster. A colleague's tweak — keeping only the low-beta names that are also high-quality, with return-on-equity above 18 (this leaves just five: HCL Tech, Hindustan Unilever, Infosys, TCS and Tech Mahindra) — lifted the return to about +269 %, the “quality-minus-junk” idea in miniature.

The two biases that quietly ruin this

This strategy is a perfect place to learn the two most common — and most dangerous — backtesting errors. Survivorship bias creeps in if you build the universe from today's index members. Those are the survivors; the companies that went bankrupt or were dropped for underperformance have vanished from your data, so your backtest looks far rosier than reality. The fix is to use the index's historical constituents at each point in time — Nifty 50 is reconstituted every six months, and the changes are in NSE press releases.

Look-ahead bias creeps in if you compute beta on the full data set and then trade. You're using information — the stock's whole-sample behaviour — that you would not have had on the day you placed the trade. The fix is to estimate beta on a training window only (say, data up to 2017) and trade from 2018 onward, recomputing periodically. In live trading you'd wrap this in a simple date-driven loop: at the start of each month, recalculate beta on data available so far and rebalance.

Putting it together

The lesson of QuantiP is that a quant strategy is a process, not a clever indicator. Collect data and wrap it in a module so a vendor change is painless. Screen ruthlessly — and never forget liquidity. Turn ideas into testable hypotheses and combine several alphas. Backtest with costs and without peeking at the future, then judge on Sharpe and drawdown, not just return. Lean on diversification, the quietest and strongest lever. And when you implement a real anomaly like Betting Against Beta, guard against survivorship and look-ahead bias as carefully as you guard the alpha itself — because in this game, the way you avoid fooling yourself is the edge.
