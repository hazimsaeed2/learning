Basic Statistics for Trading — From Excel Formulas to a Price Model

Week 3-2 · SFM-03 · Statistics for Financial Markets — a plain-English study guide

This lecture answers a deceptively simple question: when you type =STDEV.S() or =COVARIANCE() in Excel, what is actually happening? Once you understand the machinery, you can build something useful with it: a diversified portfolio, a model that predicts a price range, and the risk numbers (Value-at-Risk, expected shortfall) that professional desks live by. We work the lecturer's exact data the whole way through — two Indian bank stocks and ten years of NIFTY.

1. Returns first — and why we take the logarithm

A price on its own tells you nothing; what matters is the return from one day to the next. The lecturer uses the natural-log return:

r = ln( today's price ÷ yesterday's price )

Why the log? Because money in markets compounds continuously, not once a year. If you compound a rate r over n sub-periods, the growth factor is (1 + r/n)nt, and as n → ∞ this becomes ert. Invert that and the per-period return is the natural log of the price ratio. There's a second, very practical payoff: log returns add up. The two-day return is just day-1 plus day-2. That additivity makes everything later — annualizing, multi-day forecasts — clean. (Simple percentage returns do not add; more on that trap in §6.)

2. Standard deviation by hand — and the mystery of "n − 1"

Volatility is the heartbeat of risk, and it is the standard deviation of returns. Here is exactly what Excel does behind STDEV.S, step by step:

  1. Compute the mean x̄ of the returns.
  2. For each day, take the deviation xi − x̄ and square it (squaring makes negatives count and punishes big misses).
  3. Sum the squares, divide by n − 1, then take the square root.

On the lecturer's SBIN data (59 daily returns) this gives a sample standard deviation of 0.016094 (1.61% per day) — identical to STDEV.S.

Why divide by n − 1 and not n? (degrees of freedom)

When you centre data on the sample mean, the deviations are forced to sum to zero. So if you know the first n − 1 deviations, the last one is already determined — it carries no new information. You "spent" one degree of freedom estimating the mean, leaving only n − 1 independent pieces. Dividing by n would make the variance systematically too small. Correcting it to n − 1 is Bessel's correction, and it's why finance always uses STDEV.S (sample), never STDEV.P (population) — we never have the whole population of a stock's returns, only a sample.

3. Combining two stocks — covariance and the efficient frontier

Diversification only helps if you know how two stocks move together. That's covariance: the average product of their paired deviations. For SBIN and ICICI it comes to 6.86 × 10⁻⁵ (matching the lecture exactly). The portfolio's variance for a weight w in SBIN and 1 − w in ICICI is:

σ²p = w²σ²SBI + (1−w)²σ²ICI + 2·w(1−w)·Cov

Notice it is not just a weighted average of the two variances — the covariance term is what lets a blend be less risky than either stock alone. The lecturer builds 101 portfolios (weights 0% to 100% in 1% steps), computes each one's risk and return, and plots them. The result is the classic curved frontier:

The lowest-risk mix is about 18% SBIN / 82% ICICI, with a daily volatility of 1.01% — below both SBIN (1.61%) and ICICI (1.05%) on their own. That is diversification doing its job. The teaching point of the chart: you only ever want portfolios on the upper arm, because for any given level of risk there is a portfolio there with a higher return than its mirror image below.

4. The random walk — predicting a price range

The simplest model of a stock: tomorrow's price = today's price × a random return, where daily returns are assumed normally distributed and independent. That single assumption is powerful, because the normal distribution has fixed probabilities:

  • about 68% of returns land within ±1 standard deviation of the mean,
  • about 95% within ±2 standard deviations.

So from just the historical mean and standard deviation of NIFTY's returns, we can state a range for where it will likely be. With NIFTY at 18,065, the model gives a next-day 95% range of roughly 17,684 – 18,471.

Why model returns rather than prices directly? Because a price is chained to yesterday — a ₹102 stock cannot be ₹1,000 tomorrow — so prices are not independent. Returns have no such leash: whether a stock is ₹10 or ₹10,000, a 5% move is equally plausible. That independence is what lets us treat returns as well-behaved normal draws.

5. Annualizing volatility — the √T rule

Here is a trap: returns add across days, but standard deviation does not. What adds is variance (because for independent returns the covariance terms vanish). So for T days:

σ²T = T · σ²1  ⟹  σT = σ1 · √T

A stock with 1% daily volatility is not 252% volatile per year — it's 1% × √252 ≈ 16%. On the real NIFTY data, the daily 1.089% annualizes to 17.3%. The same rule, run backwards, lets you compare a "10% annual" strategy against a "20% over 10 years" strategy: scale both to the same horizon first.

6. Simple vs log returns — the drift correction

If you take the simple average of yearly percentage returns, you will overstate what an investor actually earned. The lecturer's fund example — yearly returns of 15, 23, 26, −11, −5% — makes it vivid:

The rule

Either work in log returns (and the problem disappears), or, if you insist on simple returns, subtract the drift term μ − σ²/2 (this comes from Itô's lemma). Some regulators actually ban funds from advertising the naive simple average because it flatters performance.

7. Monte Carlo — simulate instead of solve

Rather than trusting one formula, we can simulate thousands of possible futures. Each day we draw a random normal return — in Excel that's =NORMINV(RAND(), mean, std) — and roll the price forward with next = previous × e^return. Do that for 20 days, 5,000 times, and you get a whole cloud of outcomes:

The simulation's 95% range for NIFTY in 20 days, 16,544 – 20,012, agrees with the closed-form answer (16,538 – 20,096) — three different methods, one answer. The real power of simulation is the questions a formula can't easily answer:

  • Value-at-Risk (the 5% floor): the 5th-percentile price is about 16,796 — we're 95% confident NIFTY stays above it over 20 days.
  • Expected shortfall (CVaR): if the floor breaks, the average of the worst 5% of outcomes is about 16,456. It answers "how bad is bad?" — two stocks can share the same 5% probability of falling, but one may fall to 98 and the other to 50.

8. Bollinger Bands — a tradable mean-reversion rule

Take a 20-day moving average and draw bands ±2 standard deviations around it. If prices were perfectly normal, ~95% of closes would sit inside, so a simple strategy falls out: buy when price closes below the lower band, sell/exit when it closes above the upper band — betting it reverts to the mean.

Reality check — fat tails

On the real ten-year NIFTY, about 9% of closes fall outside the ±2σ bands, not the ~5% the normal model predicts. Markets have fat tails — extreme moves happen more often than a bell curve says (1929, 2020 fell well beyond 3σ). That's why practitioners sometimes model out to 4 or 5 standard deviations. The normal assumption is a useful approximation, never the literal truth.

The thread tying it all together

Every tool here — portfolio risk, price ranges, VaR, Bollinger Bands — rests on two ideas: returns are roughly normal, and standard deviation measures risk. Master the mean, the variance (with its n−1), and the covariance, and the rest is just applying them.
