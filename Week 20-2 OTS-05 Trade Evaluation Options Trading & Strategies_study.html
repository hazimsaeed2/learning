Trade Evaluation & Hedging: Turning a Volatility Idea into a Disciplined Trade

This session moves from the theory of volatility into the messy practice of running the trade — how to hedge without bleeding to death on costs, which volatility to feed your model, how to close on expiry, how to judge whether a strategy is actually good, and finally a complete SPY case study from plan to post-mortem.

Everything here serves one purpose: a volatility trade should earn its money from volatility, not from where the price happened to go. The average P&L of such a trade is Vega × (implied vol − realized vol) — notice there is no price term. To make reality match that clean equation, you must continuously cancel the price sensitivity (delta) so that the only thing left moving your premium is volatility. That cancelling is hedging, and almost the whole lecture is about doing it intelligently.

The lecture's downloadable exercise is a small Greeks calculator built on the mibian library. Feed it a 30-day 105 option on a \$100 stock at 30% volatility and it returns the call (price \$1.566, delta 0.30, gamma 0.04, theta −0.05) and the put (price \$6.566, delta −0.70, gamma 0.04). Those numbers are the raw material of everything that follows: delta and gamma tell you how much and how often to hedge, while theta is the time-decay you are trying to collect. Once you can compute the Greeks, you can hedge them — and only then can you evaluate whether the hedged trade was any good.

Dynamic delta hedging — the core idea

Take a one-year at-the-money call: stock \$100, strike 100, volatility 30%. Its premium is about \$11.92 and its delta is 0.56. Delta says: for every \$1 the stock moves, the option's value moves \$0.56. To neutralise that, you short 0.56 shares — now your book has zero delta and is, for the moment, immune to small price moves.

But delta is not constant. If the stock rallies to \$110, the call is now worth about \$18.14 and its delta has risen to 0.68. You are no longer hedged — there is a fresh +0.12 of delta — so you short another 0.12 shares. Repeating this re-balancing as the price wanders is exactly what dynamic (delta) hedging means.

A gentler first version of the same instinct: hold a \$100 stock and sell a call above it, collecting premium. If the stock falls you keep the premium as a cushion; if it rises the short call caps your gain. With \$10 of premium collected, your breakeven sits at \$110. It "feels" like free money — until you remember that every time the price crosses the strike you are buying and selling shares, and those transactions are not free.

The problem with hedging continuously

If you re-hedge on every tick, two things happen: your transaction costs approach infinity, and you spend all day fighting the market — buying as it rises, selling as it falls. Simulate a delta-hedged short call with no volatility edge (implied equals realized) and the result is sobering: the average P&L is dragged slightly negative by costs, and about half the trades lose money. Hedging is non-negotiable for a vol trade, but doing it constantly destroys the very edge you were trying to capture. The rest of the lecture is the search for the optimal amount of hedging.

Idea 1 — hedge on a schedule

Instead of every tick, hedge once a day, or every two days, or once a week. Cheaper, but now the underlying is not fully masked between hedges, so some price risk leaks into your P&L.

Idea 2 — hedge on a price move

Hedge every \$10 the stock moves. But a \$10 move means nothing for a \$1,000 stock and everything for a \$1 stock, so generalise it: hedge every two standard deviations of price.

Idea 3 — hedge on delta itself (delta bands)

Most fundamental of all: since the thing we are hedging is delta, set a threshold on delta. Rather than always aiming for delta = 0, accept a band — say, "I'm comfortable as long as my delta is between these two values" — and only hedge when it escapes the band.

Whalley–Wilmott delta bands

This is the utility-based method that makes "idea 3" rigorous. The philosophy comes from behavioural finance and econometrics: you can never perfectly eliminate risk, only mitigate it — just as a motorcyclist could hit 200 mph but chooses a speed where the thrill stops being worth the danger. The method returns two delta levels (a band), and the half-width is approximated by:

Δband = ( 3 · e−r(T−t) · λ · S · Γ² / 2κ )1/3

Read the equation by asking what each term is doing:

  • e^(−r(T−t)) — a discount factor over the time left to expiry; as theta accelerates near expiry, the band responds.
  • λ (lambda) — your proportional transaction cost. Trading Apple at \$130 with a \$1 cost gives λ = 1/130; an index at \$100 with a \$0.05 cost gives λ = 0.0005. Higher costs → wider bands (hedge less).
  • S — the underlying price.
  • Γ² (gamma squared) — the dominant term. Gamma measures how fast delta itself changes; high gamma means delta runs away quickly, so the band must be wider. As you gain experience you will feel your bands mostly through gamma.
  • κ (kappa) — your risk-aversion, the personality term. A risky trader and a cautious trader cannot use the same numbers and call it the same trade.
Lambda and kappa are the only two awkward inputs (everything else you can read off the screen). Lambda you compute from your broker's costs. Kappa you calibrate from your own behaviour: decide the delta you are happy to let drift before you bother hedging — say 0.2 — substitute it into the equation, solve for kappa (the lecture's ballpark is ~0.24), and then reuse that same kappa on every new contract you ever trade.

Narrow bands mean you hedge often: tighter risk control but higher cost — the choice of a risk-averse trader. Wide bands mean you hedge rarely: cheaper, but you carry more residual risk — the choice of someone comfortable with risk. Short positions are usually given narrower bands because they are riskier.

How often to hedge — dispersion and the elbow

There is a second dimension beyond where to hedge: how many times. The accuracy of your realised P&L versus the theoretical Vega·(IV−RV) is governed by a simple relation:

dispersion ≈ 1 / √(number of hedges)

More hedges → tighter, more accurate P&L — but more cost. Plot dispersion against the hedge count and you get an elbow: from 0 to about 50 hedges the accuracy improves sharply; beyond that you are paying real money for almost no benefit. Find the elbow and cap your hedging there.

Why does any single trade still lose even after all this care? Path dependency. A back-test runs the premium down one price path; nudge the price, vol, or rate even slightly and the premium would have travelled a completely different route, turning in-the-money into out-of-the-money. So a single back-test is one sample, not the truth. The honest approach is a Monte-Carlo of many simulated paths, then average the outcome — and accept that on average you capture the vol edge, while individual trades scatter.

Which volatility do you hedge with?

You enter on implied volatility (the premium reflects the market's implied view) and you settle at expiry on realized volatility. So which σ should feed your delta and gamma calculations? There is no single right answer — it depends on what you are trying to see:

hedge using…day-to-day mark-to-marketfinal P&L
implied volsmooth (low MTM variance)uncertain — wide final spread
realized volnoisytight, accurate final number

The lecture's simulation makes it concrete: a one-year option sold at 40 vol with realized 30. Hedge at implied and the day-to-day MTM is calm but the final P&L is all over the place; hedge at realized and the daily marks jump around but the final outcome clusters tightly. If you care about your daily statement, hedge at implied. If you care about where the trade ultimately lands, hedge at realized — which means you must forecast it (with GARCH, or simply VIX).

Drift — the trend Black–Scholes forgets

Black–Scholes prices off the current price only; it has no opinion about a trend. In a strongly trending market that is a real flaw. The practical fix, when you are short gamma in a trending market, is to bias your hedging volatility low — concretely, don't short proactively at the model's price; wait and place a limit order higher. If the model says short at 282.92 but the tape is grinding up, sell at 300 instead. You don't need to re-solve the Black–Scholes differential equation with a drift term; a deliberate bias gets you to the same place.

Closing positions on expiry

Exercise early or hold?

Holding to expiry ties up capital that could be earning the risk-free rate, so weigh that interest against the option's remaining time (extrinsic) value and any dividend. The clean example: an 80-strike call on a \$100 stock is worth 20.10 — \$20 of intrinsic value plus \$0.10 of time value. News arrives of a \$1 dividend, which will drop the stock to \$99 on the ex-date. Exercise early and you bank 19 intrinsic + \$1 dividend = \$20; hold and your option simply tracks the lower stock to 19.10. Because the dividend (\$1) exceeds the extrinsic value (\$0.10), early exercise wins. The rule generalises: exercise early when the dividend you would capture is bigger than the time value you would throw away.

Pinning & pin risk: on expiry the underlying tends to settle near the at-the-money strike more often than chance would suggest. That is dangerous — a position that looks in-the-money at 3:15pm (your last trade) can settle out-of-the-money after the exchange computes the settlement price. Knowing pinning lets you decide whether to close an hour early or let the exchange settle.

Cash vs physical settlement

Cash settlement simply credits the \$ difference. Physical settlement delivers the underlying — a stock into your demat, or, for commodities, the actual goods, with shipping or warehouse-storage charges that have caught out traders who saw a paper profit turn into a ledger loss the next day. Note too that European options can only be nullified (buy back the contract) or held to expiry — you cannot take early delivery — whereas American options can be exercised early.

One last reminder: on expiry day the Greeks look strange — in-the-money deltas snap toward 1 and out-of-the-money options become worthless. That is normal expiry behaviour, not a bug in your code.

Evaluating a trade or strategy

Distinguish an idea (a hypothesis), a trade (the idea backed by some analysis), and a strategy (entries, exits, and what to do when the pattern breaks). A "good trade" is not simply one that made money — it is one that, repeated 100 times, produces a consistent equity curve.

The classic mistake is judging a strategy by cumulative return or CAGR alone. A lucky first few trades can flatter the whole curve. Instead look at its shape: how long did it spend below zero, how deep were the drawdowns, what is the maximum drawdown (how far it can fall from a peak), and is the win/loss asymmetry favourable? You want at least ~100 trades to say anything statistically, so keep a journal; and remember live results lag back-tests because of slippage and because a crowded strategy loses its alpha.

Risk-adjusted ratios put reward over a definition of risk:

ratiorisk in the denominator
Sharpestandard deviation of returns — but it penalises big upside moves as harshly as downside, which is its flaw
Sortinostandard deviation of losses only
Calmarmaximum drawdown
Sterlingthe average of the last few (or largest) drawdowns

The discipline that matters most: match the metric to the edge. Grading a volatility trade with a Sharpe ratio makes no sense — measure instead how much alpha you earn per unit of volatility increase. And test the strategy separately across bull, bear and range-bound regimes, because "a great 10-year win-rate" hides the fact that those ten years were many different markets.

The SPY case study — plan, hedge, attribute

Putting it all together on 3 June 2019, the goal is to sell SPY options to harvest the variance premium (implied usually exceeds realized).

  1. Expiration. The 1-, 3- and 6-month contracts all show an at-the-money implied vol of about 18.8%. Choose the one-month — shorter-dated options carry higher vol, and a one-month horizon validates the idea quickly.
  2. Structure. A short strangle rather than a straddle: with the ATM at 275, sell the 262 put and the 286 call. The out-of-the-money strikes give more room than an at-the-money straddle.
  3. Risk profile & tail hedge. Mapping P&L across price scenarios shows losses everywhere and a roughly half-million-dollar loss in a 30% crash, so buy 100 of the 232 puts (2-delta) to cap the tail. The risk profile visibly improves.
  4. Hedging volatility. Because we want an accurate final number, we hedge on forecast realized vol: VIX gives 14.1%, a GARCH model 13.7%, so we use the average, 13.9% — using the Whalley–Wilmott bands with κ ≈ 0.24.
  5. Expected P&L as a range. The Monte-Carlo expectation is \$16,900 with a standard deviation of \$8,200 — a one-sigma range of roughly \$8,700 to \$25,100. Never quote a single number.

Post-trade. The trade actually made \$24,218 — inside the band, but near the top. Why? Realized vol didn't just fall to the forecast 13.9%, it crashed to 10.6%. Had we known that, the expectation would have been ~\$28,700. And SPY rallied from 275 to 293 after entry; had we built in the upward drift (shorting a touch higher), the expectation would have been ~\$21,700, closer to the real outcome. The lesson is not to celebrate the number: the profit was partly luck, and the value lies in the attribution — understanding why the result landed where it did, so the next trade is better-planned.

The OTS-05 process in one breath

  1. Hedge the price out so the P&L is pure volatility — but not continuously.
  2. Use Whalley–Wilmott delta bands to decide how loosely to hedge (λ, κ, and above all gamma).
  3. Cap the number of hedges at the elbow of the dispersion curve.
  4. Pick the hedging vol by purpose — implied for smooth marks, realized for an accurate final number.
  5. Bias for drift when short gamma in a trend.
  6. Close by weighing interest, time value and dividends; respect pinning and settlement type.
  7. Judge the strategy on the shape of its equity curve and a metric that matches the edge.
  8. Plan as a range, then attribute the outcome.
